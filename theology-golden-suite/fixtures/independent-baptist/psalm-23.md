**Lesson Summary:**
Psalm 23 is one of the most beloved passages in all of Scripture, yet familiarity can blunt its force. ## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Psalm 23 is attributed to David, identified in the superscription as "A Psalm of David." David's background as a literal shepherd before becoming king gives this poem autobiographical credibility. He knew what it meant to lead, protect, and provide for vulnerable animals in rugged terrain. That lived experience shapes every metaphor in this psalm.

The psalm belongs to the genre of individual trust psalms — songs expressing personal confidence in God during real-life circumstances. It is not an abstract theological treatise; it is a declaration of faith forged in experience. The shift from third person ("He makes me lie down," vv. 1–3) to second person ("You are with me," vv. 4–5) is intentional and theologically significant: as David moves into the darkest valley, the language becomes intimate. Doctrine becomes conversation.

**Key Word Insights**

The Hebrew name *YHWH* (LORD, in all capitals in most translations) is the covenant name of God — the self-existent, eternally faithful One. David does not say "a shepherd" or "my helper." He anchors his confidence in the covenant-keeping God of Israel.

The word translated "want" (*chaser* in Hebrew) means to lack, to be in need, to be deficient. David's declaration is not that life will be painless, but that nothing genuinely necessary will be missing. This is a statement of sufficiency, not ease.

"He restores my soul" uses the Hebrew *shub*, meaning to turn back, to return. The image is of a sheep that has wandered — and a shepherd who retrieves it. Spiritually, this speaks to God's ongoing renewal of the believer's inner life.

"The valley of the shadow of death" (*tsalmaveth*) can also be rendered "deep darkness" — a word combining shadow and death. It describes life's most threatening passages, not just physical dying.

**Doctrinal Connections**

The psalm presents God's provision across six dimensions: rest (green pastures), refreshment (still waters), spiritual restoration (soul), moral guidance (paths of righteousness), protective presence (the dark valley), and eternal hospitality (the table and house of the LORD). Each provision corresponds to a genuine human need.

The closing verse — "I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever" — carries eschatological weight. For the dispensationalist, this anticipates the believer's eternal dwelling with God, confirmed in the New Testament through Christ's promise in John 14:2–3.

Jesus' claim in John 10:11 — "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down His life for the sheep" — is the New Testament fulfillment of Psalm 23's promise. Christ died for the sins of all people everywhere, making the Shepherd's provision available to every person who will trust Him.

**Age-Appropriate Depth Note**

Mid-life adults often wrestle with whether God's provision is real in the middle of financial strain, career uncertainty, or family crisis. This psalm does not promise exemption from the dark valley — it promises the Shepherd's presence *through* it. That distinction matters enormously for this age group.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Hook — Relevant Quotation (3–4 minutes)**

Open by reading this quote from Charles Spurgeon: *"The Lord is my shepherd — what a title! The Lord who created all things, who holds the universe in His hand — He is my shepherd. Not a shepherd in general, but mine."*

They do not share this publicly. It becomes their personal anchor for the lesson.

**Transition:**
"David wrote Psalm 23 out of real experience — not a comfortable life, but a life that had seen betrayal, danger, and loss. What he says in this psalm, he earned the right to say. Let's find out what he actually claimed."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Psalm 23. We're going to read it together, and then I want to show you why this psalm is far more demanding — and far more powerful — than most people realize.

> "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want. He makes me lie down in green pastures; He leads me beside quiet waters. He restores my soul; He guides me in the paths of righteousness for His name's sake. Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I fear no evil, for You are with me; Your rod and Your staff, they comfort me. You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies; You have anointed my head with oil; my cup overflows. Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life, and I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever."
> — Psalm 23:1–6 (NASB)

Now, here's what I want you to notice first. David doesn't say "The LORD *was* my shepherd" or "I hope the LORD *will be* my shepherd." He says *is*. Present tense. Active claim. This is not a memory or a wish — it is a declaration of present-tense reality.

And that first declaration — "I shall not want" — is not David saying his life was easy. Read his other psalms. He was hunted by Saul, betrayed by his own son, and carried enormous personal failure. "I shall not want" means: *nothing I genuinely need will be missing.* That's a statement about God's sufficiency, not about the absence of hardship.

Think about what a shepherd actually does. He doesn't just point sheep toward food and water — he *leads* them there. He scouts the terrain. He finds the still waters because sheep won't drink from fast-moving currents — they'll go thirsty before they'll risk it. The shepherd finds what the sheep cannot find for themselves and brings them to it safely. That is the picture David is painting of God.

Now look at verse 3: "He restores my soul." That word in Hebrew — *shub* — means to turn back, to return. The image is of a sheep that has wandered off and gotten lost or stuck. The shepherd goes after it and brings it back. Spiritually, this is God's ongoing work of renewal in the believer's life. Not a one-time event — an ongoing reality. Every time you've drifted spiritually and found your way back, that was the Shepherd at work.

Verse 3 also says He guides in "paths of righteousness for His name's sake." That phrase — *for His name's sake* — is critical. God's guidance of your life is not only for your benefit; it is connected to His own character and reputation. He guides you because He is faithful, because He is who He says He is. Your wandering doesn't change His nature. His faithfulness is the engine behind His pursuit of you.

Now verse 4 is where this psalm gets real: "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death." Notice David doesn't say *if* — he says *even though*. He assumes the dark valley is coming. He's not surprised by it. And he doesn't say God removes him from it — he says God is *with him through it*. That's the promise. Not extraction. Presence.

This is where the psalm shifts from talking *about* God to talking *to* God. "You are with me." The theology becomes personal. In the darkest moment, doctrine becomes conversation.

Here's what this means practically for someone in this room who is carrying financial pressure right now. God is not promising you a raise or a windfall. He is promising you His presence, His guidance, and His provision of what you actually need — not necessarily what you fear losing. Philippians 4:19 says, "My God will supply all your needs according to His riches in glory in Christ Jesus." Not all your wants. Not all your preferences. Your *needs* — according to His riches, not yours.

Verse 5 gives us a striking image: a table prepared in the presence of enemies. This is not a private, hidden blessing. God's provision is displayed openly, even in hostile circumstances. The anointed head and overflowing cup are images of honor and abundance — God's generosity exceeding what is merely sufficient.

Now look at verse 6 carefully. "Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life." The word "follow" is actually a strong Hebrew word — it means to pursue, to chase. Goodness and mercy are not casually trailing behind you. They are actively pursuing you. Every day. That's the Shepherd's commitment to His sheep.

And the psalm ends where all genuine trust ultimately leads: "I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever." This is not just comfort language. It is eschatological confidence — the believer's eternal home is secured. Jesus said in John 14:2, "I go to prepare a place for you." The Shepherd who provides in this life is the same One who has prepared the destination for the next.

Here's your challenge for this week: Take that notecard you wrote on at the beginning of class — that one area where trusting God's provision is hardest. Every morning this week, read Psalm 23:1 over that situation. Not as a magic formula — as a declaration of who your Shepherd actually is. Let the reality of His character speak into your specific need. By Sunday, you'll have something to report.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: Six Provisions Mapping (Individual Written Reflection)**
*Time: 6–8 minutes | Materials: Paper or notecard, pen*

Draw a simple two-column chart on the board with headers: **Provision in Psalm 23** | **My Current Need.**

Students work individually and silently. They identify each of the six provisions David describes (rest, refreshment, restoration, guidance, protective presence, eternal hospitality) and write one honest word or phrase beside each one — naming where they most need that specific provision right now. Finances, a strained relationship, a career crossroads — whatever fits.

This is not shared publicly. It extends the opening notecard exercise into deeper personal application.

*Adaptation: Smaller classes may choose to share one provision voluntarily after completing the chart.*

---

**Activity 2: Shepherd's Vocabulary — Word Study Spotlight**
*Time: 4–5 minutes | Materials: None*

Call out three Hebrew words covered in the lesson — *chaser* (want/lack), *shub* (restore/return), and *tsalmaveth* (shadow of death/deep darkness). Ask students to pair up and answer one question per word:

- *Chaser:* "What does David's declaration actually promise — and what does it NOT promise?"
- *Shub:* "When have you experienced God bringing you back from a place of spiritual drift?"
- *Tsalmaveth:* "What does it mean that God promises presence through the valley rather than removal from it?"

*Adaptation: In a larger class, assign one word per table group and have a spokesperson summarize.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** What does the Hebrew word *chaser* (translated "want") actually mean, and how does that change the way you read David's opening declaration?

**2.** **Understanding:** Why does the psalm shift from talking *about* God in verses 1–3 to talking *to* God beginning in verse 4? What does that shift tell us about what happens when we enter the dark valley?

**3.** **Analysis:** David says goodness and lovingkindness will "follow" him — but the Hebrew word means to *pursue* or *chase*. How does that stronger meaning change the picture of God's commitment to His people?

**4.** **Application:** Philippians 4:19 promises God will supply every *need* according to His riches. Where is the line between trusting God's provision and presuming upon it? How do you tell the difference in a real financial situation?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Jesus said in John 10:11, "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down His life for the sheep." How does knowing that the Shepherd of Psalm 23 is the same One who died for you personally change the way you read this psalm?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can distinguish between God's promise of *sufficiency* and a promise of *ease*. Strong answers will reference David's real hardships alongside his confident declaration. Watch for students connecting the Psalm 23 shepherd to Jesus in John 10 without prompting.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged this lesson will begin applying the psalm's provisions to named, specific life situations rather than speaking in generalities. They will show comfort with the idea that the dark valley is assumed — not feared as a sign of God's absence.

**Closing Reflection:** "If the Shepherd's goodness and mercy are actively chasing you every day, what would it look like to stop outrunning them?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**The Shepherd Who Lacks Nothing: Trusting God's Complete Provision**

**Key Idea:** Because the LORD is your Shepherd, every genuine need — spiritual, emotional, material, and eternal — is fully met in Him. This is not a promise for crisis moments only; it is the daily reality of every person who has trusted Christ.

**Memory Verse:** "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want." — Psalm 23:1 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* "I shall not want" is a statement of *sufficiency*, not ease. David's life included betrayal, danger, and loss — yet he declared nothing genuinely necessary would be missing.
* God doesn't just point you toward provision — He *leads* you there, the way a shepherd scouts terrain and finds still water for sheep who can't navigate it alone.
* The dark valley is assumed, not avoided. God's promise is His *presence through it*, not removal from it.
* Goodness and mercy don't casually trail behind you — the Hebrew word means they actively *pursue* you, every day of your life.
* The psalm ends with eternal confidence: the Shepherd who provides in this life has already prepared your permanent home in the next.

**Gospel Connection:** Jesus declared in John 10:11, "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down His life for the sheep." Psalm 23's Shepherd is not an abstract concept — He is Jesus Christ, who died for the sins of all people everywhere so that the provision, guidance, and eternal home described in this psalm could be yours personally. If you have never trusted Him as your Savior, that invitation stands open today.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Which of the six provisions in Psalm 23 — rest, refreshment, restoration, guidance, protective presence, or eternal home — do you need most right now, and why?
**2.** How does knowing God's mercies are *chasing* you change the way you think about a hard season you are currently in?
**3.** What is the difference between trusting God's provision and presuming upon it in your financial decisions this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** Take the specific need you identified during class — the area where trusting God's provision is hardest. Every morning this week, read Psalm 23:1 aloud over that situation. Not as a formula, but as a declaration of who your Shepherd actually is. Come back next week ready to share what God showed you.

**Prayer:** "Lord, You are my Shepherd and I confess I don't always live like it. Teach me to trust Your sufficiency in the places where I am most tempted to fear. Where I have wandered, restore me. Where the valley is dark, remind me You are there. Let Your goodness and mercy catch me this week. Amen."
