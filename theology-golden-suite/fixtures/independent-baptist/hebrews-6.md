**Lesson Title:** *Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Confidence of Genuine Faith*

**Lesson Summary:** Hebrews 6:1-12 calls believers to move beyond spiritual infancy toward genuine maturity in Christ. The passage contains one of Scripture's most debated warning sections, which this lesson approaches through an eternal security framework while taking the warning's seriousness at face value. Students will leave understanding both the urgency of spiritual growth and the security of genuine saving faith.

**Main Theme:** Spiritual maturity is not optional — God calls every believer to press forward, and genuine faith always produces visible fruit.

Many in this age group have also wrestled with the Hebrews 6 warning passage before, so expect questions. Engage their experience; don't talk past it.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews was written to Jewish believers — likely in the late first century — who were under intense social and religious pressure to abandon Christianity and return to Judaism. The author's repeated warnings are pastoral urgency, not theological uncertainty about their standing. The recipients knew the Old Testament deeply, which is why the author argues from Levitical priesthood, Melchizedek, and covenant theology throughout.

Hebrews 6:1-12 follows a sharp rebuke in 5:11-14 where the author calls the readers spiritually dull — people who should be teachers but still need milk. Chapter 6 opens mid-argument: *because* you need to grow up, *let us press on* to maturity.

**The Six Foundational Doctrines (vv. 1-2)**

The author lists six elementary teachings: repentance from dead works, faith toward God, instruction about baptisms (plural — likely referring to Jewish ceremonial washings versus Christian baptism), laying on of hands, resurrection of the dead, and eternal judgment. These are not bad doctrines — they are foundational. The point is that a foundation is meant to be built upon, not camped on indefinitely.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4-6) — Interpretive Key**

This is one of the most discussed passages in the New Testament. The language is striking: those who were "once enlightened," "tasted of the heavenly gift," "made partakers of the Holy Spirit," "tasted the good word of God." Four major interpretive positions exist: (1) these describe genuine believers who can lose salvation; (2) these describe professing believers who were never genuinely saved; (3) this is a hypothetical warning; (4) this describes genuine believers whose *restoration* would be impossible if they could fall — but they cannot.

The Independent Baptist position, grounded in eternal security, best fits view 2 or 4. The word "tasted" (*geuomai* in Greek) does not always mean full appropriation — it can mean a partial or surface-level experience. Judas Iscariot, for example, experienced all the outward benefits of apostolic ministry without genuine saving faith. The warning functions as a serious pastoral caution: those who experience spiritual light and then deliberately, finally reject Christ demonstrate they were never truly regenerated. The impossibility of renewal is the point — not that genuine believers can fall away, but that those who fully and finally reject the gospel after such exposure have hardened themselves beyond recovery.

**The Agricultural Imagery (vv. 7-8)**

The author shifts to a farming illustration. Land that receives rain and produces useful crops is blessed. Land that receives the same rain but produces only thorns is worthless — fit for burning. This mirrors Matthew 7:16-20. The rain falls on both; the difference is the nature of the soil. Genuine faith produces fruit. Persistent fruitlessness signals something is wrong at the root.

**The Encouragement (vv. 9-12)**

The author immediately softens: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." This is not sarcasm — it is genuine pastoral confidence. God is not unjust; He remembers every act of love and service. The call is to imitate those who through *faith and patience* inherit the promises. Maturity is not achieved in a sprint; it requires endurance.

**Doctrinal Connections**

Eternal security does not produce complacency — it produces confidence to press forward. A believer who understands they are sealed by the Spirit (Ephesians 1:13-14) is freed to grow rather than paralyzed by fear of falling. The warning passage, rightly understood, motivates self-examination without undermining assurance.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Opening Hook — Relevant Quotation (3-4 minutes)**

Write this quote on the board before class: *"Not to go forward is to go backward."* — Thomas à Kempis

Ask students: "Do you agree with this statement? Can you think of a time in your own life — spiritually, professionally, or physically — when standing still actually meant sliding backward?"

Allow 2-3 brief responses. Don't resolve the tension yet — let it sit.

**Warm-Up Paired Sharing (4-5 minutes)**

Ask students to turn to the person next to them and answer this question: "What's one area of your spiritual life where you feel like you've genuinely grown in the last five years — and one area where you feel stuck?"

After 2-3 minutes of sharing, bring the group back together. Say: "What you just described — that tension between growth and stagnation — is exactly what the author of Hebrews is pressing into today. Let's open to Hebrews 6."

**Transition:** "This passage is one of the most misunderstood in the New Testament. Today we're going to read it carefully, understand what it actually says, and let it do what it was meant to do — push us forward."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Hebrews 6, starting at verse 1. Before we read, remember what we saw at the end of chapter 5 — the author just told these believers they had become "dull of hearing." They should be teachers by now, but they still need someone to teach them the basics. That's the runway we're landing on today.

Read Hebrews 6:1-3 aloud:

> "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity, not laying again a foundation of repentance from dead works and of faith toward God, of instruction about washings and laying on of hands, and the resurrection of the dead and eternal judgment. And this we will do, if God permits."

Notice the author lists six foundational doctrines — repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, eternal judgment. These aren't wrong things to know. They are the foundation. But here's the key: a foundation is not the building. You don't move into a concrete slab. The foundation exists so something can be built on top of it. The author is saying — stop camping on the foundation. Build.

Think about what it means to be spiritually stuck at the elementary level. Imagine someone who has been a carpenter for twenty years but still only knows how to drive a nail. Every year, same skill, same level. That's not faithfulness — that's stagnation. God designed us for growth, and growth requires forward movement.

Now here's where the passage gets challenging. Read verses 4 through 6. This is the section people wrestle with most:

> "For in the case of those who have once been enlightened and have tasted of the heavenly gift and have been made partakers of the Holy Spirit, and have tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come, and then have fallen away, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance, since they again crucify to themselves the Son of God and put Him to open shame."

People read this and ask: "Can a Christian lose their salvation?" That's a fair question — but it's not the question the author is answering. Look at the language carefully. These people were *enlightened*, they *tasted*, they experienced something real. But "tasting" doesn't always mean fully receiving. When you taste something at a restaurant, you haven't eaten the meal. You've encountered it.

The author is describing people who experienced everything surrounding genuine faith — the light of the gospel, the community of believers, the moving of the Spirit — and then deliberately, finally walked away. The warning is this: if someone could reach that level of spiritual exposure and still harden their heart completely, there is no further revelation, no deeper argument, no stronger appeal that would reach them. They have exhausted the means God uses to draw people.

This is not a warning that a genuine believer can lose their salvation. Eternal security stands — Scripture is clear that those who truly trust Christ are sealed by the Holy Spirit and held in the Father's hand. What this passage warns against is the danger of prolonged exposure to the gospel without genuine response. It's a call to self-examination: *Am I actually in the faith, or am I just familiar with it?*

Now look at the farming picture in verses 7 and 8. The same rain falls on two fields. One produces useful crops. One produces thorns and thistles. The difference isn't the rain — the difference is what's underneath. Genuine faith, like good soil, responds to what God pours into it and produces something visible. Persistent fruitlessness over a lifetime is worth examining seriously.

Here's the pastoral encouragement — and don't miss this shift. Verse 9: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, things that accompany salvation." The author doesn't leave them in fear. He says: I believe you are genuinely saved. And here's what that means — God is not unjust. He sees your work. He sees your love. He remembers your service. Verse 10 is one of the most quietly comforting verses in this letter.

But then verse 11 delivers the challenge: "We desire that each one of you show the same diligence so as to realize the full assurance of hope until the end." Full assurance doesn't come from sitting still. It comes from pressing forward. The believer who is actively growing, serving, and bearing fruit has a settled confidence. The believer who is coasting, drifting, or spiritually passive will struggle with assurance — not because they've lost their salvation, but because fruitlessness creates doubt.

Here's the bottom line: God's faithfulness is the ground you stand on. His promises don't shift. But genuine faith doesn't stay still — it moves, it grows, it produces. The question this passage asks each of us is not "Am I still saved?" The question is: "Am I pressing on?"

Take that question home with you. Write it down somewhere you'll see it this week. Where am I pressing forward — and where have I been coasting? That honest answer, brought before God in prayer, is exactly where maturity begins.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: Foundation vs. Building (Paired Sharing)**
*Time: 5-6 minutes | Materials: None*

Pair students and give each pair this prompt: "Name one foundational doctrine from Hebrews 6:1-2 that you learned early in your faith — then describe one way your understanding of that doctrine has deepened or grown since then."

After 3 minutes of paired sharing, invite two or three pairs to share briefly with the group. The goal is to make the "foundation vs. building" concept personal and concrete. Adaptation for large classes: keep sharing to one sentence per pair.

**Activity 2: Two Fields — Self-Inventory**
*Time: 5-6 minutes | Materials: Index card or scrap paper*

Give each student a card. Ask them to draw a simple vertical line down the middle. Label the left side "Fruit I see in my life" and the right side "Areas that feel like thorns or stagnation." Students write privately — this is not collected or shared aloud. The purpose is to make the agricultural imagery from verses 7-8 personally applied rather than abstract.

Close the activity by reading verse 9 aloud: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." Remind students this honest inventory is not condemnation — it is the beginning of forward movement.

*Adaptation for smaller classes: Invite voluntary sharing from anyone who wants to name one item from either column.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** What six foundational doctrines does the author of Hebrews list in verses 1-2, and what is his main point in telling believers to move *beyond* them?

**2.** **Understanding:** In verses 4-6, the author describes people who were "enlightened," "tasted of the heavenly gift," and "made partakers of the Holy Spirit." What is the difference between *tasting* something and *fully receiving* it — and why does that distinction matter for interpreting this passage?

**3.** **Analysis:** How does the farming illustration in verses 7-8 connect to Jesus's teaching in Matthew 7:16-20? What does persistent fruitlessness reveal, according to both passages?

**4.** **Application:** Verse 11 calls believers to "show the same diligence so as to realize the full assurance of hope until the end." How does active spiritual growth strengthen assurance — and what happens to assurance when a believer drifts or coasts?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The teacher's closing question was: *"Where am I pressing forward — and where have I been coasting?"* What is one honest answer you're willing to carry into this week?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can distinguish between the warning passage describing hardened, never-genuinely-saved individuals versus genuine believers losing salvation. Students who grasp this will frame the warning as a call to self-examination rather than a threat to assurance.

**Spiritual Indicators:** A student demonstrating genuine understanding will express both sober self-examination *and* settled confidence in God's faithfulness — not fear. Watch for students who connect fruit-bearing to the Spirit's work rather than self-effort alone.

**Closing Reflection:** "If God is not unjust and truly remembers every act of love and service you've offered Him, what does that tell you about how He sees your journey right now?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Confidence of Genuine Faith**

**Key Idea:** God calls every believer to move beyond spiritual infancy and press toward genuine maturity — and the security of your salvation is the very ground that frees you to grow without fear.

**Memory Verse:** "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, things that accompany salvation, though we are speaking in this way." — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The six foundational doctrines in Hebrews 6:1-2 are not the destination — they are the starting line. A foundation is built *upon*, not camped on.
* The warning in verses 4-6 describes people who experienced the light of the gospel and the community of faith without ever genuinely receiving Christ — and then hardened completely. It is a call to honest self-examination, not a threat to the saved believer's security.
* The same rain falls on both fields in verses 7-8. Genuine faith, like good soil, responds to what God pours in and produces visible fruit over time.
* God is not unjust. He sees your work, remembers your love, and honors your faithfulness — even in seasons that feel invisible (v. 10).
* Full assurance grows through diligent forward movement, not passive waiting.

**Gospel Connection:** The author's confidence in verse 9 — "we are convinced of better things concerning you, *things that accompany salvation*" — points to a salvation that produces evidence. Christ's finished work on the cross is the only basis for standing before God, and the Spirit He sends into every genuine believer is the one who produces the fruit, sustains the growth, and seals the promise. You press on not to *earn* maturity but because you are already held by the One who never lets go.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your spiritual life are you building on the foundation — and where have you been camping on it?
**2.** What fruit can you point to in your life that reflects the Spirit's work over the past year?
**3.** What would it look like, practically, to "show diligence" in one specific area of your faith this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, spend ten minutes journaling your honest answer to this question: *"Where am I pressing forward, and where have I been coasting?"* Bring that answer to God in prayer — not with shame, but with the confidence that He is faithful to complete what He began in you (Philippians 1:6).

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that my standing before You rests on Christ alone, not on how well I've grown. Forgive me for the seasons I've coasted. By Your Spirit, move me forward. Show me where to build. Help me bear fruit that reflects a genuine, living faith — and let that fruit bring glory to You. Amen."
