## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Free Will Baptist
**Lesson Title:** *Press On or Fall Away: The Urgent Call of Hebrews 6*

Hebrews 6:1–12 delivers one of Scripture's most sobering warnings alongside one of its most encouraging affirmations — and understanding both is essential to authentic Christian living. This lesson examines the writer's urgent call to spiritual maturity, the real danger of apostasy for those who have genuinely experienced God's grace, and the confidence that faithful perseverance brings. Students will wrestle honestly with what it means to move forward in Christ rather than drift backward toward spiritual ruin.

**Main Theme:** Genuine faith presses forward toward maturity; deliberate, persistent rejection of Christ leads to a point of no return.

**Key Takeaway:** Continued, active faith is not optional — it is the path of life, and students must examine whether they are pressing on or standing still.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often carry spiritual complacency disguised as stability. Many have heard the gospel for decades without deepening. This passage confronts comfortable familiarity with Christ that never matures into Christlikeness — a real danger for this age group.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews was written to Jewish Christians facing intense social pressure to abandon Christ and return to Judaism. The author (unnamed in the text; tradition has suggested Paul, Apollos, Barnabas, and others, though Scripture does not identify the author) writes with pastoral urgency to people who had genuinely experienced Christian life but were in danger of deliberate, sustained retreat from it. Hebrews 6:1–12 sits immediately after the rebuke of 5:11–14, where the writer scolds readers for remaining spiritual infants when they should be teachers. Chapter 6 opens as a direct continuation: *therefore*, press on.

**The Call to Maturity (vv. 1–3)**

"Elementary teaching about Christ" refers to foundational doctrines — repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, and judgment. The writer is not dismissing these; he is insisting they be the *foundation*, not the ceiling. The Greek word *teleiotes* (maturity/perfection) implies completion and wholeness. Spiritual maturity in Hebrews is not sinless perfection but a robust, growing, functioning faith that moves beyond doctrinal basics into deep discipleship.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4–6)**

This is among the most debated passages in the New Testament. The writer describes people who have been:
- *Enlightened* — received the light of the gospel
- *Tasted of the heavenly gift* — genuinely experienced God's grace
- *Made partakers of the Holy Spirit* — real participation in the Spirit's work
- *Tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come*

These are not descriptions of superficial exposure. The Greek *metochous* (partakers) in verse 4 is used elsewhere in Hebrews for genuine believers (3:1, 3:14). From a Free Will Baptist perspective, this passage confirms that genuine believers can, through persistent and willful rejection of Christ, reach a point where restoration becomes impossible — not because God refuses to forgive, but because the heart has hardened beyond response. This is apostasy: not a stumble or a season of doubt, but a conscious, sustained crucifying of Christ afresh (v. 6). The impossibility is not God's limitation but the apostate's own spiritual condition.

**The Agricultural Illustration (vv. 7–8)**

The two fields receiving the same rain represent people receiving the same grace. Productive ground bears fruit and receives blessing. Ground producing thorns and thistles faces burning — a clear echo of judgment. The rain is impartial; the outcome depends on the ground's response. This directly supports the Free Will Baptist conviction that prevenient grace enables all people to respond, but does not compel them.

**The Encouragement (vv. 9–12)**

The writer's tone pivots sharply: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." This is not contradiction — it is pastoral balance. The warning is real; so is the confidence. The writer points to their *works* and *love* as evidence of genuine faith. Verse 12 calls for continued faith and patience, not anxious fear. The goal is not terror but perseverance — "imitate those who through faith and patience inherit the promises."

**Key Doctrinal Connection**

This passage is a cornerstone text for conditional security. Salvation is real and present, but continued faith is necessary. Apostasy is possible but never accidental — it requires sustained, willful rejection. The appropriate response is not fear but faithful, forward movement.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — Surprising Fact Hook (3–4 minutes)**

Open with this: "Here's a surprising reality — researchers who study adult learning have found that most people stop acquiring genuinely new skills in their primary field by their mid-thirties, defaulting instead to refining what they already know. The temptation to coast on existing knowledge is nearly universal." Then ask: "What do you think happens spiritually when we do the same thing — when we stop growing and simply coast on what we already know about Christ?"

Allow two or three brief responses.

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Discussion Prompt (2–3 minutes)**

Ask students: "Think about your spiritual life five years ago versus today. In what specific ways are you more mature — or are you essentially in the same place? Be honest with yourself."

Give students thirty seconds of quiet reflection before inviting two or three responses.

**Transition:** "The writer of Hebrews had something urgent to say to people who were in danger of standing still — or worse, moving backward. Let's open to Hebrews 6:1 and hear what the Spirit is saying."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Let's read our passage together. I'll read Hebrews 6:1–12 from the New American Standard Bible, and I want you to listen for two things: a warning and an encouragement — because this passage contains both, and you need both.

> "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity, not laying again a foundation of repentance from dead works and of faith toward God, of instruction about washings and laying on of hands, and the resurrection of the dead and eternal judgment. And this we will do, if God permits. For in the case of those who have once been enlightened and have tasted of the heavenly gift and have been made partakers of the Holy Spirit, and have tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come, and then have fallen away, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance, since they again crucify to themselves the Son of God and put Him to open shame."
> — Hebrews 6:1–6 (NASB)

Now, before anyone panics, let me tell you what this passage is *not* saying. It is not saying that if you've ever doubted, ever sinned badly, ever walked through a dry season — you're done. That is not the text. The writer is describing something far more serious and far more deliberate.

Notice the description in verses 4 and 5. These are people who have been *enlightened* — they received the light of the gospel. They *tasted the heavenly gift* — they genuinely experienced God's grace, not just heard about it. They were *made partakers of the Holy Spirit* — that word "partakers" in Greek is *metochous*, and it's the same word used in Hebrews 3:1 to describe genuine believers. This is not someone who sat in a church pew and was bored. This is someone who genuinely knew Christ.

And yet, verse 6 says they "have fallen away" — and the result is that it is *impossible* to renew them to repentance. Why? Because they are, as the text says, "crucifying to themselves the Son of God and putting Him to open shame." They have not stumbled — they have made a deliberate, sustained decision to reject Christ entirely. The impossibility here is not about God's willingness to forgive. God is always willing to forgive the repentant heart. The impossibility is that this person's heart has become so hardened through persistent, willful rejection that genuine repentance is no longer accessible to them.

Think about what that means. Apostasy — real apostasy — is not an accident. It doesn't happen in one bad week. It is the result of a long pattern of saying no to the Spirit's conviction, no to the community of faith, no to the Word of God — until finally, the capacity to say yes is gone. This is why the writer's urgency matters. He is not trying to terrify you into paralysis. He is trying to wake you up before you drift that far.

Now look at verses 7 and 8 — the agricultural illustration. Two fields receive the same rain. One produces fruit and receives blessing. The other produces thorns and thistles and faces burning. The rain is identical. What differs is how the ground responds. That rain is a picture of God's grace — prevenient grace that reaches every person, that enables a genuine response without forcing one. The outcome is not determined by the rain. It is determined by the ground.

Where is your ground right now? Are you producing fruit — growth, love, service, deepening faith? Or are you producing thorns — spiritual stagnation, excuses, comfortable distance from God?

Now, here is where the passage turns — and this turn matters enormously. Verse 9: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation." The writer does not leave them in the warning. He pivots to confidence. He sees their works. He sees their love. He says, "I don't think that's where you are — but I'm telling you this so you don't drift there."

Verses 11 and 12 give us the prescription: "And we desire that each one of you show the same diligence so as to realize the full assurance of hope until the end, so that you will not be sluggish, but imitators of those who through faith and patience inherit the promises."

Three words deserve your attention: *diligence*, *faith*, and *patience*. Diligence means this is not passive. You don't drift into maturity. You press toward it. Faith means continued trust — not a one-time decision made years ago that you're living off of today, but an active, present, ongoing trust in Christ. And patience — the Greek word here is *makrothumia*, which means long-suffering endurance. Not white-knuckling it, but steadily, faithfully staying the course because you know who you're following.

Here's the challenge for mid-life adults specifically, and I say this with love: this is the season of life where spiritual coasting feels most natural. You've been a Christian for twenty or thirty years. You know the Bible. You know the songs. You know the answers. And precisely because of that, the temptation to stop pressing forward is real. The writer of Hebrews would say: that is exactly the moment to press harder.

The goal is not to be afraid of losing your salvation every time you sin. The goal is to be the kind of person who is actively, diligently, faithfully moving toward Christ — in your workplace, in your home, in your private prayer life — so that the warning of verses 4–6 is simply not your story.

Press on. That is the call. Not backward to the basics you already know. Not sideways into comfortable spiritual stagnation. Forward — toward the full, mature, fruit-bearing life that God's grace makes possible and that your continued faith makes real.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — Scripture Memory Exercise**
*Materials: Index cards (one per student) | Time: 5–6 minutes*

Write Hebrews 6:1a on the board: *"Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity."* Give each student an index card. Have them write the verse on one side and on the other side complete this sentence: *"One area where I need to press forward rather than coast is ___________."*

Pair students and have each partner read the verse aloud twice from the card, then set it face-down and attempt it from memory. Cards go home as a week-long memory prompt.

*Adaptation: For larger classes, do this in groups of three. For smaller classes, recite together as a group.*

**Activity 2 — Two Fields Self-Assessment**
*Materials: None | Time: 4–5 minutes*

Draw two columns on the board labeled **"Fruit"** and **"Thorns."** Ask students to silently identify — without sharing — one area of their spiritual life currently producing fruit and one area that has gone stagnant. Then open briefly: "Without naming the thorn, what would it take to turn that ground around?"

Take three or four responses. Keep the tone honest but not shaming.

*Adaptation: In a small class of fewer than eight, invite voluntary sharing of both columns.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** According to Hebrews 6:1–3, what are the "elementary teachings" the writer lists? Why does he call believers to move *beyond* them rather than *away* from them?

**2.** **Understanding:** The passage describes people who were "enlightened," "tasted the heavenly gift," and were "made partakers of the Holy Spirit." Why is it significant that these descriptions point to genuine believers rather than casual church attenders?

**3.** **Analysis:** The agricultural illustration in verses 7–8 shows two fields receiving identical rain. How does this image support the Free Will Baptist conviction that prevenient grace enables response without compelling it? What does the difference in outcomes depend on?

**4.** **Application:** The teacher identified three key words from verses 11–12: *diligence*, *faith*, and *patience*. In a workplace setting, what would each of these look like in practice — not just on Sunday, but Monday through Friday?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The writer pivots in verse 9 from warning to confidence. What evidence of genuine faith — works, love, service — could someone observe in your life right now? Where do you most need to press forward?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who understand that the "falling away" in verses 4–6 describes deliberate, sustained rejection — not stumbling or doubt — and that the impossibility reflects the apostate's hardened condition, not God's refusal to forgive.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students grasping the lesson will speak about maturity as active and ongoing rather than a past event, connect their daily work life to spiritual growth, and show honest self-awareness about areas of stagnation without spiraling into fear.

**Closing Reflection:** "If someone who knew you well were asked whether your faith looks more like productive ground or ground going to thorns, what do you think they would honestly say — and what would you want them to say?"

## Section 8: Group Handout (Standalone)

**Press On or Fall Away: The Urgent Call of Hebrews 6**

**Key Idea:** Genuine faith does not stand still. Hebrews 6 calls every believer to press actively toward maturity — because spiritual coasting is far more dangerous than it feels.

**Memory Verse:** "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity." — Hebrews 6:1 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The "elementary teachings" of the faith are the *foundation*, not the finish line — maturity means building upward, not camping at the basics.
* The warning of verses 4–6 describes real apostasy: a deliberate, sustained rejection of Christ that hardens the heart beyond response — not a stumble, not a dry season, not a moment of doubt.
* Two fields receive the same rain; one bears fruit, one bears thorns. God's grace reaches everyone — what differs is how we respond.
* The writer's confidence in verses 9–12 is the goal: a life of diligence, active faith, and patient endurance that makes the warning simply not your story.
* Mid-life is precisely when spiritual coasting feels safest — and precisely when pressing forward matters most.

**Gospel Connection:** Christ died for every person — and His grace is continuously available to those who keep turning toward Him. The call to press on is not a threat; it is an invitation to experience the full, fruit-bearing life that His grace makes possible. Continued faith is not a burden placed on top of salvation — it is what salvation looks like when it is alive and growing.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your life right now are you pressing forward in faith — and where are you coasting?
**2.** What would *diligence* toward spiritual maturity look like in your workplace this week?
**3.** What is one "elementary" area of your faith you have mastered in knowledge but not yet lived out consistently?

**Weekly Challenge:** Choose one specific habit to develop this week that moves you from knowing to doing. It might be a daily Scripture reading, a conversation you have been avoiding, an act of service, or a pattern of prayer. Write it on an index card, carry it with you, and at the end of the week ask yourself: did I press on?

**Prayer:** "Father, forgive me for the seasons when I have coasted. I do not want familiar knowledge of You to substitute for genuine growth. By Your grace, I choose to press forward — in my home, in my work, and in my walk with You. Produce fruit in me that honors Christ. In His name, amen."
