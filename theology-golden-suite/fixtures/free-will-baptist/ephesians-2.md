## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Free Will Baptist
**Lesson Title:** *Dead No More: Grace, Faith, and the Life God Gives*

Ephesians 2:1-10 stands as one of the New Testament's most concentrated declarations of what salvation actually is — where it comes from, what it costs us, and what it produces. This lesson traces the movement from spiritual death to resurrection life, grounding every step in God's initiative while affirming the genuine human response of faith that Free Will Baptist theology holds dear.

**Main Theme:** Salvation is entirely God's gracious work, received through genuine faith — and it transforms not only our standing before God but our daily walk.

**Key Takeaway:** Because God made us alive when we were spiritually dead, we are called to live out the good works He prepared for us — not to earn grace, but as the natural overflow of it.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36-50) often carry complex histories with faith — seasons of faithfulness, seasons of drift, financial pressures, and questions about whether their lives are truly counting for something. This passage speaks directly to that tension, affirming both the certainty of God's grace and the ongoing call to purposeful, Spirit-empowered living.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Paul wrote Ephesians from prison (ca. AD 60-62), likely Rome, to a predominantly Gentile congregation in Ephesus — a city dominated by the cult of Artemis and saturated with occult practice. His letter is structured in two halves: chapters 1-3 (doctrinal: who we are in Christ) and chapters 4-6 (practical: how we live because of it). Ephesians 2:1-10 sits at the theological heart of the first half, forming a single extended sentence in the Greek original — verses 1-7 build toward the main verb "made alive together" in verse 5, making resurrection the grammatical and theological climax.

**The Condition: Spiritual Death (vv. 1-3)**

The Greek word translated "dead" (*nekros*) is unambiguous — not weakened, not sleeping, not spiritually sick. Paul describes a state of complete spiritual unresponsiveness. Humans in this condition "walked according to the course of this world, according to the prince of the power of the air" — a reference to satanic influence over the fallen age. The phrase "sons of disobedience" is a Hebraic idiom meaning those whose defining characteristic is rebellion against God. Critically, Paul includes himself: "we too all formerly lived in the lusts of our flesh." This is not an accusation hurled at outsiders — it is a confession shared by every believer.

Free Will Baptist theology affirms that this spiritual death is real and serious — humanity cannot save itself. However, prevenient grace — God's grace that goes before salvation — enables every person to respond to the gospel. Spiritual death does not mean humans retain no God-given capacity to respond to grace; it means that capacity is entirely dependent on God's prior initiative. This is the crucial distinction from both Pelagianism (humans save themselves) and hard Calvinism (humans cannot respond at all).

**The Intervention: God's Rich Mercy (vv. 4-6)**

The pivot in verse 4 — "But God" — is among the most powerful transitions in all of Scripture. The motivation is entirely God's: "rich in mercy," driven by "His great love." The verbs in verses 5-6 are compound Greek forms: *suzōopoieō* (made alive together), *sunegeirō* (raised together), *sunkathizō* (seated together). The *sun-* prefix means "with" — believers are co-resurrected and co-seated with Christ. This is positional language: our standing before God is determined by our union with Christ, not by our moral performance.

**The Means: Grace Through Faith (vv. 8-9)**

"For by grace you have been saved through faith" — the Greek perfect tense (*sesōsmenoi este*) indicates a past action with ongoing present results. You have been saved, and you remain in that saved condition. Grace is the sphere; faith is the instrument. Paul adds "and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God" — the antecedent of "that" (*touto* in Greek) is grammatically neuter, while "faith" (*pistis*) is feminine, suggesting Paul refers to the entire salvation event as God's gift, not faith itself as mechanically implanted. This preserves the genuineness of human faith while insisting salvation's source is entirely divine.

**The Purpose: Good Works (v. 10)**

"We are His workmanship" — *poiēma* in Greek, from which we derive "poem." Believers are God's masterpiece, created in Christ Jesus for good works "which God prepared beforehand." This does not eliminate human freedom; it establishes divine intentionality. God has purposes ready for every believer to walk into. Works do not produce salvation — they are salvation's fruit and evidence. For mid-life adults navigating financial decisions, career questions, and family responsibilities, this verse reframes stewardship as vocation: every area of life is a theater for God-prepared good works.

**Doctrinal Connection: Conditional Security**

This passage affirms the security of those who remain in Christ — seated with Him in heavenly places. Free Will Baptist theology holds that this security is conditional on continued faith. The same grace that saves is the grace that sustains, and continued faith is necessary to remain in that saving relationship.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Hook — The Problem of Self-Rescue (5 minutes)**

Open with this tension: "Imagine you're underwater, thirty feet down, and you've run out of air. Someone on the surface throws you a rope. The question isn't whether you deserve the rope — it's whether you'll grab it." Give students thirty seconds to sit with that image silently, then ask: "What does it feel like to need rescue that badly? Have you ever been in a moment — spiritually, financially, relationally — where you knew you couldn't fix it yourself?"

Allow two or three brief responses. Don't resolve the tension yet — let it breathe.

**Warm-Up Discussion (3 minutes)**

Ask: "When you hear the word 'dead' used spiritually, what comes to mind? Does it feel like an overstatement, or does it ring true from your own experience before Christ — or maybe from a season when your faith went cold?"

**Transition:** "Paul doesn't soften this language. He means it. Let's look at exactly what he says — and what God does about it."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Ephesians 2, beginning at verse 1. I want to read the whole passage before we dig in, because it works as a single movement — a before-and-after story that covers the whole of what salvation is.

Read Ephesians 2:1-10 aloud from the NASB.

Now, let's talk about what just happened in those ten verses.

**We Were Dead — Not Just Struggling**

Paul opens with a word that should stop us cold: dead. Not spiritually tired. Not morally confused. Dead. The Greek word *nekros* means exactly what it sounds like — no pulse, no response, no capacity for self-rescue. And Paul is not talking about people who never heard of God. He's talking about the condition of every human being apart from God's intervention, including himself. He says in verse 3, "we too all formerly lived in the lusts of our flesh." This is a confession, not a condemnation of someone else.

Here's why this matters for us practically: if the problem is spiritual death, the solution cannot be self-improvement. You cannot jog your way out of a coffin. You cannot budget your way into a right standing before God. You cannot be disciplined enough, generous enough, or religious enough to fix a dead spirit. The diagnosis has to be honest before the cure makes sense.

Think about what it means to be "walking according to the course of this world" — that phrase in verse 2. Paul is describing a kind of drift. Nobody woke up one morning and decided to be spiritually dead. It happened gradually, through patterns, through the pull of culture, through what he calls "the prince of the power of the air." That's a reference to satanic influence over the fallen age we live in. We absorb it through what we consume, what we chase, what we measure our worth by. For many of us in this room, the drift happened quietly — a season of busyness, financial pressure, relational pain — and somewhere in the middle of it, our spiritual life went cold.

Paul says that's not just a bad season. That's the default condition of humanity without God.

**But God — The Two Most Important Words in This Passage**

Verse 4 begins with two words that change everything: "But God." Everything before verse 4 is the problem. Everything after is the answer. And notice what drives God's action: "rich in mercy" and "His great love." God doesn't rescue us because we earned it, or because we finally got serious, or because we showed enough potential. He rescues us because of who He is.

This is where Free Will Baptist theology is both clear and beautiful. God's grace goes before us — what theologians call prevenient grace. Before you ever felt a twinge of spiritual hunger, before you ever walked into a church, before you ever opened a Bible, God was already at work in your life, making it possible for you to respond. That grace doesn't force you — it enables you. It's like light coming into a dark room. The light doesn't drag you to the window. But it makes it possible for you to see.

The verbs in verses 5 and 6 are stunning. Paul says God "made us alive together with Christ," "raised us up with Him," and "seated us with Him in the heavenly places." Each of those verbs carries a Greek prefix meaning "together with." Our resurrection is bound up with Christ's resurrection. Our spiritual standing is determined by our union with Him — not by our track record. When God looks at a believer, He sees someone clothed in the righteousness of His Son.

**Grace Through Faith — Getting the Order Right**

Verse 8 is one of the most quoted verses in all of Scripture: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast."

Notice the grammar carefully. Grace is the sphere — the environment in which salvation happens. Faith is the instrument — the means by which we receive it. And Paul adds that the whole salvation event is "the gift of God." You didn't generate your own faith. You didn't manufacture your own desire for God. The entire package — the grace that reached you, the prevenient grace that enabled you to respond, the salvation you received — is God's gift.

But — and this is crucial — receiving a gift requires a real act of the will. When someone hands you something, you have to open your hand. Faith is that open hand. It is genuine, it is yours, and it matters. God does not believe for you. He makes it possible for you to believe, and then He calls you to do so.

**You Are God's Poem**

Verse 10 is where this passage lands, and it's where I want us to sit for a moment. "We are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

The Greek word for "workmanship" is *poiēma* — it's where we get the English word "poem." You are God's poem. A poem is not accidental. A poem is crafted with intention, with care, with purpose in every line. God didn't save you to park you. He saved you for something — specifically, for good works He already has in mind for you.

Now, here's the stewardship application that I think hits home for many of us in this season of life. We spend enormous energy thinking about our finances, our careers, our retirement plans, our family obligations. All of that is real. But what if every one of those arenas is actually a theater for the good works God prepared for you before you were born? Your financial decisions are not separate from your spiritual life — they are one of the primary places where you either walk in the good works God prepared, or you don't.

You are not saved by those works. But you are saved for them. The difference is everything.

Here's the challenge I want to leave you with today: God has already prepared something for you to walk into. The question is not whether the works exist — they do. The question is whether you're paying attention. Whether you're walking in step with the Spirit closely enough to recognize the good works when they appear in front of you — in your budget, in your relationships, in your workplace, in your neighborhood.

You were dead. God said "But God." He made you alive. Now walk like it.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "But God" Timeline (10 minutes)**
*Materials: Paper, pens*

Each student draws a simple horizontal line across their paper. On the left half, they write 2-3 words or phrases describing their spiritual life *before* a significant encounter with God — drift, busyness, emptiness, whatever is honest. On the right half, they write what changed. In the center of the line, they write the words "BUT GOD" in large letters.

Students are not required to share, but invite 2-3 volunteers to describe what sits on either side of their "But God" moment. This grounds the theological movement of Ephesians 2:1-10 in personal testimony.

*Adaptation for small classes:* Share in pairs first, then open to the full group.

---

**Activity 2: "God's Poem" — Stewardship Stanza (8 minutes)**
*Materials: Paper, pens*

Remind students that *poiēma* means poem — they are God's crafted work. Ask each person to write 3-4 lines (not necessarily rhyming) finishing this prompt:

*"The good works God prepared for me look like..."*

Encourage financial, relational, and vocational specificity — a budget decision, a conversation at work, a family commitment. Invite willing participants to read their stanza aloud.

*Adaptation for larger classes:* Complete individually, then share at tables rather than with the full group.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses the word "dead" rather than "weakened" or "struggling" to describe humanity's spiritual condition. What does that specific word choice tell us about humanity's need for God's intervention?

**2.** **Understanding:** The phrase "But God" in verse 4 marks the turning point of the passage. What does Paul say motivated God to act — and what does that tell us about the nature of grace?

**3.** **Analysis:** Paul says salvation is "not of yourselves" and "not as a result of works," yet verse 10 says we were "created for good works." How do you explain that without contradiction? Where does the line fall between works that earn and works that flow?

**4.** **Application:** The lesson described every financial decision, career choice, and family responsibility as a potential "theater for good works God prepared." Which specific area of your life right now feels most like a place God might be calling you to walk in those prepared works?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Has there been a season in your life that felt like the spiritual drift Paul describes in verse 2 — walking according to the pull of the culture rather than the Spirit? What did returning look like, or what would it look like?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who understand that prevenient grace *enables* faith without *compelling* it — that human response is genuine, not mechanical. Students should be able to distinguish grace as the *sphere* of salvation from faith as the *instrument* without collapsing one into the other.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students grasping this lesson will begin connecting ordinary life decisions — especially financial ones — to Spirit-empowered purpose rather than treating faith as compartmentalized from daily stewardship. Watch for language that reflects ownership of their identity as God's *poiēma*.

**Closing Reflection:** "If God has already prepared good works for you to walk into, what might it cost you — in time, money, or comfort — to actually walk in them this week?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Dead No More: Grace, Faith, and the Life God Gives**

**Key Idea:** You were spiritually dead, and God intervened — not because you earned it, but because He is rich in mercy. That same grace now calls you to walk in a life of purposeful, Spirit-empowered good works.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8-9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Spiritual death is Paul's diagnosis for every human apart from God — not weakness, not confusion, but complete unresponsiveness to God. Self-improvement cannot fix it.
* "But God" is the turning point of the entire passage. God acts out of His own mercy and love — not because we qualified.
* Prevenient grace means God was already at work in your life before you responded. Grace enables genuine faith; it does not replace it.
* You are God's *poiēma* — His poem, His masterpiece — crafted with intention and saved for a purpose already prepared.
* Good works are not the price of salvation. They are its fruit. Every area of life — including your finances — is a theater for those prepared works.

**Gospel Connection:** The gospel of Ephesians 2 is not "try harder." It is "God made you alive." Christ's death and resurrection are the ground of our co-resurrection with Him. When you trust Christ, you are united with Him — raised with Him, seated with Him. That union is the source of every good work that follows. Faith opens the hand to receive what God has already given.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your life right now do you sense the pull of spiritual drift — the quiet default toward what the culture values rather than what God prepared?
**2.** What would it look like to treat one specific financial or vocational decision this week as a "good work God prepared beforehand"?
**3.** How does knowing you are God's *poiēma* — crafted with purpose — change the way you see your daily responsibilities?

**Weekly Challenge:** Choose one area of your finances or daily schedule this week and deliberately ask: *"Is this walking in the good works God prepared for me?"* Write your answer in a journal entry before the week ends. Be specific — name the decision, name what faithfulness looks like, and name what you are trusting God to provide.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that when I was dead, You said 'But God.' Thank You for mercy I did not earn and grace I could not manufacture. Help me this week to walk in the good works You have already prepared — in my spending, my giving, my work, and my relationships. Make me the poem You intended. In Jesus' name, amen."
