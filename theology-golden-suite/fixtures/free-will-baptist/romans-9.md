## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Free Will Baptist
**Lesson Title:** *Vessels of Mercy: God's Sovereign Purpose and Human Responsibility in Romans 9*

Romans 9 is one of the most debated chapters in the New Testament, and for good reason — it stands at the intersection of God's sovereign freedom and genuine human responsibility. This lesson approaches the passage through a Free Will Baptist lens, affirming that God's purposes in history are real and powerful, while also insisting that election is conditional, Christ's atonement is universal, and prevenient grace enables every person to respond without compelling them.

**Main Theme:** God's freedom to show mercy does not override human responsibility to respond in faith.

**Lesson Summary:** Paul defends God's faithfulness to His covenant promises while wrestling with Israel's unbelief. The passage reveals that God's election operates through His foreknowledge, not through arbitrary selection, and that the "vessels of mercy" are those who respond to His gracious call.

**Key Takeaway:** God's mercy is genuinely available to every person, and our response to that mercy — not a predetermined decree — determines whether we become vessels of honor or dishonor.

**Audience Note:** Mid-life adults have often wrestled with questions of fairness, divine control, and personal responsibility. This passage meets them exactly there.

## Section 3: Theological Background

**Literary and Historical Context**

Romans 9–11 forms a single theological unit within Paul's letter, addressing a pointed objection: if Israel, God's chosen people, has largely rejected the Messiah, has God's word failed? Paul writes as a Jewish believer in profound grief over his kinsmen (9:2–3), establishing that his argument is not anti-Israel but deeply pastoral. The audience in Rome included both Jewish and Gentile believers navigating real tension over Israel's place in God's redemptive plan.

**The Nature of Election in Romans 9**

Paul's primary concern in 9:6–13 is not individual salvation but the corporate, historical purposes of God worked through chosen lineages. When Paul references Isaac over Ishmael and Jacob over Esau (9:7–13), he is explaining how God's covenant line was traced — not arbitrarily selecting individuals for heaven or hell. Crucially, the citation "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" (9:13, quoting Malachi 1:2–3) refers to nations and their historical roles, not to the eternal destiny of two individuals. Free Will Baptist theology affirms that election is conditional, grounded in God's foreknowledge (Romans 8:29) of who will freely respond to prevenient grace.

**Pharaoh and God's Hardening (9:17–18)**

The hardening of Pharaoh is frequently misread as God arbitrarily destroying a man He never gave a chance. Scripture's full account (Exodus 4–14) shows Pharaoh hardening his own heart first. God's "hardening" is best understood as God's judicial confirmation of choices Pharaoh had already made — a pattern consistent with Romans 1:24–28, where God "gave them over" to what they had already chosen. This preserves both God's sovereignty and genuine human responsibility.

**The Potter and Clay (9:20–24)**

The potter imagery draws directly from Jeremiah 18, where the original context is explicitly conditional: if a nation repents, God relents; if a nation turns to evil, God can reshape His purpose for it. The clay is not inert — Jeremiah's potter reworks the clay in response to what happens with it. Paul's point is that God, as Creator, has the sovereign right to determine how He responds to human choices. "Vessels of wrath" are those who persist in unbelief and resist mercy; "vessels of mercy" are those who respond to God's gracious call. This reading is consistent with 9:22's phrase "prepared for destruction," which in Greek (*katērtismena*) is a perfect passive participle that does not explicitly name God as the agent — a significant textual detail suggesting self-preparation through persistent rejection.

**Prevenient Grace and Universal Atonement**

Free Will Baptist theology holds that God's prevenient grace — His grace that goes before and enables response — is extended to every person, making genuine faith possible without making it inevitable. Romans 9:24 identifies the vessels of mercy as called "not from among Jews only, but also from among Gentiles," pointing toward the universal scope of God's saving intention. This aligns with 1 Timothy 2:4 and 2 Peter 3:9: God genuinely desires all to be saved. The chapter does not narrow salvation to a predetermined few; it defends God's freedom to extend mercy beyond ethnic Israel to all who respond in faith.

**Key Greek Insight**

The word translated "mercy" (*eleos*) appears repeatedly in 9:15–18 and carries the weight of covenant loyalty and compassionate action. God's mercy is not capricious — it is His consistent, faithful response to those who come to Him. His freedom to show mercy is not the freedom to withhold it arbitrarily, but the freedom to extend it beyond what human religious systems would allow.

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content

**Opening the Passage**

Turn with me to Romans 9, beginning at verse 1. Before we read a single word of Paul's argument, I want you to notice something about his emotional state. He writes:

> "I am telling the truth in Christ, I am not lying, my conscience testifies with me in the Holy Spirit, that I have great sorrow and unceasing grief in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were accursed, separated from Christ for the sake of my brothers and sisters, my kinsmen according to the flesh." (Romans 9:1–3, NASB)

Paul is not writing a cold theological treatise. He is writing out of heartbreak. His people — Israel — had largely rejected Jesus as Messiah, and Paul feels that rejection like a wound. He lists their privileges in verses 4–5: the adoption, the glory, the covenants, the Law, the temple worship, the promises, the patriarchs, and the Messiah Himself who came through their lineage. Everything pointed to Jesus, and still, many had not believed.

So the obvious question arises, and Paul states it directly in verse 6: *"It is not as though the word of God has failed."* That's the accusation he's answering. Has God broken His promise to Israel? Has His plan collapsed?

**God's Word Has Not Failed**

Paul's answer is that not everyone descended from Israel is truly Israel in the covenant sense. He traces this through two examples — Isaac and Ishmael, then Jacob and Esau. God's covenant line was never simply biological. It ran through the child of promise, not just the child of biology. This is not Paul inventing something new — this was always how God's purposes worked.

Now here's where people get tripped up. When Paul quotes, "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated," many readers assume God made an arbitrary decision about two individuals before they were born. But look at what Paul says in verse 11: the purpose was established "not because of works, but because of Him who calls." The choice was made before the twins were born — but that doesn't mean it was made without reference to anything. Romans 8:29 tells us God's election is grounded in His foreknowledge. God, who exists outside of time, foreknew who would respond to His grace. The selection of Jacob's line was God's sovereign freedom operating through His perfect knowledge — not a roll of divine dice.

And remember — Paul is talking about nations and their historical roles, not writing a verdict on whether Esau as an individual went to heaven or hell. Malachi 1, where that quote comes from, is about the nations of Israel and Edom, not individual eternal destinies.

**Pharaoh: Hardened or Self-Hardened?**

Paul then moves to Pharaoh in verses 17–18, and this is where many students get stuck. Did God harden Pharaoh's heart just to destroy him? Read Exodus carefully — Pharaoh hardened his own heart first, repeatedly. God's hardening came as a judicial confirmation of choices Pharaoh had already made. Think about what Romans 1 says: God "gave them over" to what they had already chosen. God's hardening is not God reaching into an innocent man and making him evil. It is God saying, "You have chosen this path — I will let that choice stand." That is terrifying, but it is just.

**The Potter and the Clay**

Verses 20–24 bring us to the potter and clay image, and this is where the lesson gets deeply personal. Paul draws from Jeremiah 18 — and if you read that passage, you'll discover something remarkable. The potter in Jeremiah's account reshapes the clay *in response to what happens with it*. If the nation repents, God relents. If the nation turns to evil, God reshapes His purpose. The clay is not inert. The potter is responsive.

Paul's point is that God, as our Creator, has the sovereign right to determine how He responds to human choices. The "vessels of wrath" in verse 22 are not people God arbitrarily condemned. The Greek word translated "prepared for destruction" is structured in a way that does not explicitly name God as the one doing the preparing — and that is not an accident. These are people who have prepared themselves through persistent rejection of mercy. The "vessels of mercy" in verse 23 are those who respond to God's gracious call — and verse 24 makes clear that call goes out to Jews and Gentiles alike.

**What This Means for You**

Here's the bottom line, and I want you to feel the weight of it: God's mercy is genuinely available to every person in this room and every person you love. Christ died for every person — not just a predetermined group. Prevenient grace — God's enabling grace that goes before our response — has already been extended to every human being, making real faith genuinely possible.

That means when you think about your spouse, your children, your parents, your siblings who haven't yet come to faith — they are not outside God's mercy. God desires them to be saved. He is not withholding grace from them. The question is whether they will respond. And that is exactly why your witness, your prayers, and your love matter. You are not trying to overcome God's will when you pray for a prodigal. You are partnering with it.

Romans 9 is not a chapter about who God has decided to exclude. It is a chapter about how wide God's mercy actually reaches — beyond ethnic Israel, beyond religious pedigree, to anyone who calls on His name. That is the God we serve. And that is the God worth telling others about.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — Individual Written Reflection**
*(8–10 minutes | Materials: paper or index cards, pens)*

Distribute an index card to each student. Ask them to write responses to two prompts — silently and honestly:

1. *Write the name (or initials) of one person in your family who has not yet responded to God's mercy.*
2. *Write one sentence describing how you have been praying for them — or why you haven't been.*

They will not share these cards publicly. After writing, ask students to hold the card and consider: "Romans 9 says God genuinely desires this person to be saved. He is not withholding grace from them. What is your role in their story?"

Close this activity by inviting two or three volunteers to share — not names, but what they felt while writing. Transition: "That tension you felt is exactly what Paul felt in verses 1–3."

**Adaptation:** For larger classes, skip verbal sharing entirely and move directly to prayer. For smaller classes, allow fuller discussion.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul opens Romans 9 with intense personal grief. What specifically was causing him sorrow, and why does his emotional state matter for how we interpret the chapter's theology?

**2.** **Understanding:** Paul uses the examples of Isaac and Esau to show that God's covenant line was never purely biological. How does this help answer the accusation that "God's word has failed"?

**3.** **Analysis:** The Greek word translated "prepared for destruction" in verse 22 does not explicitly name God as the agent. Why is that textual detail significant for how Free Will Baptists understand the "vessels of wrath"?

**4.** **Application:** If prevenient grace has genuinely been extended to every person — including family members who haven't believed — how should that change the way you pray for them and speak to them about faith?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Romans 9 ends with God's mercy extending to both Jews and Gentiles — anyone who responds to His call. Is there someone in your life you have quietly assumed is beyond God's reach? What would it look like to stop assuming that?

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged will speak about specific people they are praying for with renewed urgency. Watch for movement from fatalism ("it's up to God") toward active partnership ("I can pray and witness because God desires their salvation").

**Gospel Connection:** Romans 9 is not a chapter about exclusion — it is a defense of how wide God's mercy actually reaches. God "desires all people to be saved" (1 Timothy 2:4). Prevenient grace has already gone before every person you know, making genuine faith possible. When you pray for a prodigal family member, you are not fighting against God's will — you are partnering with it. The cross was for them too.

**Reflection Questions:**

**1.** Where have you been tempted to believe that someone you love is simply "not chosen" — and how does this passage challenge that assumption?

**2.** How does understanding God's hardening of Pharaoh as a judicial confirmation of Pharaoh's own choices change how you think about people who seem hardened to the gospel today?

**3.** What would it look like this week to pray for one family member with the kind of urgency Paul describes in Romans 9:1–3?

**Weekly Challenge:** Identify one person in your home or family circle who has not yet responded to God's mercy. Commit to praying for them daily this week — not with resignation, but with the confidence that God genuinely desires their salvation and has already extended grace toward them. Then look for one natural opportunity to express that you are praying for them.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that Your mercy is not narrow. Thank You that Christ died for every person — including the ones I love who haven't yet believed. Give me Paul's urgency and Your patience. Help me to pray faithfully, speak honestly, and trust that prevenient grace is already at work in hearts I cannot see. Make me a vessel of mercy in someone else's story. Amen."
