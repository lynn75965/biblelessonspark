## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Reformed Baptist (1689 LBCF)

**Lesson Title:** *Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of Sovereign Grace*

**Lesson Summary:** Hebrews 6:1–12 delivers one of Scripture's most sobering warnings alongside one of its most stabilizing assurances. The author calls believers away from spiritual infancy toward maturity, issues a severe warning against apostasy, and then grounds the reader's confidence not in human perseverance but in God's faithfulness to His covenant promises.

**Main Theme:** Genuine saving faith, produced by sovereign grace, presses forward toward maturity — and those who truly belong to God will do so, because God's purposes cannot fail.

**Key Takeaway:** The warning in Hebrews 6 does not undermine assurance — it clarifies what true faith looks like and points us back to the God who secures it.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults often carry real questions about assurance — their own and that of people they love. This passage meets that tension directly. Students in this age group have likely watched someone walk away from the faith, making the warning passages feel personal and urgent. Handle with pastoral care and theological precision.

## Section 3: Theological Background

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews is a sermonic letter written to Jewish Christians — likely in the late 50s or early 60s AD — who were under intense social and religious pressure to abandon Christianity and return to Judaism. The author's pastoral strategy throughout is to demonstrate Christ's absolute superiority over every element of the old covenant: angels, Moses, the Levitical priesthood, and the sacrificial system. Hebrews 6 sits within a larger unit (5:11–6:20) that interrupts the Melchizedek argument to deliver a pastoral warning and encouragement.

**The Call to Maturity (vv. 1–3)**

The "elementary teachings" (*arche*) listed — repentance, faith, baptisms, laying on of hands, resurrection, eternal judgment — are not dismissed as unimportant. They are foundational doctrines that should be the floor, not the ceiling, of Christian understanding. The author's concern is that these believers have stalled. Spiritual immaturity in mid-life adults is not merely an intellectual problem; it is a spiritual danger, because stagnation creates vulnerability to apostasy.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4–6)**

This is among the most debated texts in the New Testament. The key question: who are those described in verses 4–5?

The language is deliberately strong — "enlightened," "tasted of the heavenly gift," "partakers of the Holy Spirit," "tasted the good word of God." Reformed interpreters note that this language describes *experiential proximity* to grace, not necessarily *possession* of saving grace. The word "tasted" (*geuomai*) does not require full reception — it can describe a sampling. More critically, "partakers" (*metochos*) in Hebrews is used of those who share in an experience or community, not always of those who possess the thing itself (cf. Hebrews 3:14, where *metochos* of Christ requires holding firm to the end).

The warning, then, is addressed to those who have been embedded in the covenant community — baptized, instructed, exposed to the Spirit's work — but whose hearts remain unregenerate. To fall away from this position is to crucify Christ publicly, treating His sacrifice as worthless. The impossibility of restoration (*adunaton*) is not a denial of God's power but a description of the spiritual trajectory of one who has rejected the only remedy.

This interpretation aligns with 1 John 2:19: those who depart demonstrate they were never truly regenerate. The perseverance of the saints is not at risk here — it is being *argued for* by showing what the alternative looks like.

**Agricultural Imagery (vv. 7–8)**

Land that receives rain and produces useful crops is blessed. Land that receives the same rain but produces only thorns is worthless and near cursing. The rain — representing divine grace, the Word, the Spirit's ministry — falls on both. The difference is in the soil, which represents the heart. This mirrors Jesus's parable of the soils (Matthew 13). Fruit is the evidence of genuine regeneration, not the cause of it.

**Assurance Grounded in God (vv. 9–12)**

The author shifts tone: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." This is not sentimental optimism. The author grounds assurance in God's justice — God is not unrighteous to forget their work and love (v. 10). The elect will persevere because God's covenant faithfulness, not their own resolve, secures them. The call to "show the same diligence" (v. 11) is not a works-based condition for salvation; it is the expected fruit of genuine regeneration, confirming election through active faith.

**Key Greek Terms:**
- *Arche* (v. 1): beginning, foundation
- *Geuomai* (v. 4): to taste, sample
- *Metochos* (v. 4): partaker, sharer
- *Adunaton* (v. 4): impossible

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content

Open your Bibles to Hebrews 6, beginning at verse 1. We are going to read a passage that has caused more theological debate than almost any other in the New Testament — and when we're done, I want you to see that it is not a passage designed to terrify the genuine believer. It is a passage designed to clarify what genuine belief actually looks like, and to anchor your assurance in something far more stable than your own track record.

The author opens with a command: "Let us press on to maturity." Not "let us arrive at maturity." Press on — it is a journey, a direction, a posture. The word carries the sense of being carried forward, of momentum. And the author is concerned because these believers have lost that momentum. They are still chewing on the basics — repentance, faith, resurrection, judgment — foundational truths that should have been the launching pad for deeper understanding of Christ and His priesthood. Think about what it would mean to spend twenty years as a carpenter and still not know how to use a level. The basics matter, but they are not the destination.

Now we reach verses 4 through 6, and this is where people get nervous. The author describes people who have been "enlightened," who have "tasted of the heavenly gift," who have been "partakers of the Holy Spirit," who have "tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come" — and then says that if they fall away, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance.

Here is the question every careful reader must ask: Is this describing a genuinely regenerate person who loses their salvation? The answer, within the full counsel of Scripture, is no — and here is why.

Notice the word "tasted." In the original Greek, *geuomai* does not require full reception. It means to sample, to come into contact with. Hebrews 2:9 uses the same word to say Christ "tasted" death for us — and that is clearly full experience. But the word itself does not demand that. Context determines meaning. And the context here is a community of people who have been baptized, instructed, exposed to the preaching of the Word, and present when the Spirit moved — but whose hearts, the author is warning, may not have been genuinely transformed.

The word translated "partakers" — *metochos* — is crucial. Later in Hebrews 3:14, the author says "we have become partakers of Christ, if we hold fast the beginning of our assurance firm until the end." The *metochos* relationship with Christ is confirmed by perseverance. Here in chapter 6, the *metochos* relationship with the Holy Spirit describes people who have shared in the Spirit's activity within the community — they have witnessed miracles, experienced conviction, been moved by worship — without being regenerated by the Spirit.

This is not a theoretical category. Think about Jesus's parable of the soils. The seed falls on rocky ground, springs up with apparent life, and then withers because there is no root. The rain falls. The Word is preached. But the heart was never truly broken and remade. First John 2:19 says plainly: "They went out from us, but they were not really of us; for if they had been of us, they would have remained with us."

So what is the warning? It is this: if you have been embedded in this covenant community — baptized, taught, exposed to everything God has graciously provided — and you publicly turn away from Christ, you are treating His sacrifice as worthless. You are, as the text says, "crucifying to themselves the Son of God again and putting Him to open shame." And there is no other sacrifice. There is no Plan B. The impossibility of restoration is not a statement about God's power — it is a statement about the spiritual logic of rejecting the only remedy that exists.

Now look at the agricultural image in verses 7 and 8. Two fields receive the same rain. One produces useful crops. One produces thorns. The rain is the same — the Word, the Spirit's ministry, the means of grace God has provided. The difference is the soil. The heart. And this is exactly what Jesus described in Matthew 13. Fruit is the evidence of genuine regeneration. It does not earn salvation — it confirms it.

Then the tone shifts in verse 9, and this shift matters enormously. "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, things that accompany salvation." The author is not uncertain about these believers. He is confident — not because they have been perfectly faithful, but because God is not unrighteous to forget their work and the love they have shown toward His name. God's justice, God's covenant faithfulness, is the anchor of assurance. Not your performance. Not your consistency. God's character.

The call to "show the same diligence" in verse 11 is not a condition you must meet to stay saved. It is the expected fruit of a heart that has been genuinely transformed by sovereign grace. Those who have been effectually called will persevere — not because they are strong enough, but because the God who began the work is faithful to complete it.

Here is the challenge I want to leave with you: If this passage makes you anxious, let that anxiety drive you not to despair but to examination. Are you pressing on toward maturity? Is there fruit? Is your faith active — showing love, serving, enduring? Those are the marks of the genuine work of God in a soul. And if you see them, even imperfectly, you are looking at evidence of God's grace at work — not your own achievement.

Pray this week for the courage to press on, and for the humility to trust that the God who called you is the God who keeps you.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — "Tasted or Transformed?" Sorting Exercise**
*(Individual written reflection — 5–7 minutes)*

**Materials:** Index card or half-sheet of paper for each student

Give each student a card. Display or read aloud these four descriptors from Hebrews 6:4–5: *enlightened, tasted the heavenly gift, partaker of the Holy Spirit, tasted the good word of God.* Ask students to write privately:

- "Based on Section 3's distinction between experiential proximity and genuine possession of saving grace, which of these could describe someone in your church who later walked away — and what would be *missing* from that description?"
- "What does fruit (vv. 7–8) look like in your own home and family relationships right now?"

Do not collect. Allow 2 minutes of silence, then invite 2–3 volunteers to share one observation.

*Adaptation: Smaller classes can discuss openly; larger classes pair up first.*

---

**Activity 2 — Two Fields, One Rain**
*(Partner discussion — 3–4 minutes)*

Draw or display two simple rectangles labeled "Field A" and "Field B." Both receive rain. Ask partners to fill in: What does the rain represent in this passage? What does each field's fruit (or thorns) reveal — and what does it *not* reveal about the rain itself?

Bring the class back together and connect responses to the doctrine of sovereign grace: God's means are not deficient; the difference lies in the heart He has or has not regenerated.

*Adaptation: Solo journaling works equally well for introverted classes.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** According to Hebrews 6:1–3, what are the "elementary teachings" the author lists, and what does his command to "press on to maturity" tell us about where those teachings should leave us?

**2.** **Understanding:** The lesson distinguished between the Greek words *geuomai* (tasted) and *metochos* (partaker) and their implications. In your own words, why does that distinction matter for interpreting verses 4–6 correctly?

**3.** **Analysis:** How does 1 John 2:19 — "they went out from us, but they were not really of us" — function as a theological companion to the warning in Hebrews 6:4–6? What does it clarify about the nature of apostasy?

**4.** **Application:** The author shifts in verse 9 to ground assurance in God's justice and covenant faithfulness rather than the believers' performance. How does that shift change the way you think about your own assurance, or the assurance of someone in your family you are concerned about?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closed with this challenge: "If this passage makes you anxious, let that anxiety drive you not to despair but to examination." What specific fruit — active faith, love shown, endurance — do you see in your own life that reflects the genuine work of sovereign grace?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can articulate that the warning targets those with *experiential proximity* to grace rather than genuine regeneration, and who understand that assurance rests on God's covenant faithfulness, not human consistency. Confusion between *metochos* and full possession of saving grace is the most common misreading to gently correct.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged will begin examining their own spiritual momentum rather than only analyzing others. Watch for honest self-assessment, willingness to name areas of stagnation, and a posture of trust in God's keeping power rather than anxiety about their own performance.

**Closing Reflection:** "If your assurance rests entirely on your own track record, what does that reveal about where you believe the power of salvation ultimately lies?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of Sovereign Grace**
*Hebrews 6:1–12*

---

**Key Idea:** Hebrews 6 does not exist to shake the confidence of genuine believers — it exists to clarify what genuine faith looks like and to redirect our assurance toward the God who secures it by His own covenant faithfulness.

**Memory Verse:** "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation, though we are speaking in this way." — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The call to "press on to maturity" is a call to direction and momentum, not perfection. Spiritual stagnation is a danger, not just an inconvenience.
* The people described in verses 4–6 have experienced *proximity* to grace — baptism, instruction, the Spirit's work in the community — but the language does not require that they were genuinely regenerated.
* Fruit is the *evidence* of regeneration, not the *cause* of it. Two fields receive the same rain; what grows reveals the condition of the soil (vv. 7–8).
* Assurance is anchored in God's justice and covenant faithfulness (v. 10), not in the consistency of your own performance.
* Those effectually called by sovereign grace will persevere — not because they are strong enough, but because God's purposes cannot fail.

**Gospel Connection:** The warning in Hebrews 6 points to a sobering reality: there is no Plan B beyond Christ. His sacrifice is the only remedy for sin, and to publicly reject it after deep exposure to the covenant community is to treat that sacrifice as worthless. But for those whom God has regenerated by His Spirit, this warning is not a threat to assurance — it is a clarifying mirror. The same God who effectually called you is the God who keeps you. Your confidence rests not on how well you have held onto Him, but on the certainty that He holds you.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your life — especially in your home and family relationships — do you see the fruit that accompanies genuine regeneration? Where do you sense stagnation?
**2.** Have you ever watched someone walk away from the faith? How does the distinction between experiential proximity and genuine saving grace help you think about that experience?
**3.** What would it look like, practically, to "press on toward maturity" in your specific season of life this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** Identify one area of spiritual stagnation in your life — a discipline neglected, a relationship where faith has gone quiet, a truth you know but have stopped living. Bring it before God this week not with self-condemnation, but with the confidence that the God who began His work in you is faithful to complete it (Philippians 1:6).

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that my assurance does not rest on the strength of my grip but on the certainty of Yours. Where I have drifted, draw me back. Where I have stalled, grant momentum. Produce in me the fruit that confirms Your grace is real and at work. I press on — not in my own strength, but because You are faithful to complete what You have begun. In Christ's name, Amen."
