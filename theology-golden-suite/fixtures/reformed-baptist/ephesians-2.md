## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Reformed Baptist (1689 LBCF)

**Lesson Title:** Dead and Made Alive: The Sovereign Grace of God in Salvation

**Lesson Summary:** Ephesians 2:1–10 stands as one of Scripture's most concentrated declarations of sovereign grace, tracing the believer's journey from spiritual death to resurrection life entirely by God's initiative. Paul dismantles every foundation of human pride by showing that salvation originates in God's mercy, is secured through Christ's work, and is applied by the Spirit — all before and apart from human merit. The passage culminates in the breathtaking truth that even the faith by which we believe is itself a gift from God.

**Main Theme:** Salvation is entirely the work of a sovereign God who raises spiritually dead sinners to new life through grace alone, for His glory alone.

**Key Takeaway:** Every element of your salvation — from the first stirring of faith to your final glorification — flows from God's sovereign mercy, not your own choosing or effort.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults in this age range often carry either spiritual complacency or quiet pride about their long-standing faith. This passage confronts both tendencies by anchoring assurance not in personal faithfulness but in God's unshakable, sovereign grace.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Paul wrote Ephesians from Roman imprisonment, likely around AD 60–62. Unlike his letters to Corinth or Galatia, Ephesians addresses no specific crisis — it is a sustained theological exposition of God's eternal purpose to unite all things in Christ. Chapter 2 forms the experiential heart of that argument. Having declared God's cosmic purpose in chapter 1, Paul now grounds it in the personal: *you* were dead, and *God* made you alive.

The passage divides naturally into two movements: verses 1–3 describe the human condition apart from grace; verses 4–10 describe God's decisive intervention. The structural pivot is the two-word phrase in verse 4: "But God." Everything before it is death; everything after it is life.

**Total Depravity: More Than Moral Failure**

The Greek word *nekros* (dead) in verse 1 is not metaphorical weakness — it is the language of a corpse. Paul is not describing someone who is spiritually sick and needs assistance; he is describing someone with no capacity to respond to God whatsoever. Verse 2 specifies the mechanisms of this death: the "course of this world," the "prince of the power of the air," and the "lusts of our flesh." These are not external pressures on an otherwise neutral person — they are the native environment of the unregenerate soul. Verse 3 seals the indictment: "we were by nature children of wrath." The word *physis* (nature) indicates this is not a learned behavior but an inherited condition rooted in Adam's fall. This is total depravity in its fullest biblical expression, and it forecloses any possibility that fallen humanity contributes to its own salvation.

**"But God": Unconditional Election and Irresistible Grace**

Verses 4–6 introduce God's response — and it is entirely His initiative. The motivating cause is not human seeking but divine character: "His great love" and "His rich mercy." Election is unconditional because the elect were dead — dead men make no choices that could merit divine favor. The language of verse 5, "made us alive together with Christ," mirrors the resurrection language Paul uses elsewhere. Just as Christ's physical resurrection was an act of sovereign divine power on a dead body, so regeneration is God's sovereign act upon a spiritually dead soul. Regeneration precedes faith — one must be made alive before one can believe. This is irresistible grace: the Spirit's effectual calling cannot be ultimately refused by those whom God has chosen.

**The Gift of Faith**

Verse 8 requires careful exegesis: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God." The phrase "that not of yourselves" in Greek uses a neuter demonstrative (*touto*) that most Reformed interpreters understand as referring to the entire salvation event — including the faith itself — as God's gift. This reading aligns with Philippians 1:29 ("it has been granted to you... to believe") and John 6:44. Saving faith is not the human contribution to an otherwise divine salvation; it is itself a gracious gift sovereignly bestowed.

**Good Works: Fruit, Not Root**

Verse 10 closes the argument with precision: believers are God's "workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand." The Greek *poiema* (workmanship) implies an artifact — something made entirely by its maker. Good works are the necessary fruit of genuine salvation, evidence of the Spirit's ongoing work of sanctification, but they are never the basis of justification. This guards against both antinomianism (works don't matter) and moralism (works earn standing before God).

**Cross-References:** Romans 3:10–12; John 6:44; Titus 3:5; Philippians 1:29; Romans 8:29–30

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — "Picture This" Hook (5 minutes)**

Open with this scenario: "Picture a man who has been in a medically induced coma for three years. Doctors have tried everything. Then one morning, with no warning, he opens his eyes, sits up, and speaks clearly. Now — did that man *choose* to wake up? Did something in him *decide* to respond? Of course not. The life came from outside him entirely."

Ask the class: "What would it take to make a spiritually dead person alive? And who has to do the work?"

Let two or three students respond briefly. Do not resolve the question yet — let it hang.

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Discussion Prompt (3 minutes)**

Ask: "When you think about the moment you came to faith, how much of it felt like *your* decision, and how much felt like something happened *to* you that you couldn't explain?"

**Transition:** "Paul answers both of those questions with surgical precision in Ephesians 2. Let's read it together and let the text speak."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Ephesians 2, beginning at verse 1. We're going to read the whole passage together before we unpack it, because the argument only works when you see it whole.

Read Ephesians 2:1–10 aloud.

Now — before we go verse by verse, I want you to notice the shape of this passage. Verses 1 through 3 are one long, unrelenting description of what you and I were before God intervened. Then verse 4 hits like a door swinging open: "But God." And everything from verse 4 to verse 10 is what God did, why He did it, and what it produces. That's the architecture. Death — then life. Us — then God.

**What We Were: Spiritually Dead**

Paul doesn't say we were spiritually weak, or spiritually confused, or spiritually searching. He says we were *dead* — the Greek word is *nekros*, the same word used for a physical corpse. Now, why does that matter? Because a corpse doesn't cooperate with the doctor. A corpse doesn't decide to get better. A corpse has zero capacity to initiate anything. Paul is not describing people who needed a little help from God to tip the scales. He is describing people with no spiritual pulse whatsoever.

And then he tells us what that death looked like in practice. We followed "the course of this world" — meaning the entire value system of a culture that has organized itself around everything except God. We were under the influence of "the prince of the power of the air" — Paul's way of describing Satan's dominion over the unregenerate world. And we "lived in the lusts of our flesh, indulging the desires of the flesh and of the mind." Notice: Paul includes himself. "Among them we too all formerly lived." This is not Paul pointing at pagans across town. He is saying: this was us.

Verse 3 closes the indictment with the most sobering phrase in the section: "we were by nature children of wrath." The word *physis* means nature — not habit, not environment, not bad choices. Nature. This is what we were at the root level, inherited from Adam, deserving of God's righteous judgment. Total depravity doesn't mean we were as evil as we could possibly be. It means every part of us — mind, will, affections — was bent away from God, incapable of turning toward Him on our own.

**"But God": The Two Words That Change Everything**

Verse 4. "But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us..."

Think about what those two words carry. Everything in verses 1–3 is true of you — and then God acts. Not because you sought Him. Not because you showed potential. Not because He looked down the corridor of time and saw that you would eventually choose Him. He acted because of *His* great love and *His* rich mercy. The motivation is entirely internal to God. This is unconditional election — God's sovereign choice rooted in His own character, not in anything He foresaw in us.

Verse 5: He "made us alive together with Christ." This is the language of resurrection. Just as Christ's resurrection was a sovereign act of divine power on a dead body — the body didn't cooperate, the body didn't decide to rise — so regeneration is God's sovereign act on a spiritually dead soul. The Spirit comes in, and the dead hear the voice of the Son of God, and they live. That's John 6:44 and John 5:25 in experiential form. Regeneration precedes faith. You don't believe your way into new life — you are given new life, and then you believe.

**The Gift Nobody Talks About**

Verse 8 is one of the most carefully worded sentences in all of Paul's letters: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God."

Here's where it gets precise. Some people read this and say: "Grace is God's part, faith is my part, and together we get salvation." But that's not what Paul says. The phrase "that not of yourselves" — in Greek, *touto ouk ex humōn* — uses a demonstrative that points back to the whole salvation event, faith included. Salvation in its entirety, including the faith through which you believe, is the gift of God. This aligns perfectly with Philippians 1:29, where Paul says it has been "granted" — literally, gifted — to you to believe. Faith is not your contribution to an otherwise divine process. Faith is itself a gracious gift sovereignly given by the Spirit through effectual calling. This is irresistible grace: when God purposes to save someone, He gives them the very faith by which they come to Him.

Verse 9 makes the implication explicit: "not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." If faith were your independent contribution, you could boast — you made the decisive move. But if faith itself is a gift, then boasting is eliminated at every level. God gets all the glory, because God did all the work.

**What You Were Made For**

Verse 10 is the landing point: "For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

The word *poiema* — workmanship — is where we get the English word "poem." You are God's crafted work, His artifact. And the good works you now do? God prepared them beforehand. They are the evidence of His ongoing work in you through sanctification, the fruit of the new life He sovereignly gave. Good works are never the root of your standing before God — Christ's righteousness is your standing. But good works are the necessary, God-ordained fruit of genuine salvation. The truly elect will persevere in holiness — not because they muster the willpower, but because God continues the work He began.

Here is the challenge I want you to carry into your week: If your salvation is entirely God's sovereign work — from the first breath of spiritual life to the final day of glory — then your prayer life should look like someone who knows that. Go home and talk to God about your salvation as if He did all of it. Because He did.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — "But God" Timeline (Small Group Discussion, 10–12 minutes)**

Materials needed: None

Divide into groups of 3–4. Give each group this prompt: "Walk through Ephesians 2:1–10 together and identify every verb that has God as its subject — what God *did*. Then list every verb that describes what *we* were or did before verse 4."

After 6–7 minutes, bring groups back together. Ask one spokesperson per group to share their list. Guide the class to notice the overwhelming asymmetry: the human verbs are all passive or negative; God's verbs are all active and life-giving.

**Debrief question:** "Based on this verb exercise alone, who is doing the saving?"

*Adaptation: For a smaller class, do this as one group with the teacher guiding.*

**Activity 2 — Personal Devotional Audit (Individual Reflection, 4–5 minutes)**

This is not shared aloud — it is personal preparation for the closing challenge from the teaching.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses the Greek word *nekros* to describe our condition before salvation. What does that word mean, and why is "dead" more significant than saying we were spiritually "sick" or "weak"?

**2.** **Understanding:** What three forces does Paul identify in verses 2–3 that characterized life apart from grace? How do these work together to describe total depravity rather than simply bad behavior?

**3.** **Analysis:** Why do Reformed interpreters argue that the phrase "that not of yourselves" in verse 8 refers to the entire salvation event — including faith itself — rather than just the grace component? What other Scripture passages support this reading?

**4.** **Application:** If regeneration precedes faith — meaning God makes the dead alive before they believe — how should that truth change the way you pray for an unbelieving family member or friend?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Verse 10 calls believers God's *poiema* — His crafted workmanship. How does understanding your good works as God-prepared fruit rather than self-generated effort reshape how you approach your devotional life and daily obedience?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can articulate the *nekros* / corpse distinction without prompting, who correctly identify "But God" as the structural and theological pivot, and who can explain why faith as a gift eliminates all boasting. Students who conflate faith with human contribution have not yet grasped verse 8's precision.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Genuine understanding surfaces when students speak of prayer with greater dependence, express wonder rather than pride about their conversion, and frame their good works as response to grace rather than contribution toward standing before God.

**Closing Reflection:** "If God prepared your good works beforehand and gave you the faith to believe, what would it look like to spend one week in prayer treating your salvation as entirely His gift — and nothing of your own?"

## Section 8: Group Handout (Standalone)

**Dead and Made Alive: The Sovereign Grace of God in Salvation**

**Key Idea:** Salvation is not a partnership between God and the sinner — it is entirely God's sovereign act of raising the spiritually dead to new life through Christ, for His glory alone.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8–9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Before God intervened, every person is spiritually *dead* — not sick, not searching, but a corpse with zero capacity to reach toward God.
* The two words "But God" (v. 4) are the hinge of the entire passage and the hinge of every believer's story. God acts first, always.
* Even the faith by which you believe is a gift of sovereign grace — Ephesians 2:8 and Philippians 1:29 together make this unmistakable.
* You are God's *poiema* — His crafted poem, His workmanship. Good works are the fruit He prepared for you to walk in, never the root of your standing before Him.
* Assurance of salvation rests not on your faithfulness to God, but on God's faithfulness to His own sovereign purpose.

**Gospel Connection:** Ephesians 2 does not describe a salvation that God made *possible* and you made *actual* by your decision. It describes a resurrection — God raising the dead by His own power, in His own time, for His own glory. Christ's death secured this new life effectually for all whom the Father chose. The Spirit applies it irresistibly through effectual calling. Your conversion was not the moment you found God; it was the moment God, who had always known you, opened your eyes. That is the gospel of sovereign grace.

**Reflection Questions:**

**1.** When you think about your own coming to faith, what does it mean to you now that God gave you even the faith to believe?

**2.** How does knowing your good works were "prepared beforehand" by God change the pressure you feel to perform or prove yourself spiritually?

**3.** Who in your life needs you to pray for them with the confidence that God can raise the spiritually dead — because He did exactly that for you?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, set aside ten minutes each day to pray through your salvation as if God did every part of it — because He did. Thank Him for the specific grace of the faith He gave you. Ask Him to do the same sovereign work in someone you love who does not yet believe.

**Prayer:** "Father, I confess that I came to You with nothing — dead in my trespasses, enslaved to the world, and under Your just wrath. You made me alive. You gave me faith. You seated me with Christ. All of it was You. Let that truth silence every whisper of pride and deepen every prayer I offer. Make my life the good work You prepared it to be, for Your glory alone. Amen."
