## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** General Baptist — Arminian, evangelistic, prevenient grace, conditional election based on God's foreknowledge of faith, unlimited atonement, eternal security

**Lesson Title:** *The Potter's Purpose: God's Mercy, Human Freedom, and the Gospel for All*

**Lesson Summary:** Romans 9 is one of the most debated chapters in Scripture, and mid-life adults deserve an honest, grounded engagement with it. This lesson examines Paul's anguish for Israel, God's sovereign freedom to show mercy, and the consistent thread of human responsibility running through the passage. Students will see that God's purposes in election are rooted in His foreknowledge and His desire that all people come to saving faith.

**Main Theme:** God is sovereign in His mercy, and that mercy — offered through Christ — is available to every person who responds in faith.

**Key Takeaway:** God's freedom to show mercy is not a barrier to salvation — it is the very foundation of hope for every person you know who doesn't yet know Christ.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults often carry both theological curiosity and real-world urgency about family members, coworkers, and friends who are far from God. This passage speaks directly to that burden.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Structural Context**

Romans 9–11 functions as a single theological unit addressing one urgent question: Has God's word to Israel failed? (9:6). Paul does not pivot away from his gospel argument — he deepens it. Chapter 9 establishes God's sovereign freedom in mercy; chapter 10 establishes human responsibility to believe; chapter 11 holds both in tension with the hope of Israel's future restoration. Teachers must resist reading chapter 9 in isolation, which distorts its meaning.

**Historical Context**

Paul writes to a mixed Jewish-Gentile congregation in Rome, likely around AD 57. Jewish Christians were wrestling with why most of Israel had rejected their Messiah. Was God's covenant faithfulness in question? Paul's answer is pastoral and theological: God's purposes have always operated through a chosen line within Israel (Isaac, not Ishmael; Jacob, not Esau), and those purposes now expand through Christ to include all who believe.

**Key Theological Concepts**

*Election and Foreknowledge:* Paul's statement that God loved Jacob and hated Esau (v.13, quoting Malachi 1:2–3) refers to national election — God's choice of Israel's covenant line through Jacob — not individual eternal destiny. The language of "hating" is a Semitic idiom for preference or lesser priority, not divine animosity. From a General Baptist perspective, election is conditional: God elects "in Christ" those whom He foreknew would respond in faith (Romans 8:29). This is not God reacting to human choices but God's eternal knowledge embracing those who freely receive His grace.

*Prevenient Grace and Pharaoh:* The hardening of Pharaoh (vv.17–18) is frequently misread as God arbitrarily choosing to damn someone. Exodus reveals that Pharaoh first hardened his own heart (Exodus 8:15, 32). God's hardening was judicial — a confirmation and intensification of Pharaoh's own willful rebellion. Prevenient grace was extended; Pharaoh repeatedly refused it. God's sovereign act worked through and after Pharaoh's own choices.

*The Potter and the Clay (vv.20–24):* Paul borrows from Jeremiah 18, where the potter reshapes clay that has been marred. The point is God's sovereign right to determine the purpose and timing of His mercy — not that individuals have no moral agency. The "vessels of wrath" (v.22) are those who have persistently rejected God; the "vessels of mercy" (v.23) are those who have received it. God's patience with vessels of wrath (He "endured" them) reflects His desire that even they might repent.

*"I Will Have Mercy on Whom I Have Mercy" (v.15):* This quotation from Exodus 33:19 establishes that mercy is never owed — it is always a gift. But this does not mean mercy is withheld arbitrarily. The broader scriptural witness, including 1 Timothy 2:3–4 and 2 Peter 3:9, makes clear God desires all people to be saved. God's freedom in mercy means no one can demand it; it does not mean God secretly desires some to perish.

**Greek Word Insight**

The word translated "hardened" in verse 18 is *sklērunō* — to make stiff or stubborn. In the Septuagint, this same word describes Pharaoh's self-hardening. Paul's use carries the sense of a judicial confirmation, not an originating cause.

**Cross-References**
Romans 8:29–30; Exodus 4:21; 8:15, 32; Malachi 1:2–3; Jeremiah 18:1–6; 1 Timothy 2:3–4; 2 Peter 3:9

## Section 4: Opening Activities

Bring a lump of clay or modeling compound to class. Before saying anything, set it on the table where everyone can see it. Let the silence create curiosity. ## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Romans 9. Before we read a single verse, I want you to notice something. Paul opens this chapter not with an argument — but with anguish.

Verse 1: "I am telling the truth in Christ, I am not lying, my conscience testifies with me in the Holy Spirit, that I have great sorrow and unceasing grief in my heart." And then verse 3: "For I could wish that I myself were accursed, separated from Christ for the sake of my brethren, my kinsmen according to the flesh."

Do you feel the weight of that? Paul is saying, "If it were possible — if it could somehow work — I would trade my own salvation for Israel's." That's not theology. That's a broken heart. And it matters that we start here, because Romans 9 is not primarily an abstract debate about predestination. It grows out of a pastor's grief over people he loves who are perishing.

Here's the question that drives the whole chapter: Has God's word failed? Has God broken His covenant promises to Israel? Because most of Israel has rejected Jesus as Messiah, and that looks like a problem.

Paul's answer begins in verse 6: "But it is not as though the word of God has failed." And he explains why. God's covenant purposes have always operated through a chosen line within Israel — not every biological descendant automatically. Isaac, not Ishmael. Jacob, not Esau. God's word hasn't failed because God's purposes were never based on ethnicity alone.

Now, here is where this gets important for us. Paul quotes from Malachi: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated." That sounds severe. But in the ancient Near East, "love and hate" in this kind of comparison is a way of expressing priority or preference — not personal animosity. God chose the covenant line through Jacob. That's the point. And critically, Paul says this choice was made before the twins were born, "so that God's purpose according to His choice would stand, not because of works but because of Him who calls."

From a General Baptist perspective, this is God's foreknowledge at work. Romans 8:29 tells us God foreknew those who would respond in faith. God's election is not arbitrary — it is grounded in His eternal knowledge of those who would freely receive His grace. God doesn't choose people despite their faith; He chooses people in connection with the faith He foreknew they would exercise. That's not God reacting to us — it's God's eternal wisdom embracing those who come to Christ.

Think about what that means for the person you thought of during our opening question. God's election is not a locked door keeping your coworker out. It is the very framework by which God reaches toward every person who will respond.

Now Paul addresses Pharaoh, and this is where people get tangled. Verse 17: "For the Scripture says to Pharaoh, 'For this very purpose I raised you up, to demonstrate My power in you, and that My name might be proclaimed throughout the whole earth.'" And verse 18: "So then He has mercy on whom He desires, and He hardens whom He desires."

Does that mean God picked Pharaoh to be damned? No. Go back to Exodus. Pharaoh hardened his own heart first — Exodus 8:15, Exodus 8:32. God's hardening came after Pharaoh's repeated, willful rejection. What God did was confirm and intensify a direction Pharaoh had already chosen. The Greek word for "hardened" here — *sklērunō* — carries the sense of making stiff or stubborn. It's a judicial act, not an originating one. God did not create Pharaoh's rebellion. He worked through it.

Prevenient grace — God's grace that goes before and enables every person to respond — was extended to Pharaoh. He refused it, again and again. God's hardening was the consequence of that refusal, not the cause of it.

Then Paul turns to the potter and the clay. Look at verse 20: "On the contrary, who are you, O man, who answers back to God? The thing molded will not say to the molder, 'Why did you make me like this,' will it?" This is not saying human beings are passive lumps with no moral agency. Remember the original context in Jeremiah 18 — the potter reshapes clay that has been marred. The image is about God's sovereign right to determine how and when He works, not about God creating people for destruction.

The "vessels of wrath" in verse 22 are those who have persistently resisted God. Notice what Paul says about them — God "endured" them "with much patience." That word "endured" tells you something about God's heart. He is not eager to judge. He is patient, waiting, hoping. That matches perfectly with 1 Timothy 2:3–4, where God "desires all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth."

God's freedom in mercy — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" — means no one can demand it, no one earns it, no one deserves it. But it does not mean God secretly wants some people to perish. The whole sweep of Scripture contradicts that. God's freedom in mercy is the foundation of hope, not a ceiling on it.

So here is where this lands for us today, practically. You have people in your workplace, in your neighborhood, in your family who don't know Christ. Romans 9 does not give you an excuse to stop praying for them or pursuing them with the gospel. It gives you a reason to pray with confidence — because the God who is sovereign in mercy desires their salvation even more than you do.

Paul's grief in verse 1 is the model. He didn't say, "Well, if God has elected them, He'll get them." He said, "I have unceasing grief in my heart." Sovereign grace and passionate evangelism are not opposites. They are partners.

Here is your challenge this week: That person you thought of at the beginning of class — pray for them by name every day this week. And look for one moment to serve them, bless them, or speak a word of gospel hope into their life. The Potter is still working. Be willing to be the hands He uses.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: The Clay in Your Hands**
*Materials: Small portions of modeling clay or Play-Doh, one per student*
*Time: 6–8 minutes*

Distribute clay as students arrive or at the start of this activity. Ask each person to hold it and shape it however they like while you read Romans 9:20–23 aloud. Then ask: "What did your clay resist? What did it simply receive?" Let 2–3 students respond briefly. Draw the connection: the clay's point is not passivity — it's that the clay doesn't dictate the potter's purpose or timing. God's sovereign mercy shapes us; we receive or resist it.

*Small class adaptation (under 8): Do this as a full group conversation. Larger class: pair-share before opening to the room.*

---

**Activity 2: Intercession by Name — A Focused Prayer Practice**
*Materials: Index cards or small paper, pens*
*Time: 5–7 minutes*

Ask each student to write one name — a coworker, neighbor, or family member who doesn't yet know Christ. Beneath the name, they write one sentence: "God, You desire \_\_\_\_\_ to be saved. I am asking You to work in their life this week." Collect cards (anonymously if preferred) and close the activity by praying over the stack aloud, asking God to move in each unnamed situation. Students keep their own card as a weekly prayer reminder.

*Adaptation: In a larger class, students can pray silently over their card while you pray aloud.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul opens Romans 9 with personal grief rather than theological argument. What specifically does he say in verses 1–3, and what does that emotional starting point tell us about the purpose of the chapter?

**2.** **Understanding:** How does the Jacob-and-Esau example (vv. 10–13) relate to national covenant purposes rather than individual eternal destiny? What difference does that distinction make?

**3.** **Analysis:** Walk through the sequence of events with Pharaoh. Based on what we covered from Exodus 8:15 and 8:32, why is God's hardening best understood as judicial confirmation rather than an originating cause? How does the Greek word *sklērunō* support that reading?

**4.** **Application:** The lesson connected God's sovereign freedom in mercy to evangelistic confidence rather than evangelistic paralysis. How does knowing that God "desires all men to be saved" (1 Timothy 2:3–4) change the way you approach a conversation with someone spiritually resistant at work?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Paul said he had "unceasing grief" for people he loved who were perishing. Is there someone in your life who produces that kind of burden in you? What is one concrete step you could take toward them this week?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can articulate the difference between God's sovereign freedom in mercy and the idea of arbitrary exclusion. Strong responses will reference the potter's patience ("endured with much patience," v.22) and connect 1 Timothy 2:3–4 to the Romans 9 argument without prompting.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students are grasping the lesson when they move from theological curiosity to missional urgency — when they stop asking "Does God want to save everyone?" and start asking "Who am I going to pursue this week?" Watch for students who name a specific person rather than speaking in generalities.

**Closing Reflection:** "Paul was willing to wish himself accursed for the sake of people he loved. Who in your life is worth that kind of love from you?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**The Potter's Purpose: God's Mercy, Human Freedom, and the Gospel for All**

**Key Idea:** God is completely free in His mercy — no one earns it or demands it — and that same sovereign mercy is the foundation of hope for every person who has not yet responded to Christ.

**Memory Verse:** "For this reason it is by faith, in order that it may be in accordance with grace, so that the promise will be guaranteed to all the descendants." — Romans 4:16 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Paul's grief over Israel in Romans 9:1–3 models the heart every believer should carry for people far from God — not detached theology, but genuine anguish.
* God's choice of Jacob over Esau was about the covenant line through which the Messiah would come — not a declaration that Esau was personally condemned.
* Pharaoh hardened his own heart first (Exodus 8:15, 32). God's hardening was a judicial response to Pharaoh's repeated, willful rejection — not the originating cause of his rebellion.
* "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" (Romans 9:15) means mercy is never owed. It does not mean God secretly wants some people to perish. First Timothy 2:3–4 makes His desire plain: He wants all people saved.
* The potter image from Jeremiah 18 is about God's sovereign right to determine how and when He works — and His patience with those who have resisted Him is itself an act of mercy.

**Gospel Connection:** Romans 9 is not a chapter about who is locked out. It is a chapter about a God whose mercy cannot be manipulated, earned, or predicted by human categories — and who extends that mercy freely through Jesus Christ to every person who responds in faith. If you have never personally received that mercy, today is the day. And if you have, someone in your life is still waiting to hear about it.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where have you been tempted to assume that someone in your life is simply "not the type" God would save? How does Romans 9 challenge that assumption?
**2.** How does understanding God's patience with "vessels of wrath" (v.22) change the way you pray for someone who has repeatedly resisted the gospel?
**3.** What would it look like this week to be the hands of the Potter in someone else's life?

**Weekly Challenge:** Write one name on a card — someone at work, in your neighborhood, or in your family who doesn't yet know Christ. Pray for them by name every day this week. Then find one moment to serve them, encourage them, or speak a word of hope. The Potter is still working. Make yourself available.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that Your mercy is not something I earned or deserved — it is a gift You freely gave. Give me a heart like Paul's: genuinely burdened for people I love who don't yet know You. Show me who to pray for, who to serve, and what to say. Use me as Your hands this week. In Jesus' name, Amen."
