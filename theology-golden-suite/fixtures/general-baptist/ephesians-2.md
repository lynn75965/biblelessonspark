## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** General Baptist — Arminian, evangelistic, grace-centered, Scripture-grounded

**Lesson Title:** *Dead No More: Grace, Faith, and the Life God Gives*

**Lesson Summary:** Ephesians 2:1-10 presents one of Scripture's most complete portraits of salvation — from spiritual death to new life in Christ. Paul traces the human condition, God's merciful intervention, and the purpose for which believers are recreated. This lesson examines what it means to be made alive by grace through faith, and how that transformation shapes daily living.

**Main Theme:** Salvation is entirely God's work of grace, received through faith, and directed toward a life of purpose.

**Key Takeaway:** Because God made you alive in Christ when you were spiritually dead, your life now has a direction and a design that only grace could produce.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36-50) often carry significant spiritual history — some have walked with Christ for decades, others carry regret over wasted years. This passage speaks powerfully to both. The "before and after" structure of Ephesians 2 resonates with adults who can clearly identify their own "before." The emphasis on God's purpose for their lives addresses the meaning-seeking that characterizes this life stage.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Structural Context**

Ephesians 2:1-10 is a single extended sentence in the original Greek (vv. 1-7), which Paul uses to carry the reader from death to life without pause — as if the momentum of grace itself resists interruption. Paul writes to believers in Ephesus, a cosmopolitan city where multiple religious systems competed for allegiance. His purpose in chapters 1-3 is doxological and doctrinal: to establish who believers are *in Christ* before addressing how they should live (chapters 4-6). The "you" of verse 1 is personal and direct.

**Historical and Audience Context**

The Ephesian believers were predominantly Gentile (see 2:11-12). Paul's description of their former life — following "the course of this world" and "the prince of the power of the air" — would have resonated with people who had literally worshiped pagan deities. Their "before" was not merely moral failure but active participation in spiritual systems opposed to God.

**Key Greek Word Insights**

- *Nekros* (dead, v. 1): This is physical-death vocabulary applied to spiritual reality. Paul is not describing people who are weakened or wounded — they are dead. They cannot initiate their own spiritual recovery.
- *Charis* (grace, vv. 5, 8): Unmerited favor — God's disposition to act generously toward those who have no claim on His goodness. Paul uses *charis* repeatedly in Ephesians (1:6, 7; 2:5, 7, 8) as the controlling theme.
- *Pistis* (faith, v. 8): Trust, reliance, confident dependence. Faith is not a meritorious work — Paul explicitly guards against this in verse 9. Faith is the open hand that receives what grace offers.
- *Poiema* (workmanship, v. 10): From this word comes the English "poem." Believers are God's *poiema* — His crafted work, His masterpiece. The word implies intentionality, artistry, and purpose.

**Doctrinal Framing — General Baptist Perspective**

The passage affirms that human beings in their natural state cannot save themselves (*nekros* confirms this), yet God's prevenient grace — His reaching toward all people — makes genuine response possible. Salvation is entirely God's initiative (vv. 4-5: "But God... made us alive"), yet it is received through faith, not imposed. This preserves both God's sovereignty in providing salvation and human responsibility in receiving it. Verse 8 is precise: "by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God." The gift is the salvation itself — the entire package of grace — not faith as a separate meritorious contribution.

Verse 10 is theologically crucial and often underemphasized. Believers are not saved *by* good works (v. 9) but *for* good works (v. 10). These works were "prepared beforehand" — God has a specific design for each believer's life. This is the purpose-dimension of salvation, directly relevant to mid-life adults asking "What does God want from my life now?"

**Cross-References**

- Romans 6:23 frames the same death-to-life movement
- John 11 (Lazarus) provides a narrative picture of spiritual resurrection — the dead man could not call himself out of the tomb; Christ called him
- Titus 3:5 reinforces that salvation is "not on the basis of deeds which we have done in righteousness"
- 2 Corinthians 5:17 connects the "new creation" language to Paul's broader theology of transformation

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — "Picture This" Scenario (5 minutes)**

Open with this prompt: "Imagine you're hiking and you come across someone collapsed on the trail — completely unresponsive. What can that person do to help themselves? Nothing. They need someone else to act first."

Ask students: "What does it feel like to be the one who can't help themselves? Have you ever been in a situation — physically, emotionally, spiritually — where you simply had nothing left to give?"

Allow two or three brief responses, then say: "Paul opens Ephesians 2 with exactly that picture — not someone struggling, not someone trying hard and falling short, but someone who is spiritually *dead*. And then he shows us what God does about it."

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Discussion (3 minutes)**

Ask: "Before you came to faith in Christ, how would you have described your spiritual condition — even if you didn't have the vocabulary for it at the time?"

This surfaces personal "before" stories and prepares hearts for the text.

**Transition:** "Let's read what Paul says about that 'before' — and the extraordinary 'but God' that changes everything."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Ephesians 2, beginning at verse 1. We're going to read the whole passage first, and then walk through it together.

> "And you were dead in your trespasses and sins, in which you formerly walked according to the course of this world, according to the prince of the power of the air, of the spirit that is now working in the sons of disobedience. Among them we too all formerly lived in the lusts of our flesh, indulging the desires of the flesh and of the mind, and were by nature children of wrath, even as the rest. But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us, even when we were dead in our transgressions, made us alive together with Christ — by grace you have been saved — and raised us up with Him, and seated us with Him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus, so that in the ages to come He might show the surpassing riches of His grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus. For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast. For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."
> — Ephesians 2:1-10 (NASB)

Now, notice the structure. Verses 1-3 are the "before." Verses 4-10 are the "after." And the hinge between them is two of the most important words in the Bible: *But God.*

**The "Before" — Verses 1-3**

Paul doesn't soften this. He says you were *dead* — not sick, not wounded, not spiritually confused. Dead. The Greek word is *nekros*, and it's the same word used for a corpse. Dead people don't reach for help. They don't make better choices. They don't turn over a new leaf. They need someone to act on their behalf.

And Paul layers on what that death looked like in practice. You walked according to the course of this world — meaning the values, assumptions, and priorities of a world that has pushed God to the margins. You followed "the prince of the power of the air" — that's Paul's description of Satan's influence over a culture that operates without God. And you lived in the desires of your flesh and mind. Notice he includes the *mind* — this isn't just about physical sin. Spiritual death affects how we think, what we value, what we chase.

Here's what this means practically: no one drifts into salvation. No one arrives at God by following their natural instincts. The natural trajectory of a life apart from God leads away from Him, not toward Him. This is why God's initiative matters so much.

**The Hinge — "But God"**

Verse 4 is one of the great pivots in all of Scripture. After this unflinching portrait of spiritual death, Paul writes: "But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love..."

God acts — not because we earned it, not because we were searching, not because we showed potential. He acts because He is rich in mercy and great in love. This is the heart of prevenient grace — God moves toward us before we move toward Him. He doesn't wait for us to clean ourselves up. He makes us alive while we are still dead.

Think about Lazarus in John 11. Jesus stands at the tomb. Lazarus has been dead four days. Does Jesus wait for Lazarus to show signs of life before calling him out? No — He calls, and life follows the call. That's the picture Paul is painting here. God's grace precedes our response. It makes our response *possible*.

**Grace, Faith, and the Gift — Verses 8-9**

Verse 8 is one of the most carefully constructed statements in Paul's letters: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast."

Let's unpack this. Grace is the *source* — God's unmerited favor, His decision to act generously toward people who have no claim on His goodness. Faith is the *means* — not a work we perform, but the open hand that receives what grace offers. And then Paul adds a safeguard: "not of yourselves, it is the gift of God." The gift is salvation itself — the whole package. Paul is making sure no one reads this and thinks, "Well, at least my faith was my contribution." No. Even our capacity to respond comes from God's grace reaching toward us.

This is why salvation produces humility, not pride. You didn't out-think, out-work, or out-perform your way to God. He came to you.

**The Purpose — Verse 10**

Here's where many people stop reading too soon. Verse 10 is not a footnote — it's the destination. "For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

The word *workmanship* in Greek is *poiema* — the root of our English word "poem." You are God's crafted work. Not an accident. Not a rough draft. A *poiema* — something made with intention, artistry, and purpose.

And here's the stunning claim: the good works you are called to were *prepared beforehand*. God didn't save you and then wonder what to do with you. He had a design in place. Your devotional life, your service, your faithfulness in ordinary days — these are not extras. They are the very reason you were recreated in Christ.

For those of us in this season of life, this matters enormously. You may wonder if your best years are behind you, or whether the choices of your past have disqualified you from something meaningful. Paul says no. You are a *poiema*. God prepared works for you to walk in — present tense, active, ongoing.

Here's your challenge this week: spend time in prayer asking God specifically, "What are the works You prepared for me to walk in right now?" Not someday. Right now. The God who made you alive in Christ has a path for your feet today.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — "But God" Timeline (Whole-Group Interactive, 10 minutes)**

**Materials:** Whiteboard or large paper, marker

**Instructions:**
Draw a simple horizontal line on the board. Label the left end "Dead in sin" and the right end "Created for good works." Mark a bold dividing point in the middle and write "BUT GOD" there.

Ask the whole group: "What belongs on the left side — what does Paul say characterized life before Christ?" Take responses and write them above the line (following the world, fleshly desires, children of wrath). Then ask: "What belongs on the right side — what does God's intervention produce?" Write those responses (made alive, raised up, seated with Christ, *poiema*, prepared works).

Then point to "BUT GOD" and ask: "What does this pivot cost us? What does it cost God?" Let responses surface the grace-not-works distinction from verse 9.

**Adaptation:** Smaller classes can do this as a personal worksheet; larger groups can call out answers together.

**Activity 2 — *Poiema* Reflection (Personal, 3 minutes)**

**Materials:** Index cards or paper scraps, pens

**Instructions:**

Cards are private — no sharing required. Encourage students to keep the card in their Bible this week as a prayer prompt, connecting to the devotional application from verse 10.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses the word *nekros* — a corpse — to describe spiritual condition before Christ. What three specific characteristics does he list in verses 1-3 that describe what that death looked like in daily life?

**2.** **Understanding:** The lesson identified "But God" in verse 4 as a pivot point. What does Paul say motivated God to act — and what does that tell us about the basis of salvation?

**3.** **Analysis:** Verse 8 says salvation is "by grace through faith" and then adds "not of yourselves, it is the gift of God." Why does Paul include that clarification? What misunderstanding is he preventing?

**4.** **Application:** The Greek word *poiema* — translated "workmanship" — carries the idea of a crafted masterpiece. How does understanding yourself as God's *poiema* change the way you approach your daily devotional life and ordinary responsibilities?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Paul says the good works were "prepared beforehand." What difference does it make to know that God designed a purpose for your life before you began walking in it?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can distinguish grace as the *source* from faith as the *means* — and who understand that verse 9's "not of works" guards against pride while verse 10's good works flow *from* salvation rather than contributing to it. Students who conflate earning salvation with living it out need gentle redirection to the passage's structure.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students grasping the lesson will speak about their daily lives with a sense of divine intentionality rather than accident or regret. Watch for language that connects personal purpose to God's design rather than personal achievement or past failure.

**Closing Reflection:** "If God prepared specific works for you to walk in before you ever took a step toward Him, what would it look like to actually ask Him what those works are — and then go looking for them this week?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Dead No More: Grace, Faith, and the Life God Gives**

**Key Idea:** You were spiritually dead — unable to save yourself — and God made you alive in Christ by grace through faith. That salvation has a purpose: a life of good works He designed specifically for you.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8-9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Before Christ, Paul describes every person as spiritually *dead* — not struggling, not searching, but dead. We could not initiate our own rescue.
* "But God" — two words that change everything. God acted out of mercy and love, not because we earned it.
* Grace is the *source*, faith is the *means*. Salvation is entirely God's gift — received, never achieved.
* You are God's *poiema* — His crafted masterpiece. The word means something made with intention and artistry, like a poem.
* The good works God calls you to were *prepared beforehand*. Your life in Christ has a design, not just a direction.

**Gospel Connection:** Ephesians 2 is the gospel in miniature. Every person begins in the same condition — dead in sin, following a world that has pushed God aside. But God, who is rich in mercy, sent Christ to do what we could never do for ourselves. His death and resurrection are the ground of our being "made alive together with Christ." Salvation is not self-improvement — it is resurrection. And the same God who raised Jesus from the dead has raised you to walk in a life He personally designed.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** When you think about your own "before Christ" story, what does Paul's word *dead* — not sick, not confused, but dead — clarify about what God actually rescued you from?
**2.** If faith is the open hand that receives grace rather than a contribution you make, how does that change the way you think about your standing before God today?
**3.** What is one specific area of your life right now where you sense God may have prepared a good work for you to walk in?

**Weekly Challenge:** Set aside fifteen minutes this week — away from noise and distraction — and pray through this question: *"Father, what are the works You prepared for me to walk in right now?"* Write down whatever comes to mind. Bring it back next week.

**Prayer:** "Lord, I confess that I could not save myself. Thank You for the mercy that reached me when I was dead, and for the grace that made me alive in Christ. Help me to live this week as Your *poiema* — Your crafted work — walking in the purposes You prepared for me. Show me the good works You designed for my life, and give me the courage and faithfulness to walk in them. Amen."
