**Lesson Summary:**
Romans 9:1–24 is one of Scripture's most debated passages, yet its core message aligns powerfully with the Missionary Baptist conviction that God's mercy is real, His word has not failed, and salvation is genuinely offered to all who respond. This lesson examines Paul's anguish for the lost, God's sovereign mercy, and the consistent biblical pattern that divine purpose and human response are not opposites. Students will leave with a clearer understanding of why God's grace is both trustworthy and personally urgent.

God's mercy operates through His faithful purposes, yet every person bears genuine responsibility to respond to the gospel.

**Key Takeaway:**
God's mercy is not arbitrary — it flows from His character and calls every person to a genuine, personal response.

Mid-life adults (36–50) have often wrestled with hard theological questions, experienced personal loss, and watched people they love reject the gospel. This passage meets them where they are — in the tension between trusting God's purposes and feeling the weight of souls around them.

## Section 3: Theological Background

**Literary and Structural Context**

Romans 9–11 functions as a unified theological unit addressing one pressing question: Has God's word to Israel failed? Paul has just declared the unshakeable security of believers in Christ (Romans 8:31–39), which raises an immediate problem — if God's promises are that certain, why has Israel largely rejected her Messiah? Romans 9 is Paul's first answer: God's word has not failed because God's purpose was never based on ethnic descent alone.

The passage moves in three movements: Paul's personal grief (vv. 1–5), God's faithful purposes in Israel's history (vv. 6–13), and the defense of God's justice in showing mercy (vv. 14–24).

**Historical Context**

Paul writes to a mixed Jewish-Gentile congregation in Rome, approximately A.D. 57. Jewish Christians may have felt displaced by growing Gentile membership; Gentile believers may have dismissed Israel's covenant history as irrelevant. Paul refuses both errors. He insists Israel's story is God's story — and that story is not finished.

**Key Word Insights**

- *Anathema* (v. 3, Greek: ἀνάθεμα): Paul wishes himself "accursed" — cut off from Christ — for his kinsmen's sake. This is not hyperbole; it mirrors Moses' similar intercession in Exodus 32:32. It reveals the posture of a true missionary heart: anguish for the lost that transcends personal comfort.
- *Ekklētos* / "called" (v. 11): The word carries the sense of divine summons and purpose, not mechanical predetermination. God's calling in this context concerns His redemptive purposes through chosen instruments, not the eternal fate of individuals predetermined without reference to faith.
- *Mercy* (vv. 15–16, Greek: *eleeō*): God's mercy is His free, unearned compassion. Paul quotes Exodus 33:19 to establish that mercy originates in God's character alone — not in human lineage, effort, or worthiness.

**Doctrinal Framing — Missionary Baptist Perspective**

The passage is often read through a lens of individual predestination, but Paul's primary argument concerns *corporate* and *historical* purposes — why God chose Isaac over Ishmael, Jacob over Esau, and why He hardened Pharaoh. These choices concern God's redemptive plan through history, not a decree determining every individual's eternal destiny apart from faith.

Verse 16 — "So then it does not depend on the man who wills or the man who runs, but on God who has mercy" — does not eliminate human response; it eliminates human *merit* as the basis of salvation. Salvation is not earned by effort ("the man who runs") or by will power alone. It is received by faith from a God who freely extends mercy.

The potter and clay image (vv. 20–24) affirms God's absolute right to work His purposes through history. Importantly, Paul's point is not that God arbitrarily creates individuals for damnation — the "vessels of wrath" are those who have already positioned themselves through unbelief. God's patience with them (v. 22, "endured with much patience") demonstrates His desire for repentance, consistent with 2 Peter 3:9.

**Theological Connections**

This passage connects directly to Romans 10:13 — "Whoever will call on the name of the Lord will be saved." Paul's argument in chapters 9–11 is not a closed system of determinism but a defense of God's faithfulness that opens the door wide to all people. The missionary implication is clear: because God's word has not failed, the gospel can be trusted and must be proclaimed.

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content

**Opening the Passage**

Turn with me to Romans 9, beginning at verse 1. Paul has just finished one of the most triumphant passages in all of Scripture — Romans 8 ends with the declaration that nothing can separate us from the love of God. You'd expect chapter 9 to keep that momentum going. Instead, Paul stops and says something that sounds almost shocking.

He writes: "I am telling the truth in Christ, I am not lying, my conscience testifies with me in the Holy Spirit, that I have great sorrow and unceasing grief in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were accursed, separated from Christ for the sake of my brethren, my kinsmen according to the flesh" (Romans 9:1–3, NASB).

Paul is saying he would be willing to be cut off from Christ — eternally lost — if it would mean Israel's salvation. That is not rhetorical exaggeration. That is a missionary heart fully exposed. Moses said something similar in Exodus 32. This is what happens when the love of God genuinely takes root in a person — it produces anguish for those who are perishing.

Here's the question this raises for us: Do we feel that? Not guilt, not obligation — genuine grief? Because Paul's burden is the starting point of everything else he's about to say.

**The Real Question Paul Is Answering**

Now, here's what most people miss about Romans 9. They read it as if Paul is sitting down to write a systematic theology of predestination. He's not. He's answering a specific, urgent question: Has God's word to Israel failed?

If Jesus is the Messiah, and Israel largely rejected Him, does that mean God's covenant promises collapsed? Paul's answer is no — and the rest of the chapter explains why.

Verse 6: "But it is not as though the word of God has failed. For they are not all Israel who are descended from Israel." Paul's point is that God's covenant purposes were never based on ethnic descent alone. Even within Israel's own history, God worked through chosen individuals — Isaac, not Ishmael; Jacob, not Esau — to advance His redemptive plan.

This is critical: when Paul says "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" (v. 13, quoting Malachi 1:2–3), he is not describing God's attitude toward two individual souls before birth. He is describing the historical purposes God assigned to two nations. The context in Malachi is national, not individual. Paul is making a point about God's freedom to direct history according to His purposes — not declaring that individuals are predestined to hell without any choice.

**Mercy Is Not Arbitrary — It's Rooted in Character**

Verse 15 is the theological anchor of this passage. Paul quotes God's words to Moses in Exodus 33: "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." Then he draws the conclusion in verse 16: "So then it does not depend on the man who wills or the man who runs, but on God who has mercy."

What does that mean? It means salvation cannot be earned. It cannot be achieved by human effort — "the man who runs" — or manufactured by sheer willpower. Mercy originates in God's character, not in anything we bring to the table.

But here's what this does NOT mean: it does not mean God randomly selects some people for salvation and leaves everyone else without a genuine opportunity. The very next chapter — Romans 10:13 — says, "Whoever will call on the name of the Lord will be saved." Paul wrote both sentences. They are not in conflict. The point of verse 16 is not that human response is irrelevant — it's that human merit is eliminated. You don't earn mercy. You receive it.

Think about what that means for stewardship. If you are a believer today, you are living proof that God's mercy reaches people. That mercy was extended to you — not because you were more deserving, more spiritual, or more ready than anyone else. It came because God is merciful. That reality should produce two things: deep humility and urgent generosity with the gospel.

**The Potter and the Clay**

Paul closes this section with the potter and clay image in verses 20–24. Some people read this and hear: "God makes some people for heaven and some for hell, and nobody has any say." But look more carefully.

Paul says God "endured with much patience vessels of wrath prepared for destruction" (v. 22). That phrase "endured with much patience" is doing enormous work. A God who arbitrarily destines people for destruction doesn't endure patiently with them. Patience implies opportunity. It implies that God is waiting, not forcing.

The potter image is about God's sovereign right to work His purposes through history — to use Israel's unbelief to open the door of mercy to the Gentiles, to accomplish His plan through means that don't require our approval or understanding. It is a call to humility before God's wisdom, not a declaration that human choices are meaningless.

**The Conversation This Passage Demands**

Here's the closing challenge. Paul starts this chapter with anguish over people he loves who are separated from God. He ends it with a declaration that God's mercy is available to vessels prepared for glory — "even us, whom He also called, not from among Jews only, but also from among Gentiles" (v. 24).

You are in that "us." So is every person in your life who hasn't yet responded to the gospel. The question this passage leaves us with is not "Has God already decided?" The question is: "Are you carrying the burden Paul carried — and are you doing something about it?"

Think of one person whose name came to your mind at the start of class. This week, have a conversation with them. Not a lecture. A conversation. Ask how they're doing. Let them know you care. That's where Romans 9 lands — not in theological debate, but in a missionary heart that refuses to stay silent.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "The Burden Inventory" — Whole-Group Interactive**
*(8–10 minutes | No materials needed)*

After teaching through Paul's anguish in verses 1–3, pause the group and say: *"Paul named his burden. Let's name ours."*

Ask each person to silently write one name — someone whose spiritual condition genuinely weighs on them. Then ask three volunteers to share *why* that person matters to them, without naming them. After each share, ask the group: *"Does Paul's heart in Romans 9:1–3 sound like this?"*

Close by reading verse 3 aloud together and asking: *"What would it take for our burden to match Paul's?"*

*Adaptation for small groups (under 8):* Have everyone share briefly. *For large groups:* Keep to three volunteers; others reflect silently.

---

**Activity 2: "Merit vs. Mercy" — Whole-Group Sorting**
*(5–7 minutes | Whiteboard or large paper optional)*

Write two columns: **MERIT** and **MERCY**. Read these phrases aloud one at a time and ask the group to call out which column each belongs in:

- "I prayed more than most people"
- "God is compassionate by nature"
- "I grew up in church"
- "Whoever calls on His name will be saved"
- "I cleaned up my life first"
- "It doesn't depend on the man who runs" (Romans 9:16)

Debrief: *"Romans 9:16 wipes out the left column entirely. What does that do to your sense of why you're saved?"*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** What specific question is Paul answering in Romans 9, and why does that question matter for understanding the whole chapter?

**2.** **Understanding:** Paul says he could wish himself "accursed" for Israel's sake. What does that tell us about the difference between genuine missionary burden and routine religious obligation?

**3.** **Analysis:** When Paul quotes "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated," he is referencing Malachi 1:2–3. How does understanding that context as national rather than individual change how you read Romans 9?

**4.** **Application:** Romans 9:16 says salvation doesn't depend on "the man who wills or the man who runs." How does eliminating human merit — while keeping genuine human response — shape the way you talk about the gospel with someone who thinks they're "not good enough" to be saved?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closed with a challenge to have a conversation — not a lecture — with one person who came to mind. What is the single biggest thing that keeps you from having that conversation?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who understand that Paul's argument is primarily about God's faithfulness to His covenant purposes through history, not a closed system of individual predestination. Strong responses will distinguish between God's sovereign direction of history and fatalistic determinism, and will connect Romans 9:16 to mercy rather than to the elimination of human response.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged this passage will show a combination of humility ("I didn't earn this") and urgency ("someone I love hasn't received it yet"). Watch for those who move from theological curiosity to personal weight — that shift reflects Paul's own movement in the passage.

**Closing Reflection:** *"If God's word has not failed — if His mercy is real and His purposes are faithful — what is the cost of staying silent about the gospel with the person whose name you wrote down today?"*

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Whosoever Will: God's Mercy, Human Responsibility, and the Heart of the Gospel**

**Key Idea:** God's mercy is not arbitrary — it flows from His character and calls every person to a genuine, personal response. His word has not failed, and the gospel can be fully trusted.

**Memory Verse:** "So then it does not depend on the man who wills or the man who runs, but on God who has mercy." — Romans 9:16 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Paul opens Romans 9 not with doctrine but with anguish — he would give up everything for the salvation of those he loves. That is the posture this passage calls us toward.
* God's word to Israel has not failed. His covenant purposes were never based on ethnic descent alone — they were always advancing His redemptive plan through history.
* Verse 16 eliminates human *merit*, not human *response*. You cannot earn mercy. But "whoever will call on the name of the Lord will be saved" (Romans 10:13) — the door is genuinely open.
* The potter and clay image is about God's sovereign right to direct history wisely — not a declaration that individuals are destined for destruction without genuine opportunity. God "endured with much patience" those vessels, which means He waited.
* You are in the "us" of verse 24 — called by mercy, not by merit. That changes everything about how you hold the gospel.

**Gospel Connection:** Romans 9 is not a barrier to the gospel — it is the foundation beneath it. Because God's purposes are faithful and His mercy is rooted in His own character, the gospel you carry is not fragile. It will not fail. Every person you know who has not yet responded is still within reach of the same mercy that reached you. The cross was sufficient for all of them.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** When you think about someone whose spiritual condition burdens you, does your concern look more like Paul's anguish or like routine obligation? What's the difference?
**2.** How does knowing you were saved by mercy — not merit — change the way you approach someone who thinks they are too far gone?
**3.** What one honest conversation could you initiate this week with someone who needs to hear that the door is still open?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, reach out to the person whose name came to mind during class. Don't plan a sermon — plan a conversation. Ask how they're doing. Invest in the relationship. Let your burden for them become visible in a way they can receive. Stewardship of the gospel begins with showing up.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that Your word has not failed and Your mercy never runs out. Give me the burden Paul carried — not guilt, but genuine love for those around me who haven't yet responded. Remind me that I am living proof of Your mercy, and use that truth to make me generous with the gospel. Show me who to reach, and give me the courage to start the conversation. In Jesus' name, Amen."
