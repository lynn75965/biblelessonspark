## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Primitive Baptist — Sovereign Grace, Predestinarian, Old School

**Lesson Title:** *Dead, Raised, and Seated: The Sovereign Grace of God in Salvation*

**Lesson Summary:** Ephesians 2:1-10 presents the full arc of sovereign grace — from the utter ruin of total depravity to the glorious reality of regeneration, justification, and God's eternal purpose in His elect. This lesson examines what it means to be spiritually dead, what God alone accomplishes in raising sinners to new life, and why salvation is entirely His work from beginning to end. Students will see that grace is not merely God's assistance to willing sinners, but His sovereign, effectual act upon those who were dead in trespasses.

**Main Theme:** Salvation belongs entirely to God — from election to regeneration to the good works He prepared beforehand.

**Key Takeaway:** Because you were dead and God made you alive, every part of your salvation is a gift of sovereign grace, not a product of human effort or merit.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults in this age range often carry the weight of past failures and present doubts. The absolute security and sovereign love declared in this passage speaks directly to that weariness with fresh, stabilizing power.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Paul wrote Ephesians from prison, likely Rome, around A.D. 60-62. The letter is not crisis-driven like Galatians but is a sustained, doxological exposition of God's eternal purpose in Christ. Ephesians 2:1-10 sits immediately after the towering prayer of 1:15-23, where Paul asks that believers would know "the surpassing greatness of His power toward us who believe" — the same power that raised Christ from the dead. Chapter 2 then demonstrates that power by narrating what God did for sinners who were utterly incapable of responding.

The passage divides cleanly into three movements: the condition of humanity apart from grace (vv. 1-3), the divine intervention in sovereign mercy (vv. 4-7), and the purpose and result of that intervention (vv. 8-10).

**Key Greek Word Insights**

*Nekros* (dead, v. 1) — This is physical-death language applied to spiritual condition. Paul does not say sinners are sick, weakened, or disadvantaged. They are dead. A corpse does not cooperate with its resurrection; it receives it. This single word dismantles every system that requires human initiation in salvation.

*Synezōopoiēsen* (made alive together, v. 5) — A compound verb Paul uses only here and in Colossians 2:13. God is the sole grammatical subject. The elect were made alive *with* Christ — their quickening is bound to His resurrection. This is not a response to faith; it is the cause of it.

*Poiēma* (workmanship, v. 10) — The Greek word from which we derive "poem." Believers are God's crafted masterwork, His artistic creation. The good works they walk in were prepared beforehand (*proetoimasen*) — a word denoting prior arrangement, not reactive planning.

**Doctrinal Framing — Primitive Baptist Perspective**

Total depravity is not merely moral corruption but spiritual death. The unregenerate person does not lack sufficient information or motivation — they lack life itself. Regeneration, therefore, cannot be produced by hearing a sermon, making a decision, or responding to an invitation. God the Holy Spirit acts directly, sovereignly, and effectually upon the elect, imparting spiritual life apart from human means. Faith and repentance are the first fruits of that life, not its preconditions.

Verse 8 — "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God" — has historically been understood within Primitive Baptist theology as identifying the entire salvation event, including the faith itself, as God's gift. This is consistent with Philippians 1:29, which states that faith is *granted* to believers.

Unconditional election is the foundation beneath this passage. The mercy of verse 4 ("But God, being rich in mercy") is not triggered by anything foreseen in the sinner. It flows from God's own character and His eternal purpose. Particular redemption underlies verse 5 — those made alive together with Christ are those for whom Christ's resurrection is effectually applied.

**Cross-References**

- John 5:25 — The dead hear and live; the hearing is the result of life given, not its cause
- Romans 8:29-30 — Foreknown, predestined, called, justified, glorified: an unbroken chain
- Colossians 2:13 — "When you were dead... He made you alive together with Him"
- Titus 3:5 — Saved "not on the basis of deeds... but according to His mercy"

**Age-Appropriate Depth Note**

Mid-life adults often wrestle with whether their faith is genuine or whether past sin disqualifies them. This passage answers both: genuine faith is evidence of God's prior work, and God's prior work cannot be undone. Anchor the teaching there.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Opening Hook — Relevant Quotation (5 minutes)**

Open with this statement from the English Puritan John Owen, read slowly and without attribution until after discussion: *"A man may be a partaker of the divine nature who never heard of the doctrine of the divine nature."*

Ask the class: "What do you think that means? Can someone receive something from God before they understand it theologically?" Let two or three responses surface, then reveal the source and connect it to today's passage: Paul is about to describe something God did *to* people who were dead — people incapable of understanding, choosing, or responding.

**Warm-Up Discussion Prompt (3-4 minutes)**

Ask: "Think about a time you received something you had absolutely no part in earning — not a reward, not a result of effort, just a pure gift. How did that feel different from something you worked for?"

Allow brief sharing, then say: "Ephesians 2 is about the greatest gift ever given — to people who weren't even alive to receive it. Let's look at what God says about what we were, what He did, and why."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Turn in your Bibles to Ephesians 2, beginning at verse 1. We're going to read the whole passage together before we unpack it, because Paul builds an argument here that you need to see as a whole before examining the parts.

> "And you were dead in your trespasses and sins, in which you formerly walked according to the course of this world, according to the prince of the power of the air, of the spirit that is now working in the sons of disobedience. Among them we too all formerly lived in the lusts of our flesh, indulging the desires of the flesh and of the mind, and were by nature children of wrath, even as the rest. But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us, even when we were dead in our transgressions, made us alive together with Christ — by grace you have been saved — and raised us up with Him, and seated us with Him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus, so that in the ages to come He might show the surpassing riches of His grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus. For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast. For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."
> — Ephesians 2:1-10 (NASB)

Now. Let's not move past verse 1 too quickly. Paul says you *were dead*. Not struggling. Not confused. Not spiritually ill. Dead. In the ancient world, everyone understood what a corpse could and could not do. A dead man cannot hear an argument and be persuaded. He cannot feel conviction and respond. He cannot choose life because he has no capacity to choose anything. Paul uses the strongest possible language to describe the human condition apart from God's intervention — and he does it on purpose, because the solution has to match the problem.

Here's why this matters practically: if the problem is that we're sick, then maybe the gospel is medicine we choose to take. If the problem is that we're confused, then maybe the gospel is information we decide to accept. But if the problem is that we're *dead*, then the only solution is resurrection — and dead men don't raise themselves.

This is the foundation of what Primitive Baptists have always confessed: regeneration is entirely God's act. The Holy Spirit does not assist a willing sinner; He gives life to a dead one. Faith is not what opens the door to God's grace — faith is the first breath of a soul God has already made alive.

Look at verse 4. Two of the most glorious words in all of Scripture: "But God." Everything before verse 4 is the diagnosis. Everything after is the cure. And notice — Paul does not say "but you responded" or "but you believed" or "but you made a decision." He says *But God*, being rich in mercy, because of His great love — *He* made us alive. God is the sole actor. We are the recipients.

Think about the raising of Lazarus in John 11. Jesus stood at the tomb and called, "Lazarus, come forth." Lazarus did not first stir, sense something happening, and then decide to respond. The voice of Christ carried the power of life within it. Lazarus came out because Jesus called him out. John 5:25 says the same thing directly: "Truly, truly, I say to you, an hour is coming and now is, when the dead will hear the voice of the Son of God, and those who hear will live." The hearing and the living happen together — the life is given in the calling.

Now look at verses 5 and 6. God "made us alive together with Christ," "raised us up with Him," and "seated us with Him in the heavenly places." Notice the tense — these are accomplished realities. Not "will seat" but "seated." In God's eternal purpose, the elect are already positioned in Christ. Your standing before God is not determined by the quality of your faith on your best day. It is determined by your union with Christ, which God established before the foundation of the world.

Verses 8 and 9 are where Paul makes the exclusion explicit: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." The gift is not merely the opportunity to be saved. The gift is salvation itself — including the faith through which it is received. Philippians 1:29 confirms this: "it has been granted to you... to believe in Him." Faith is granted. It is not generated from within the natural man; it is given by God to those He has made alive.

This should produce something very specific in us: not pride, not passivity, but profound, stabilizing gratitude. If your salvation rested on the quality of your initial response, you would have reason to doubt it every time you fail. But if your salvation rests on God's sovereign act — His choice, His power, His love — then your failures do not unravel what He has done. The elect persevere not because they are strong, but because God holds them.

Verse 10 brings it home. "We are His workmanship" — His *poiēma*, His crafted poem, His masterpiece. And the good works we walk in were "prepared beforehand." God did not save you and then wonder what to do with you. He had a purpose for you before you drew breath. That purpose includes how you live in your family, how you treat your neighbors, how you serve this congregation, and how your life speaks of grace to everyone around you.

Here is the challenge to carry with you: develop the daily habit of tracing your spiritual life back to its source. When you pray, when you serve, when you endure — ask yourself, "Where did this come from?" The answer is always the same. It came from God, who loved you when you were dead, made you alive in Christ, and prepared a path for you to walk. That is not a doctrine to memorize. That is a reality to live inside of every single day.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "But God" Pivot Exercise — Whole Group (8-10 minutes)**

*Materials needed:* Whiteboard or large paper, marker

Draw a vertical line down the center of the board. Label the left column **"Dead In..."** and the right column **"But God..."**

Ask the class to call out words and phrases from Ephesians 2:1-3 that describe humanity apart from grace — trespasses, sins, disobedience, children of wrath, etc. Fill the left column as they respond.

Then ask: "Now, what does God do in response to every single item on this list?" Fill the right column together using verses 4-6: mercy, love, made alive, raised, seated.

Close by asking: "Notice that nothing on the left side changed before God acted on the right side. What does that tell us about the nature of grace?"

*Adaptation:* Smaller classes can do this as a written individual exercise first, then share aloud.

---

**Activity 2: Poiēma Reflection — Brief Personal Writing (4-5 minutes)**

*Materials needed:* Index cards or paper, pens

Ask each student to write one sentence completing this prompt: *"A good work God prepared beforehand for me to walk in — in my neighborhood, workplace, or community — is..."*

Invite two or three volunteers to share. Affirm that these are not self-generated ambitions but Spirit-directed purposes rooted in verse 10.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses the word *dead* in verse 1 rather than words like "sick" or "weakened." What specific things does spiritual death, as Paul describes it in verses 1-3, tell us a person *cannot* do on their own behalf?

**2.** **Understanding:** The teaching emphasized that the Greek verb *synezōopoiēsen* — "made alive together" — has God as its sole grammatical subject. Why is that grammatical detail theologically significant for how we understand regeneration?

**3.** **Analysis:** Verses 8-9 say salvation is "not of yourselves" and "not as a result of works." How does the Primitive Baptist reading of this passage — that the gift includes faith itself — differ from a reading that says faith is the human contribution and grace is God's contribution?

**4.** **Application:** The lesson connected verse 10's "prepared beforehand" to community outreach and daily life. What does it look like practically to approach your relationships and neighborhood as someone walking in works God already arranged — rather than works you invented?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Mid-life often brings doubt about whether past failures disqualify us. How does the sequence in this passage — dead, made alive, seated in heavenly places — answer that doubt specifically?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can articulate the three-part movement of the passage (death → divine action → divine purpose) and who can distinguish regeneration as God's act from faith as its fruit. Correct any framing that presents faith as the cause of new life rather than its evidence.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students are genuinely processing this passage when they express gratitude rather than pride, when they speak of their good works as God's preparation rather than personal achievement, and when doubts about their standing are answered by pointing to God's act rather than their own consistency.

**Closing Reflection:** "If every part of your salvation — including your faith — is a gift from God given to you while you were spiritually dead, what is the only appropriate response you can bring to Him today?"

## Section 8: Group Handout (Standalone)

**Dead, Raised, and Seated: The Sovereign Grace of God in Salvation**

**Key Idea:** Ephesians 2:1-10 reveals that salvation is entirely God's work — He found us dead, made us alive, and prepared the path we now walk. Nothing in us initiated it; nothing in us can undo it.

**Memory Verse:** "But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us, even when we were dead in our transgressions, made us alive together with Christ — by grace you have been saved." — Ephesians 2:4-5 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Spiritual death means total inability — not weakness, not confusion, but the complete absence of life. Dead people do not choose resurrection.
* "But God" (v. 4) is the hinge of the entire passage. Everything before it is diagnosis; everything after is cure — and God is the only actor.
* Being "made alive," "raised," and "seated" in Christ are accomplished realities, not future possibilities. Your standing before God rests on your union with Christ, not the quality of your best day.
* Faith itself is God's gift (v. 8; Philippians 1:29). It is the first breath of a soul God has already made alive — evidence of regeneration, not its cause.
* You are God's *poiēma* — His crafted masterwork (v. 10). The good works you do in your home, neighborhood, and congregation were arranged by God before you were born.

**Gospel Connection:** The same power that raised Jesus Christ from the dead is the power God exercised when He made you alive in Christ. Your salvation is not a cooperative project between your willingness and God's offer — it is a resurrection. Christ's victory over death is the ground of your new life, and His intercession before the Father is the guarantee of your perseverance. Sovereign grace does not begin a work and leave it unfinished.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your daily life do you still act as though your standing before God depends on your performance rather than on what God accomplished in Christ?
**2.** What "good work" in your community or neighborhood do you sense God has prepared for you to walk in — one you may have been avoiding or overlooking?
**3.** How does knowing that faith is God's gift — not your contribution — change the way you pray for people in your life who do not yet show signs of spiritual life?

**Weekly Challenge:** Each morning this week, before you begin your day, pause and trace your spiritual life back to its source. Pray specifically: "Lord, this faith, this desire to serve, this love for Your people — none of it originated in me. It came from You." Then ask Him to show you one prepared work to walk in that day.

**Prayer:** "Father, we confess that we were dead — not searching, not straining toward You, but dead in our sins. We marvel that You, being rich in mercy, loved us anyway and made us alive in Christ. We did not choose You first; You chose us. We did not generate faith; You granted it. Teach us to live inside this grace — not with pride, not with fear, but with the deep, settled gratitude of those who know they have been raised from the dead. Show us the works You prepared for us and give us grace to walk in them, to Your glory alone. Amen."
