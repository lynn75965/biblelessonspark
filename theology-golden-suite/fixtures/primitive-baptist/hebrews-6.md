## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Primitive Baptist — Sovereign Grace, Predestinarian, Old School

**Lesson Title:** *Pressing On: The Marks of Those Whom God Has Secured*

**Lesson Summary:** Hebrews 6:1–12 calls believers to spiritual maturity while presenting one of Scripture's most debated warning passages. Interpreted through the lens of sovereign grace, this lesson examines what the warnings reveal about spiritual profession versus genuine regeneration, and what the fruit of true election looks like in daily life. Students will see that the exhortation to press forward is not a threat to the elect but a God-ordained means of their perseverance.

**Main Theme:** God's sovereign grace produces genuine spiritual fruit in His elect, and the warnings of Scripture serve as instruments through which He keeps His people pressing toward maturity.

**Key Takeaway:** The elect press on not to secure their salvation, but because God has already secured them — and that security produces diligence, not complacency.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults often wrestle with spiritual plateau and quiet doubts about their standing before God. This passage speaks directly to both the danger of stagnation and the comfort of sovereign assurance.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews was written to Jewish Christians — likely in the late first century — who faced intense pressure to abandon Christ and return to the Levitical system. The author's identity remains debated; tradition has suggested Paul, though Scripture does not state this explicitly. The letter is a sustained argument for Christ's superiority over every institution of the old covenant. Chapter 6 arrives after a sharp rebuke in 5:11–14 for spiritual dullness. The audience had been believers long enough to be teachers but remained spiritually immature — still needing milk rather than solid food.

**The Elementary Teachings (vv. 1–3)**

The "elementary teaching about Christ" (v. 1) refers to foundational doctrines: repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, and eternal judgment. The author does not dismiss these — they are essential. His point is that mature believers must build *upon* this foundation, not perpetually revisit it. The Greek word *teleiótēta* (maturity/perfection) implies completeness and full development. The call is to forward movement, not spiritual stagnation.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4–6) — Sovereign Grace Interpretation**

This is among the most contested passages in all of Scripture. The description — those "who have been enlightened," "tasted of the heavenly gift," "made partakers of the Holy Spirit," "tasted the good word of God" — sounds like genuine regeneration to many readers. Primitive Baptists, holding to sovereign grace, distinguish carefully here.

Several interpretive options exist within the Reformed and Old School Baptist tradition:

1. **Hypothetical warning:** The author presents an impossible scenario — *if* the elect could fall away, restoration would be impossible. The very impossibility underscores the security of the elect.
2. **Descriptive of professors, not possessors:** The language describes those who experienced significant outward spiritual privileges — hearing, tasting, participating in the community of faith — without genuine regeneration. "Tasting" need not mean full reception; Judas "tasted" the ministry of Christ without being regenerate.
3. **Warning as a means of preservation:** God uses these warnings instrumentally to keep His elect from apostasy. The warning is real; the falling away of the truly elect is not.

The phrase "it is impossible to renew them again to repentance" (v. 6) is crucial. If these were genuinely regenerate, their final apostasy would mean God's sovereign work could be undone — which contradicts Romans 8:29–30 and John 10:28–29 explicitly. The warning, therefore, is best understood as either hypothetical or descriptive of those who were never truly born of God.

**Agricultural Imagery (vv. 7–8)**

The land that drinks rain and produces useful vegetation receives God's blessing; land that produces thorns is "worthless" and near a curse. This mirrors Matthew 7:16–20. Genuine regeneration produces fruit; outward profession without life produces only thorns. The rain falls on both — sovereign grace distinguishes the outcome.

**The Marks of Genuine Election (vv. 9–12)**

The author shifts to warm assurance: "we are convinced of better things concerning you" (v. 9). The "better things" include salvation itself. He points to their *work* and *love* shown toward God's people (v. 10) as evidence of genuine spiritual life. The exhortation to "show the same diligence" (v. 11) is not a threat but a pastoral encouragement — God's elect press on because He is pressing them. The goal is full assurance through faith and patience, not anxious striving.

**Doctrinal Connections**

This passage directly engages total depravity (humans cannot produce genuine fruit apart from regeneration), perseverance of the saints (the truly elect will not finally fall), and unconditional election (God's love and calling are not reversed, v. 10). The sovereign grace framework does not eliminate the seriousness of the warning — it clarifies *for whom* it is ultimately intended and *how* God uses it.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — Surprising Fact Hook**
*(3–4 minutes)*

Open by sharing this: Studies of long-distance runners consistently show that the most dangerous point in a race is not the beginning or the finish — it is the middle, where fatigue sets in and the temptation to quit is strongest. Spiritual life follows a similar pattern. Many people begin with visible enthusiasm and finish well — but the middle years can be the most spiritually hazardous. Ask students: "If you're honest, where do you feel you are in your spiritual race right now — early energy, middle-mile fatigue, or finishing strong?"

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Discussion Prompt**
*(2–3 minutes)*

Ask: "What does it look like when someone who once seemed deeply engaged with the things of God simply... drifts away? What do you think was actually happening in their heart?"

Allow two or three brief responses. Do not resolve the question yet — the passage will do that.

**Transition:** "Hebrews 6 speaks directly to both of those questions. Let's look at what God's Word says."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Hebrews chapter 6. We're going to spend our time today in the first twelve verses, and I want to tell you upfront — this is one of the most misunderstood passages in the New Testament. People have used it to argue that a believer can lose their salvation. We're going to see why that reading misses what the author is actually doing, and why the sovereign grace understanding of this text is not only more faithful to Scripture but far more comforting to the soul.

Let's begin with verses 1 through 3. The author lists what he calls "elementary teaching" — repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, eternal judgment. These are not small things. These are the foundations of Christian life. But his point is this: you cannot build a house by laying the foundation over and over again. At some point, you build *on* it. Spiritual maturity means moving forward — deeper into Christ, deeper into His Word, deeper into genuine communion with God. Stagnation is not neutral. A believer who never grows beyond the basics is like a forty-year-old still learning to read. Something is wrong.

Now we come to verses 4 through 6, and this is where people get tangled. The author describes people who were "enlightened," who "tasted of the heavenly gift," who were "made partakers of the Holy Spirit," who "tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come" — and then he says if these people fall away, it is impossible to renew them to repentance.

Here is the question every careful reader must ask: Is this describing genuinely regenerate people? And if so, what does it mean that they can fall away?

Consider this carefully. The word "tasted" does not necessarily mean "fully received." Judas Iscariot walked with Jesus for three years. He heard the Sermon on the Mount. He watched the dead raised. He participated in the ministry of the Son of God. By any outward measure, Judas had experienced extraordinary spiritual privileges — and yet Christ himself said it would have been better for that man if he had never been born. Judas tasted without being transformed.

There is also a second way to read this passage that is entirely consistent with sovereign grace: the author may be presenting a hypothetical scenario. He is essentially saying, *if* it were possible for a truly regenerate person to fall away completely and finally, there would be no recovery — because that would mean crucifying the Son of God again. The very impossibility of the scenario is the point. The elect cannot fall away precisely because God does not abandon His own work.

Think about what Romans 8:29–30 tells us — those whom God foreknew, He predestined; those He predestined, He called; those He called, He justified; those He justified, He glorified. That is a chain with no broken links. God does not begin a work and abandon it. The warning in Hebrews 6 does not break that chain — it actually serves it. God uses these very warnings as instruments to keep His elect pressing forward. The warning is real. The falling away of the truly elect is not.

Now look at verses 7 and 8 — the agricultural picture. Land that receives rain and produces good crops is blessed. Land that receives the same rain and produces only thorns is worthless, near to being cursed. The rain falls on both. The difference is what is *in* the soil. This is a picture of total depravity and regeneration. Outward religious experience — hearing sermons, being part of a congregation, participating in the ordinances — is the rain. But only the ground that God has prepared will produce genuine fruit. You cannot manufacture that from the outside.

Here is where this becomes deeply personal for us as mid-life adults. Many of us have watched people we worshipped alongside simply drift. They were in the congregation. They sang the hymns. They may have even been baptized. And then one day they were gone — not just from the meeting house, but from any visible interest in the things of God. Hebrews 6 is not saying those people lost something they once had. It is saying they may never have possessed what they appeared to have.

But now — and do not miss this — look at verse 9. The author says, "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation." That phrase — "things that accompany salvation" — is the hinge of the whole passage. He has just described people whose end is burning. Now he turns to his readers and says: *I believe better things about you.* Why? Because of what he sees in verse 10: their work, their love, their service to God's people. These are the marks of genuine spiritual life. These are the fruits that Matthew 7 says distinguish the true tree from the false one.

Verse 11 gives the pastoral exhortation: "show the same diligence so as to realize the full assurance of hope until the end." This is not a threat. This is a shepherd urging his flock to keep moving. Full assurance comes through diligence and patience — not through anxious striving to earn what God has already given, but through the consistent, Spirit-produced pursuit of God that marks those He has regenerated.

Verse 12 closes with a call to imitate "those who through faith and patience inherit the promises." This is the life of a believer under sovereign grace — not passive presumption, not frantic self-effort, but patient, faithful, fruit-bearing perseverance that God Himself is working in you.

Here is the challenge I want to leave with you today: Stop treating your spiritual life as something you can put on hold. The middle years of life are exactly when the enemy whispers that you have arrived, or that you are too tired, or that the fire you once felt is just gone. Hebrews 6 says press on. Not to earn what God has secured, but because His securing of you is meant to produce exactly this — a life that keeps moving toward Him, bearing fruit, loving His people, holding fast to hope.

If God has done the work of regeneration in your soul, that work will show itself. The question worth sitting with this week is not "Am I saved?" in an anxious, doubting way — but "What fruit is my life producing, and am I pressing toward the maturity God has called me to?"

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — Individual Written Reflection**
*(8–10 minutes | Materials: paper or index cards, pens)*

Distribute index cards. Ask students to draw a simple line down the center, labeling the left column "Thorns" and the right column "Fruit." Without sharing aloud, each student privately lists patterns in their current devotional life that might fall into each category — habits, attitudes, or spiritual disciplines that are either producing genuine growth or quietly crowding it out.

After five minutes of silent writing, invite students to turn the card over and write one honest answer to this question: *"What would pressing toward maturity look like for me specifically this week?"*

Cards are personal — no collection, no sharing required. The goal is honest self-examination before God, not performance before the class.

*Adaptation for smaller classes:* Invite two or three willing students to share their "one step forward" response only.

---

**Activity 2 — Chain Illustration**
*(3–4 minutes | Materials: none)*

Ask the class: "Romans 8:29–30 describes a chain — foreknew, predestined, called, justified, glorified. Which link, if any, do you think people most commonly assume can break — and why?"

Take two or three brief responses. Then ask: "How does understanding that chain change the way you read Hebrews 6's warning?"

This reinforces the connection between the warning passage and the security of the elect without re-teaching the theology.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** What six "elementary teachings" does Hebrews 6:1–2 list, and what is the author's reason for urging believers to move beyond them rather than continually revisiting them?

**2.** **Understanding:** The lesson presented two interpretive approaches to Hebrews 6:4–6 within the sovereign grace tradition — the hypothetical reading and the "professors, not possessors" reading. In your own words, explain what each approach argues.

**3.** **Analysis:** The agricultural imagery in verses 7–8 pictures rain falling on two kinds of ground. How does this image illustrate the difference between outward religious participation and genuine regeneration? What does the rain represent, and why does it matter that both fields receive it equally?

**4.** **Application:** Verse 9 says the author is "convinced of better things" about his readers — things that "accompany salvation." What specific evidences in your own devotional life would you point to as fruit accompanying genuine spiritual life?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closed with a challenge to stop treating spiritual life as something that can be put on hold. Where in your daily rhythms is spiritual stagnation most tempting, and what one concrete step could you take this week to press forward?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can distinguish between the two interpretive approaches to verses 4–6 without collapsing them into a "losing salvation" reading. Strong responses will connect the warning's purpose to God's means of preserving His elect rather than threatening their security.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who are genuinely engaging will move naturally from theological analysis to personal self-examination — asking honest questions about their own fruit rather than deflecting into abstract debate about the warning passage.

**Closing Reflection:** *"If God's sovereign securing of you is meant to produce diligence rather than complacency, what does your current level of diligence say about how deeply you believe that truth?"*

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Pressing On: The Marks of Those Whom God Has Secured**

**Key Idea:** God's sovereign grace does not produce passive presumption — it produces people who press forward, bear fruit, and hold fast to hope because God Himself is at work within them.

**Memory Verse:** "And we desire that each one of you show the same diligence so as to realize the full assurance of hope until the end." — Hebrews 6:11 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Moving toward spiritual maturity is not optional — stagnation is a warning sign, not a resting place.
* The warning in Hebrews 6:4–6 does not threaten the security of the truly elect; it either describes those who were never genuinely regenerate, or presents an impossible scenario that underscores why the elect cannot finally fall away.
* The agricultural picture (vv. 7–8) reminds us that outward religious experience alone does not produce genuine fruit — only the ground God has prepared will bear it.
* The marks of genuine election are visible: work, love, and service shown toward God's people (v. 10).
* Full assurance grows through diligent, patient, Spirit-produced faithfulness — not anxious striving.

**Gospel Connection:** The good news running beneath Hebrews 6 is that God does not begin His work and abandon it. Those whom He regenerated, He keeps. The same sovereign grace that chose His people before time is the grace that sustains them through every middle-mile season of fatigue and doubt. The warnings of Scripture are not cracks in that foundation — they are instruments God uses to keep His own pressing forward. If you find yourself responding to these words with honest self-examination and a desire to press on, that response itself is evidence of His work in you.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your life are you most tempted toward spiritual stagnation right now?
**2.** What fruit — work, love, service — is currently visible in your devotional life and relationships?
**3.** What is one thing you will give up or begin this week to press toward the maturity Hebrews 6 describes?

**Weekly Challenge:** Identify one specific habit of spiritual stagnation — a practice you have been postponing, a discipline that has gone cold, or a pattern of passivity — and replace it with one intentional step toward growth this week. Write it down. Tell someone you trust. Press on.

**Prayer:** "Lord, we confess that the middle miles of life can grow quiet and cold. Remind us that You have secured what we could never secure for ourselves. Produce in us the fruit that accompanies salvation — diligence, love, patience, and hope. Let our pressing on be evidence of Your pressing upon us. In Christ, Amen."
