## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Primitive Baptist — Sovereign Grace, Experiential Faith

**Lesson Title:** *The Lord Is My Shepherd: Sovereign Grace Through Every Valley*

**Lesson Summary:** Psalm 23 is not a sentimental poem for funeral programs — it is a bold confession of absolute dependence on a sovereign God who shepherds His elect through every season of life and death. This lesson examines David's declaration through the lens of unconditional election and the perseverance of the saints, showing that the sheep do not choose the Shepherd — the Shepherd claims the sheep. Students will discover that the comfort of this psalm flows directly from God's sovereignty, not from human effort or spiritual performance.

**Main Theme:** God's sovereign, personal care for His elect people is the foundation of all peace, provision, and assurance in this life and the next.

**Key Takeaway:** Every promise in Psalm 23 rests on who God is, not on what you have done — and that is precisely why those promises are unbreakable.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults carry real weight — career pressures, aging parents, shifting family dynamics. Psalm 23 meets them not with platitudes but with the bedrock reality of a Shepherd who has never lost a sheep.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Genre and Literary Context**

Psalm 23 is a Davidic psalm of trust — a sub-genre within the Psalter characterized by confident declaration rather than petition. Unlike lament psalms that move toward trust, Psalm 23 begins in trust and deepens it. The structure moves through three images: the shepherd and his flock (vv. 1–3), the traveler through dangerous terrain (v. 4), and the honored guest at a feast (vv. 5–6). These are not disconnected metaphors; they form a unified portrait of God's comprehensive care from daily provision to final destiny.

**Historical and Authorial Context**

David wrote from lived experience as a shepherd himself (1 Samuel 16:11; 17:34–36). His knowledge of sheep — their helplessness, their tendency to wander, their complete dependence on the shepherd for food, water, safety, and direction — was not academic. This experiential foundation makes the psalm's theology incarnate. David knew sheep do not survive by their own cunning; they survive because a capable shepherd is watching over them.

**Key Word Insights**

- *"The LORD"* (v. 1) — This is the covenant name *YHWH*, the self-existent, eternally faithful God. David does not say "a shepherd" but "my Shepherd," a personal possessive that reflects the elect's experiential knowledge of God's particular care.
- *"I shall not want"* — The Hebrew *lo' echsar* means "I shall lack nothing." This is not a promise of material prosperity but of covenantal sufficiency — God supplies what His people genuinely need.
- *"He restores my soul"* (v. 3) — *Yeshobeb* carries the sense of turning back, bringing around. God's restorative work is active and initiatory — He pursues the wandering soul. This aligns directly with the Primitive Baptist understanding that regeneration and renewal are God's sovereign acts, not human achievements.
- *"Paths of righteousness"* — Literally, right paths or straight tracks. God leads in ways consistent with His own character and name, not merely for the sheep's comfort but for His own glory.
- *"The valley of the shadow of death"* (v. 4) — The Hebrew *tsalmaveth* combines darkness and death. This is not hypothetical suffering; it is the deep, disorienting darkness of real affliction. The promise is not that the valley is avoided but that the Shepherd walks through it with His sheep.
- *"Goodness and lovingkindness"* (v. 6) — *Hesed* (lovingkindness/steadfast love) is the covenant loyalty of God toward His own. It is not conditional on the sheep's performance; it flows from God's own covenantal character.

**Doctrinal Connections**

The psalm embodies total depravity implicitly — sheep are helpless without a shepherd. Unconditional election surfaces in the personal possessive "my Shepherd" — this relationship was not initiated by the sheep. Particular redemption echoes in John 10:11, where Jesus lays down His life specifically for the sheep. Irresistible grace appears in "He leads me" and "He restores my soul" — the sheep do not lead themselves to green pastures. Perseverance of the saints is the explicit promise of verse 6: "Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life, and I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever." The word "surely" (*ach*) expresses certainty — this is not a hope that might be disappointed.

**Cross-References**

- Ezekiel 34:11–16 — God as the true Shepherd who seeks His scattered flock
- Isaiah 40:11 — The Shepherd gathers the lambs and carries them
- John 10:27–29 — The sheep hear His voice; no one snatches them from His hand

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Opening Hook — Relevant Quotation (5 minutes)**

"David wrote Psalm 23 not as a theory about God but as a testimony of what God had actually done for him. Let's find out why that matters for us today."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Psalm 23. We're going to read it together, and I want you to hear it as if you've never heard it before — because most of us have heard it so many times that the familiarity has softened its edges. This psalm is not soft. It is bold, it is specific, and it is grounded in a theology that should change how you face every hard thing in your life.

Read Psalm 23:1–6 aloud together.

Now — notice the very first word after "The LORD." It is the word *my*. Not "the LORD is a shepherd." Not "the LORD shepherds those who deserve it." David says, "The LORD is MY shepherd." That possessive is doing enormous theological work. David is not making a general religious statement. He is declaring a personal, covenantal relationship. And here is what Primitive Baptists have always understood that many people miss: sheep do not choose their shepherd. The shepherd claims the sheep. David could say "my Shepherd" because God had first said "my sheep." That relationship did not originate with David's devotion. It originated with God's sovereign, electing love.

So when verse 1 continues, "I shall not want" — understand what David is actually saying. He is not claiming that life will be comfortable or that his bank account will be full. The Hebrew word means "I shall lack nothing." It is a declaration of covenantal sufficiency. God will supply what His sheep genuinely need. Not necessarily what they want in the moment, but what they need for the journey He has ordained for them. That is a very different promise than the prosperity gospel offers, and it is a far more reliable one.

Look at verses 2 and 3. "He makes me lie down in green pastures; He leads me beside quiet waters. He restores my soul; He guides me in the paths of righteousness for His name's sake." Count the subjects of those verbs. He makes. He leads. He restores. He guides. Every active verb belongs to the Shepherd. The sheep's role is to be led, to be restored, to be guided. This is not passivity on the sheep's part — it is dependence. And dependence is exactly the posture God's sovereign grace produces in His people.

"He restores my soul" — that phrase deserves a moment. The Hebrew word carries the sense of turning something back around, bringing it back from where it had wandered. David is not describing a soul that has maintained perfect spiritual consistency. He is describing a soul that has wandered and been brought back — not by its own resolve, but by the Shepherd's active pursuit. If you have ever experienced a season of spiritual coldness and then found yourself drawn back to God with a hunger you didn't manufacture yourself, you have experienced what David is describing. That is the Shepherd restoring a soul.

Now we come to verse 4, and this is where the psalm gets most honest about real life. "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I fear no evil, for You are with me." The valley is real. David does not pretend it isn't. He doesn't say "if I walk through a difficult season" — he says "even though I walk through." This is the language of a man who has faced genuine darkness and is not romanticizing it. What he is saying is that the Shepherd's presence transforms the valley. The valley doesn't disappear; the fear does. And it disappears not because David has mustered enough courage, but because the Shepherd is there.

Think about what it means to a sheep that the shepherd carries a rod and a staff. The rod was for defending against predators — it was a weapon. The staff was for guiding and steadying the sheep on narrow paths. David says these instruments "comfort me." Not because they make the path easier, but because they prove the Shepherd is present and equipped. When you are in a hard season, the question is not "why is this happening?" The question is "is my Shepherd here?" And the answer of Psalm 23 is: He is always there.

Verses 5 and 6 shift the image from shepherd and sheep to host and honored guest. "You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies." This is a picture of God's provision that is not hidden or private — it is public. God honors His people openly, even when enemies are watching. The anointing with oil was a sign of welcome and dignity. The cup that overflows is abundance beyond what was asked for.

And then verse 6 — the capstone of the entire psalm: "Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life, and I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever." That word "surely" in Hebrew is a word of strong affirmation — it means "without question, without exception." This is not a wish. It is not a hope that might be disappointed. It is a declaration rooted in who God is and what He has covenanted to do for His people. Goodness and *hesed* — God's steadfast covenant loyalty — will not occasionally appear. They will *follow* the sheep. They are behind the sheep like a rearguard, ensuring nothing overtakes what God has purposed.

And it ends with "forever." Not "for as long as I stay faithful." Not "for as long as I don't wander too far." Forever. This is the perseverance of the saints not as a theological category but as a lived, experiential certainty. The sheep who belongs to this Shepherd will never ultimately be lost.

Here is the challenge I want to leave with you: make Psalm 23 your daily declaration this week. Not as a comfort ritual, but as a deliberate act of theological remembrance. When you feel the valley closing in, when provision feels uncertain, when enemies seem to have the upper hand — say it out loud: "The LORD is MY Shepherd. I shall not want." Let the theology of the psalm do its work in your soul. That is what David did. And it is what God's people have done in every generation since.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "He Does / Sheep Do" Verb Chart** *(Paired Sharing — 8 minutes)*

*Materials needed:* Paper or index cards for each pair

Have students work in pairs. Ask each pair to read Psalm 23:1–5 together and create two columns on their paper: one labeled "What God Does" and one labeled "What the Sheep Do." They should list every action from the text under the correct column.

After four minutes, bring the class back together. Ask pairs to share what they noticed. Guide the conversation toward this observation: nearly every active verb belongs to God. The sheep are led, restored, guided, comforted. Ask: "What does this distribution of action tell us about the nature of God's care for His people?"

*Adaptation:* For smaller classes, do this as a whole-group exercise on a whiteboard.

---

**Activity 2: Valley Mapping** *(Personal Reflection — 5 minutes)*

*Materials needed:* Paper or journal

Ask each student to quietly write down one "valley" season from their life — a period of genuine darkness, loss, or uncertainty. Then ask them to write one sentence answering: "Looking back, where do you see evidence that the Shepherd was present, even when you didn't feel it?"

Students are not required to share publicly. This prepares hearts for the discussion questions that follow.

*Adaptation:* For larger classes, invite two or three volunteers to share briefly.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** In Psalm 23:1, David uses the possessive "my Shepherd" rather than "a shepherd." According to what we studied, what does that personal possessive reveal about the nature of the relationship between God and His people?

**2.** **Understanding:** Verses 2–3 show God as the subject of every active verb — He makes, He leads, He restores, He guides. How does this pattern challenge the idea that spiritual growth is primarily driven by human effort or discipline?

**3.** **Analysis:** The psalm does not promise the valley will be avoided — it promises the Shepherd walks through it with His sheep. How does this honest portrayal of suffering differ from what our culture typically offers as comfort in hard times?

**4.** **Application:** Verse 6 closes with "surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life." How does the doctrine of perseverance of the saints give this word "surely" its full weight in your daily life, particularly in your family and home relationships?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** David made Psalm 23 a personal declaration, not a general religious statement. What would it look like for you to deliberately speak this psalm as theological remembrance during a difficult week?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students connecting God's active role in the psalm to sovereign grace rather than human performance. Strong answers will distinguish covenantal sufficiency in "I shall not want" from prosperity-gospel thinking, and will recognize "surely" in verse 6 as a declaration of certainty, not a wish.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students demonstrating genuine understanding will speak of God's care in personal terms, reference specific valley experiences with gratitude rather than bitterness, and show comfort rooted in God's character rather than their own faithfulness.

**Closing Reflection:** "If the Shepherd has never lost a single sheep He has claimed, what does that mean for the valley you are walking through right now?"

## Section 8: Group Handout (Standalone)

**The Lord Is My Shepherd: Sovereign Grace Through Every Valley**

**Key Idea:** Every promise in Psalm 23 rests on who God is, not on what you have done — and that is precisely why those promises are unbreakable.

**Memory Verse:** "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want." — Psalm 23:1 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The word "my" in verse 1 is a declaration of personal, covenantal relationship — the sheep did not choose the Shepherd; the Shepherd claimed the sheep through unconditional election.
* "I shall not want" means covenantal sufficiency, not material prosperity — God supplies what His people genuinely need for the journey He has ordained.
* Every active verb in verses 2–3 belongs to God: He makes, He leads, He restores, He guides. Our role is dependence, not self-direction.
* The valley is real — David does not pretend otherwise. The promise is not that darkness is avoided but that the Shepherd walks through it with His sheep.
* Verse 6's "surely" is a word of strong affirmation rooted in God's *hesed* — His steadfast covenant loyalty — not in the sheep's consistency.
* "I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever" is the perseverance of the saints as lived experience, not merely theological category.

**Gospel Connection:** Psalm 23 finds its fullest meaning in Jesus, who declared in John 10:11, "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down His life for the sheep." The Shepherd of Psalm 23 is not a distant caretaker — He is the one who entered the valley of death Himself on behalf of His people, securing forever what David could only declare in faith. Particular redemption means this Shepherd laid down His life specifically and effectually for those the Father gave Him. The sheep are safe not because they hold on tightly, but because He holds them.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your life right now do you most need to hear "the Shepherd is here" rather than "the valley will end soon"?
**2.** How does knowing that God's *hesed* follows you like a rearguard — behind you, ensuring nothing overtakes His purpose — change how you face uncertainty at home or with family?
**3.** What would it mean to speak Psalm 23 as a deliberate act of theological remembrance this week, rather than simply a familiar comfort?

**Weekly Challenge:** Each morning this week, read Psalm 23 aloud before you begin your day. Do not read it as routine — read it as a declaration of what is actually true about your Shepherd. When a moment of fear, scarcity, or darkness comes, return to verse 1 and say it by name: "The LORD is MY Shepherd."

**Prayer:** "Lord, You are my Shepherd, and I confess that I am a sheep — prone to wander, easily frightened, and wholly dependent on Your care. Thank You that my security does not rest on my faithfulness but on Yours. Walk with me through every valley. Lead me in paths that honor Your name. Let Your goodness and steadfast love follow me all the days of my life, and bring me at last to dwell with You forever. Amen."
