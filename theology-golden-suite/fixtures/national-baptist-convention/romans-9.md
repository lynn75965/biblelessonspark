## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** National Baptist Convention (USA) — Scripture-rooted, Christ-centered, evangelistic, emphasizing God's sovereignty in harmony with genuine human responsibility, unlimited atonement, and holistic Christian mission.

**Lesson Title:** *Chosen, Called, and Commissioned: Understanding God's Sovereign Purpose*

**Lesson Summary:** Romans 9:1–24 confronts us with one of Scripture's most searching questions: how do we reconcile God's sovereign purposes with human responsibility and the integrity of His promises? Paul's anguish over Israel becomes a launching point for understanding how God's mercy flows freely through Christ to all who believe. This lesson explores God's freedom to extend grace, His faithfulness to His word, and our responsibility to respond.

**Main Theme:** God's sovereign purposes are not unjust — they are merciful, faithful, and open to all through faith in Christ.

**Key Takeaway:** God's grace is not arbitrary — it flows from His character, calls us to respond in faith, and compels us to carry His mercy to others.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often wrestle with questions of fairness, unanswered prayer, and why some people seem to resist God despite every opportunity. This passage speaks directly to those tensions and rewards careful, honest engagement.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Structural Context**

Romans 9–11 forms a self-contained unit within Paul's letter, addressing a crisis of theological integrity: if Israel, God's covenant people, has largely rejected the Messiah, has God's word failed? Paul's answer across three chapters is an emphatic no. Chapter 9 establishes God's sovereign freedom in mercy; chapter 10 establishes human responsibility in faith; chapter 11 reveals God's ultimate redemptive plan. Teachers should resist reading chapter 9 in isolation — Paul's argument only resolves in chapter 11:32: "For God has shut up all in disobedience so that He may show mercy to all."

**Historical and Pastoral Context**

Paul writes with genuine anguish (vv. 1–3), not detached theology. His willingness to be "accursed" for Israel's sake echoes Moses' intercession in Exodus 32:32. This is not rhetorical flourish — it reflects the evangelistic heartbreak of watching a beloved community miss the Messiah. Mid-life adults who have prayed for family members for decades will recognize this grief immediately.

**Key Theological Concepts**

*"Not all Israel is Israel" (v. 6):* Paul distinguishes ethnic descent from covenant participation. Physical lineage does not guarantee spiritual inheritance. This was never God's design — Isaac, not Ishmael, was the child of promise; Jacob, not Esau, carried the covenant line. God's promises were always spiritually defined, not ethnically automatic.

*Election in Romans 9:* The NBC theological lens understands election in foreknowledge-based or corporate terms. God's choices of Isaac and Jacob were made before birth (v. 11) to demonstrate that His purposes do not rest on human merit — not to establish eternal damnation for Esau personally. Paul's concern is corporate and historical: which line carries the promise? The passage does not address the eternal destiny of individuals in isolation from faith.

*"I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" (v. 15):* Quoted from Exodus 33:19, this is not a declaration of divine arbitrariness but of divine freedom. God's mercy cannot be earned, demanded, or manipulated — it flows from His character. Significantly, this mercy is unlimited in scope: Christ died for the sins of the whole world, and grace calls, convicts, and enables every person — without coercing anyone.

*Pharaoh (vv. 17–18):* God "raised up" Pharaoh — meaning God placed him in a position of historical significance — to display divine power and extend the proclamation of God's name throughout the earth. Pharaoh's hardening follows a pattern: Exodus records Pharaoh hardening his own heart first (Exodus 8:15, 32), and God's judicial hardening follows. Paul uses this to show that God can work through resistant human choices without being the author of sin.

*The Potter and Clay (vv. 20–24):* Drawn from Jeremiah 18, the image does not strip humans of moral agency. The potter's authority is not cruelty — it is sovereign wisdom. Paul's point: God has the right to shape vessels for different purposes, including vessels of mercy prepared for glory. The NBC tradition rightly emphasizes that this mercy is extended to "us, whom He also called, not from among Jews only, but also from among Gentiles" (v. 24) — a universal, inclusive call.

**Greek Word Insight**

*Prothesis* (v. 11) — "purpose" — carries the sense of a deliberate, pre-arranged plan set before action begins. God's purposes are not reactive; they are proactive expressions of His character.

**Cross-References**
- Romans 3:21–26 — righteousness through faith for all
- Romans 10:13 — "Whoever will call on the name of the Lord will be saved"
- Romans 11:32 — God's mercy extended to all

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Object Lesson Preview (5–7 minutes)**

Before class, place a lump of modeling clay or air-dry clay on each table, or hold one piece visibly at the front. Begin by asking: "What can this clay do on its own? What does it become without the potter?" Let students respond briefly.

Then ask: "Have you ever felt like life was shaping you into something you didn't choose — a difficult job, a health crisis, a season of waiting on God? What does it feel like to be the clay?"

Allow 2–3 responses. Don't resolve the tension yet — let it sit.

**Warm-Up Discussion Prompt (2–3 minutes)**

Ask: "Has anyone ever felt like God was being unfair — either to you personally or to someone you love? You don't have to have the answer. Just name the feeling."

This creates honest entry into Romans 9's hardest questions.

**Transition into Teaching:**
"Paul felt that tension too. And in Romans 9, he doesn't flinch from it. Let's look at what he says — and what he doesn't say."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Romans chapter 9. We're stepping into one of the most debated passages in all of Scripture — and I want us to approach it the way Paul does: with honesty, with grief, and ultimately with hope.

Paul opens with something unexpected. Before he gives us any theology, he gives us his heart. Verses 1 through 3: "I am telling the truth in Christ, I am not lying, my conscience testifies with me in the Holy Spirit, that I have great sorrow and unceasing grief in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were accursed, separated from Christ for the sake of my brethren, my kinsmen according to the flesh."

Let that land. Paul is saying: if it were possible — and he knows it isn't — he would trade his own salvation for Israel's. That is not a theological argument. That is a broken heart. Before we ever get to questions about God's sovereignty, Paul shows us what a life shaped by God's mercy actually produces: an aching, relentless burden for people who are far from God.

Here's a question worth sitting with: Who is your "Israel"? Who are the people in your life — your family, your coworkers, your neighbors — whose lostness keeps you up at night? If nobody comes to mind, that's worth praying about before we leave today.

Now Paul moves into the theological question in verse 6: "But it is not as though the word of God has failed." This is the crisis he's addressing. If Jesus is the Messiah and Israel largely rejected Him, does that mean God broke His promise? Paul's answer is no — and here's why. He says, "For they are not all Israel who are descended from Israel." That sounds like a riddle, but it's actually a clarifying statement. God's covenant was never based on bloodline alone. It was always about faith and promise. Isaac, not Ishmael, was the child of promise. Jacob, not Esau, carried the covenant line — and God's choice between them was made before either had done anything good or bad. Paul's point in verse 11 is precise: God's purposes are not built on human performance. They rest on God's own character and call.

Now, I know some of you are thinking — "That sounds like God just picks favorites." But that's exactly the misreading Paul anticipates. He quotes God's words to Moses in verse 15: "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." This is from Exodus 33, right after the golden calf disaster — when Israel deserved judgment, not mercy. God is not saying He is arbitrary. He is saying His mercy cannot be earned, manipulated, or demanded. It flows from who He is, not from what we deserve. And here is the crucial point for our tradition: that mercy is not rationed. Christ died for the sins of the whole world. Grace calls, convicts, and enables every person. Nobody is excluded from the invitation. But nobody can force God's hand either.

What about Pharaoh? Paul brings him up in verse 17: "For the Scripture says to Pharaoh, 'For this very purpose I raised you up, to demonstrate My power in you, and that My name might be proclaimed throughout the whole earth.'" God placed Pharaoh in a position of historical significance. But look at Exodus carefully — Pharaoh hardened his own heart first, repeatedly, before God's judicial hardening followed. God did not create Pharaoh as a puppet. God worked through Pharaoh's own stubborn choices to accomplish something that spread the knowledge of God across the ancient world. That's not injustice. That's sovereignty working through history without overriding human accountability.

Then Paul gives us the potter and the clay in verses 20 through 24. And here's where the object lesson from earlier comes back. The potter has authority over the clay — not because the clay is worthless, but because the potter has a purpose in mind. Paul's question isn't "Does the clay have feelings?" It's "Does the potter have the right?" And the answer is yes. God has the right to shape history, to work through different people in different ways, to prepare vessels of mercy — and that mercy, verse 24 tells us, is extended to "us, whom He also called, not from among Jews only, but also from among Gentiles." That is an open door. That is an invitation that crosses every boundary.

Here's what this passage is not saying: it is not saying that God has pre-decided who goes to heaven and who goes to hell with no regard for human choice. Romans 10:13, just one chapter later, says "Whoever will call on the name of the Lord will be saved." Whoever. No asterisk. The same Paul who wrote Romans 9 wrote Romans 10. He holds both truths together — God's sovereign freedom and genuine human responsibility — because both are true.

So what does this mean for how we live? Think about your workplace. Think about the colleague who seems completely closed to spiritual things — maybe even hostile. Romans 9 says God's purposes are not thwarted by human resistance. His mercy is still moving. Your job is not to figure out who's "in" and who's "out." Your job is to carry the mercy you've received into every room you walk into.

Paul's grief for Israel was not passive. It drove him to preach, to plant churches, to suffer. Let your burden for the lost do the same. You are a vessel of mercy — shaped by the Potter, sent into the world.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Materials Needed:** Modeling clay (one piece per student), index cards, pens

---

**Activity 1: Clay Reflection (8–10 minutes)**

Give each student a small piece of modeling clay. Ask them to silently shape it into something that represents their current season of life — a struggle, a role, a relationship. No artistic skill required.

After 3–4 minutes, invite 2–3 volunteers to hold up their shape and complete this sentence: *"Right now, I feel like the Potter is shaping me into _______ because _______."*

Close by reading Romans 9:23 aloud: "vessels of mercy, which He prepared beforehand for glory."

*Adaptation for large classes:* Do this in pairs rather than whole-group sharing.

---

**Activity 2: "Who Is Your Israel?" Card (3–5 minutes)**

Hand each student an index card. Ask them to write — privately — the name of one person whose lostness genuinely burdens them. Below the name, write one specific way they could carry God's mercy to that person this week in a workplace or relational setting.

Cards are personal. No one shares unless they choose to.

Close with a 60-second silent prayer over those names.

*Adaptation for small classes:* Invite willing students to share the situation (not the name) and brainstorm together.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul opens Romans 9 with personal grief before presenting theology. What specifically does he say he would be willing to endure for Israel's sake, and why does that matter as a starting point for this passage?

**2.** **Understanding:** Paul says "they are not all Israel who are descended from Israel" (v. 6). What distinction is he drawing, and how does the example of Isaac versus Ishmael support his point?

**3.** **Analysis:** How does the Exodus account of Pharaoh hardening his own heart first help us understand what Paul means when he says God "hardened" Pharaoh? Why is this distinction important for understanding God's character?

**4.** **Application:** Paul holds God's sovereign freedom (Romans 9) and genuine human responsibility (Romans 10:13) together without resolving the tension. How does holding both truths simultaneously change the way you approach someone who seems resistant to the gospel?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson identified a "vessel of mercy" as someone shaped by the Potter and sent into the world. Where in your workplace or daily life is God calling you to carry that mercy this week?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can distinguish between God's mercy being *free* (not earned or coerced) and God's mercy being *arbitrary* (random or unfair). Students who grasp this will also be able to explain why Romans 9 and Romans 10:13 belong together rather than in contradiction.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Genuine engagement appears when students name a specific person they carry a burden for, express honest tension about unanswered prayer without abandoning trust in God, and connect Paul's grief to their own evangelistic responsibility rather than treating the passage as purely abstract theology.

**Closing Reflection:** *"If Paul's theology produced a broken heart for the lost — not detached doctrine — what should Romans 9 produce in you?"*

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Chosen, Called, and Commissioned: Understanding God's Sovereign Purpose**

**Key Idea:** God's sovereignty is not a barrier to mercy — it is the source of it. His grace flows freely to all through faith in Christ, and it calls us to carry that mercy into the world.

**Memory Verse:** "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." — Romans 9:15 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Paul's theology began with a broken heart — he grieved deeply for people far from God before he ever made a theological argument.
* God's covenant was never based on ethnic descent or human performance. It was always about faith and promise.
* God's mercy cannot be earned or demanded — it flows from His character. But it is unlimited in scope: Christ died for the whole world.
* God works through human choices and historical circumstances without being the author of sin or injustice.
* You are a vessel of mercy — shaped by the Potter with purpose, sent into every room you enter.

**Gospel Connection:** Romans 9 could feel like a closed door — as if God's sovereign choices leave us as helpless bystanders. But Paul himself swings the door wide open just one chapter later: "Whoever will call on the name of the Lord will be saved" (Romans 10:13). The same God who shapes history with sovereign freedom extends an unlimited invitation. Christ's death covered the sins of the whole world. The mercy Paul describes in Romans 9 is the very mercy available to you — and to everyone you know.

**Reflection Questions:**

**1.** Who is your "Israel" — the person or people whose lostness genuinely burdens you? What would it look like to let that burden move you to action rather than just anxiety?

**2.** Where have you been tempted to think God was being unfair — either to you or to someone you love? How does Romans 9's picture of God as the Potter speak to that feeling?

**3.** You are described as a vessel of mercy prepared for glory. What does that identity change about how you walk into your workplace, your neighborhood, or your family this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** Identify one person from your "Who Is Your Israel?" reflection. This week, take one concrete step to carry God's mercy toward them — a conversation, a meal, a note, a prayer offered in their presence. You don't need a sermon. You need a willing heart.

**Prayer:** "Lord, shape me like the Potter shapes clay — with purpose and wisdom I may not always understand. Give me Paul's broken heart for the people around me who don't yet know You. Remind me that Your mercy is not rationed, that Christ died for the whole world, and that I am sent as a vessel of that mercy. Use me this week. In Jesus' name, Amen."
