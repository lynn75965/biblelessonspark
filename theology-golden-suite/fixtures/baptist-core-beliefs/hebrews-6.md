## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Baptist Core Beliefs
**Lesson Title:** *Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of God's Faithfulness*

**Lesson Summary:** Hebrews 6:1–12 confronts believers with one of the most sobering passages in the New Testament, calling them to press beyond spiritual infancy toward genuine maturity. The passage issues a stark warning, then anchors hope in the unchanging character of God and the fruit visible in a life of genuine faith.

**Main Theme:** Spiritual growth is not optional — it is the natural trajectory of authentic saving faith, and God's faithfulness sustains those who press forward.

**Key Takeaway:** A genuine relationship with Christ produces forward movement, not stagnation — and God's faithfulness is the ground on which we press on.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often carry the weight of spiritual plateau. Many have been in church for decades and may privately wonder whether their faith is as alive as it once was. This passage speaks directly to that tension — not to condemn, but to call forward. Expect both conviction and comfort in the room.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews is written to Jewish believers — likely in Rome, before the destruction of the Temple in AD 70 — who are under pressure to abandon Christ and return to Judaism. The author is unknown, though tradition has suggested Paul, Apollos, or Barnabas. The letter's overarching argument is the supremacy of Christ over every element of the old covenant system.

Chapter 6 follows a sharp rebuke in 5:11–14, where the author calls the recipients "dull of hearing" — people who should be teaching others but still need someone to teach them the elementary principles of God's word. Chapter 6 opens as a direct continuation of that rebuke.

**The Six Elementary Principles (vv. 1–2)**

The author lists six foundational teachings: repentance from dead works, faith toward God, instruction about washings (likely referring to Jewish purification rites and their distinction from believer's baptism), laying on of hands, resurrection of the dead, and eternal judgment. These are not trivial matters — they are the ABC's of the faith. The point is not that these doctrines are unimportant, but that a believer must not remain permanently camped at the starting line. Maturity means building on the foundation, not perpetually re-laying it.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4–6)**

This is one of the most debated texts in the New Testament. The author describes people who have been "enlightened," have "tasted of the heavenly gift," have "been made partakers of the Holy Spirit," and have "tasted the good word of God." If such people "fall away," he says, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance.

Baptists have interpreted this passage in multiple ways, and teachers should acknowledge that honest, faithful scholars disagree. What is clear from the text:

1. The language describes someone with significant spiritual exposure and experience.
2. The agricultural metaphor in vv. 7–8 parallels Matthew 13 — ground that receives rain but produces only thorns is "worthless" and "close to being cursed."
3. The warning is real and serious — the author is not using hypothetical language to motivate; he is describing a genuine spiritual danger.
4. The passage does not say these people were definitively saved and then lost — the language of "tasting" and "partaking" may describe proximity to, rather than possession of, saving faith.

The key Baptist conviction here: genuine saving faith produces fruit (v. 9 — "things that accompany salvation"). God's faithfulness is the anchor, not human performance.

**The Assurance Passage (vv. 9–12)**

The author's tone shifts dramatically in verse 9: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." The word "beloved" (*agapētoi*) appears only here in Hebrews — it signals pastoral warmth after sobering warning. The "better things" are the fruit and labor of love visible in their lives. God is not unjust to forget their work and love shown toward His name (v. 10).

The Greek word for "sluggish" in verse 12 (*nōthroi*) is the same word translated "dull" in 5:11 — the author is tying the whole section together. The antidote to sluggishness is not guilt but imitation of those who through faith and patience inherit the promises.

**Key Doctrinal Connection**

This passage affirms that genuine faith is not static — it moves, grows, and bears fruit. For mid-life adults, the call is not to perform for God's approval, but to press forward because God's faithfulness is already established. The warning sobers; the assurance sustains.

Let students respond briefly, then transition: "Today's passage in Hebrews 6 uses that exact kind of image — and it has something urgent to say to people who have been following Christ for years."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Hebrews chapter 6. We're going to read verses 1 through 12 together, and I want to tell you upfront — this is one of those passages that has made serious Bible students uncomfortable for centuries. That's not a reason to avoid it. That's a reason to lean in.

Let me read verses 1 and 2 first.

> "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity, not laying again a foundation of repentance from dead works and of faith toward God, of instruction about washings and laying on of hands, and the resurrection of the dead and eternal judgment." — Hebrews 6:1–2 (NASB)

The author lists six foundational doctrines — repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, eternal judgment. These are not small things. These are the bedrock of Christian belief. But notice what he says: *leave* them. Not abandon them — build on them. There is a difference between a foundation and a dwelling place. You don't live in a foundation. You build on it.

Here's the question this raises for us as mid-life believers: Are we still circling the same spiritual starting line we crossed twenty years ago? Are we still asking the same beginner questions, still wrestling with the same beginner doubts, without ever pressing forward? The author says that's a problem — not because the foundation is wrong, but because you were never meant to stay there.

Now let's read the hard part. Verses 4 through 6.

> "For in the case of those who have once been enlightened and have tasted of the heavenly gift and have been made partakers of the Holy Spirit, and have tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come, and then have fallen away, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance, since they again crucify to themselves the Son of God and put Him to open shame." — Hebrews 6:4–6 (NASB)

I want to be honest with you: faithful, Bible-believing scholars have disagreed about this passage for a long time, and I'm not going to pretend there's one simple answer. What I can tell you is what the text clearly says and why it matters.

The author describes someone with enormous spiritual exposure — they've been enlightened, they've tasted the heavenly gift, they've had real contact with the Holy Spirit's work, they've experienced the power of God's Word. And yet, if they fall away, he says renewal is impossible. Why? Because they would be, in effect, crucifying Christ all over again — treating His sacrifice as worthless.

Here's what I want you to notice: the language is careful. "Tasted" and "partaker" describe proximity and experience — they don't automatically confirm possession of saving faith. Think about Matthew 13 and the parable of the soils. Some soil receives the seed enthusiastically. It springs up fast. But it has no root. The fruit test is what reveals what was really there.

Verses 7 and 8 make this agricultural picture explicit. Ground that receives rain — that receives every advantage — but produces thorns and thistles is worthless. The rain isn't the problem. The ground is the problem.

Now here is where the passage turns, and this turn is everything. Verse 9.

> "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation, though we are speaking in this way." — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

"Beloved." That word appears only once in the entire book of Hebrews — right here, right after the most sobering warning in the letter. The author is not writing to condemn these people. He is writing to wake them up and then hold them. The warning was the alarm clock. This verse is the hand on the shoulder.

"Things that accompany salvation" — that phrase is the key to the whole passage. What accompanies genuine salvation? Fruit. Labor. Love shown toward God's name. Verse 10 says God is not unjust — He sees the work, He sees the love, He does not forget.

What does this mean practically? It means assurance of faith is not found by staring at a moment in the past and saying, "I prayed a prayer in 1998, so I'm fine." Assurance is found in the present tense — in a life that is still moving, still serving, still loving, still pressing. That's not earning salvation. That's evidence of it.

Verse 12 closes with a word we need to hear. The author warns against becoming "sluggish" — and that Greek word is the same one he used back in chapter 5 when he called them "dull of hearing." He's been building to this. The antidote is not guilt. The antidote is imitation — looking at those who through faith and patience are inheriting the promises, and choosing to walk that same road.

Here is the challenge I want to leave with you: Spiritual maturity in the context of your church community is not a solo project. The author is writing to a congregation. He says *we* press on. He says *your* labor of love is visible to others. He says *imitate* those who are further down the road.

Who in this room is further down the road than you? Who could you follow more intentionally? And who might be watching you — needing you to press on so they have someone to imitate?

The warning of Hebrews 6 is real. But so is the assurance. God is not unjust. He sees your faith. He sees your labor. Press on — not to earn His approval, but because His faithfulness has already secured it.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: The Foundation vs. The House (Whole-Group Interactive)**
*Time: 8–10 minutes | Materials: Whiteboard or large paper, marker*

Draw a simple house outline on the board — foundation, walls, roof. Label the foundation "Elementary Teachings" and leave the upper structure blank.

Ask the group: "Based on Hebrews 6:1–2, what six doctrines form the foundation?" Fill them in as students call them out. Then ask: "What belongs in the upper structure — what does a maturing faith actually look like in your life and in this congregation?"

Write their responses in the walls and roof. Let the visual do the work: a house that is all foundation and no structure isn't a home anyone lives in.

*Adaptation: Smaller classes can draw their own houses individually, then share one item each.*

**Activity 2: The Fruit Test**
*Time: 5–6 minutes | Materials: Index cards, pens*

Give each student an index card. Ask them to write — privately — one honest answer to this question: "What fruit in my life right now would someone point to as evidence of genuine, growing faith?"

No sharing required. Close by reading Hebrews 6:10 aloud. Encourage students to keep the card as a personal benchmark.

*Adaptation: In larger classes, invite 2–3 volunteers to share without pressure.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** What are the six elementary teachings listed in Hebrews 6:1–2, and what does the author mean by telling believers to "leave" them?

**2.** **Understanding:** The author uses the word "tasted" to describe those in the warning passage. Based on the teaching, why is that word significant — and how does the Parable of the Soils in Matthew 13 help interpret it?

**3.** **Analysis:** The Greek word translated "sluggish" in verse 12 is the same word used for "dull" in Hebrews 5:11. What does that connection tell us about what the author is most concerned about in this letter?

**4.** **Application:** Verse 9 says the author is convinced of "things that accompany salvation." What does that phrase mean for how we think about assurance of faith — and how does it differ from simply pointing to a past decision?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The teacher closed by asking who in this class you could imitate, and who might be watching you. Who came to mind — and what would pressing on look like for you specifically in your church community this month?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who grasp that the warning passage is not primarily about losing salvation but about the danger of spiritual proximity without genuine root. Students should be able to distinguish between the foundation (elementary doctrines) and the structure of mature faith built upon it.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who are genuinely engaging will speak honestly about plateau, name specific fruit (or its absence) in their lives, and show interest in both the warning and the assurance — not just one or the other. Watch for deflection toward theological debate as a way of avoiding personal application.

**Closing Reflection:** "If God is not unjust to remember your labor and love — what labor and love do you want Him to see in the next six months?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of God's Faithfulness**

**Key Idea:** Genuine faith in Christ does not stand still. Hebrews 6 calls believers to move beyond spiritual infancy — and anchors that call in the unchanging faithfulness of God, not in human performance.

**Memory Verse:** "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation, though we are speaking in this way." — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The six foundational doctrines in Hebrews 6:1–2 are not meant to be re-laid endlessly — they are a starting line, not a finish line.
* The warning in verses 4–6 describes people with significant spiritual exposure who never moved from experience to genuine, rooted faith.
* Fruit — visible labor and love — is what "accompanies salvation." Assurance is found in a life still moving, not only in a moment from the past.
* God is not unjust. He sees your work and your love shown toward His name (v. 10). The warning sobers; His faithfulness sustains.
* Spiritual maturity is a community project. The author writes to a congregation — *we* press on together.

**Gospel Connection:** The reason pressing on is possible is not willpower — it is Christ. His death and resurrection are the foundation beneath every foundation. The author of Hebrews warns against treating His sacrifice as worthless (v. 6). Every step of growth is a step that honors what Christ accomplished. We press on because He already pressed through — through the cross, through the grave, and into the presence of God on our behalf.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your faith life have you been circling the starting line instead of building upward?
**2.** What fruit — specific, visible, present-tense — would someone point to as evidence of your growing faith right now?
**3.** Who in your church community could you intentionally imitate? Who might be watching you press on?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, identify one person in your congregation who is further down the road of faith than you are. Reach out to them — not to ask a theological question, but simply to observe how they live. Then find one person who might be watching you, and take one concrete step of faithfulness they can see.

**Prayer:** "Lord, we confess that it is easy to settle. We thank You that Your faithfulness does not depend on our momentum. Give us the courage to press on — not to earn Your approval, but because You have already secured it through Christ. Let the fruit of our lives be visible, and let our love toward Your name be something You do not forget. Amen."
