## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Baptist Core Beliefs
**Lesson Title:** *The Potter and the Clay: God's Sovereign Purpose in Salvation*

**Lesson Summary:** Romans 9:1–24 confronts believers with one of Scripture's most challenging passages — Paul's defense of God's faithfulness and righteous purpose in salvation. This lesson examines Paul's anguish for Israel, God's freedom to work through whom He chooses, and the image of the Potter shaping clay according to His own will. Students will wrestle honestly with the tension between human responsibility and God's sovereign purpose without landing on divisive doctrinal extremes.

**Main Theme:** God's saving purpose in Christ is rooted in His character and faithfulness, not in human heritage, effort, or merit.

**Key Takeaway:** Because salvation belongs to God alone, every believer's response is humility, gratitude, and urgent compassion for those who don't yet know Christ.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults often carry assumptions about fairness, merit, and what they deserve. Romans 9 directly challenges those assumptions and invites a posture of awe rather than argument. This group is mature enough to sit with theological tension without needing every question resolved.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Structural Context**

Romans 9–11 forms a self-contained unit within Paul's letter, addressing a question that his gospel raises unavoidably: if salvation is by grace through faith in Christ, what happened to God's promises to Israel? Has God's word failed? Paul's answer across three chapters is a resounding no. Romans 9 opens that defense by grounding God's faithfulness in His sovereign freedom to define and fulfill His promises on His own terms.

The passage divides into three movements: Paul's personal anguish for Israel (vv. 1–5), God's freedom in His saving purpose (vv. 6–18), and the Potter/clay image addressing human objection (vv. 19–24).

**Historical and Audience Context**

Paul writes to a mixed congregation of Jewish and Gentile believers in Rome, likely around AD 57. Jewish Christians may have felt the gospel diminished Israel's covenant standing. Gentile Christians may have grown dismissive of Israel's place in God's story. Paul addresses both tendencies simultaneously.

**Key Theological Concepts**

*God's word has not failed (v. 6).* Paul's foundational claim is that not all physical descendants of Abraham constitute the true Israel of promise. God's covenant purpose always operated through a line of promise, not merely biology — demonstrated through Isaac over Ishmael, and Jacob over Esau. These were not arbitrary choices but purposeful acts revealing that God's saving purpose in Christ operates through His call, not human lineage or achievement.

*"Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" (v. 13, quoting Malachi 1:2–3).* The word "hated" here reflects a Semitic idiom of comparative preference, not emotional hostility. More significantly, Paul's point is that God's choice preceded any action by either child (v. 11), establishing that God's saving purpose is not based on human works.

*Mercy and hardening (vv. 15–18).* Paul quotes Exodus 33:19 — God's own declaration to Moses — to show that mercy is God's sovereign prerogative. Pharaoh's hardening is the sobering counterpoint. Critically, Scripture elsewhere shows Pharaoh hardened his own heart first (Exodus 8:15); God's judicial hardening confirmed what Pharaoh had already chosen. This prevents any reading that makes God the author of sin.

*The Potter and clay (vv. 20–24).* The image draws from Jeremiah 18 and Isaiah 29:16. Paul is not arguing that humans are robots without accountability. He is silencing the creature's presumption to put God on trial. The clay does not interrogate the potter's design. The point is not that humans have no will, but that no human stands in a position to demand an accounting from God. God's freedom to show mercy to whom He will — including Gentiles — is the climax of the argument (vv. 23–24).

**Greek Word Insight**

The word translated "purpose" in verse 11 is *prothesis* — a deliberate, pre-set intention. It appears in Romans 8:28 as well ("called according to His purpose"). This is not reactive or circumstantial; it is God's settled, intentional saving purpose in Christ.

**Doctrinal Connections**

This passage grounds Baptist convictions about salvation being entirely of God's grace, not human merit. It also fuels evangelistic urgency — Paul's grief in verses 1–3 is the model response for anyone who truly grasps that people apart from Christ face judgment. Humility before God and compassion toward the lost are the twin fruits Romans 9 produces in a genuine believer.

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Romans 9. We're stepping into one of the most challenging passages in the entire New Testament — and one of the most rewarding if we're willing to stay with it.

Before Paul makes a single argument, he does something that should stop us in our tracks. Verses 1 through 3: "I am telling the truth in Christ, I am not lying, my conscience testifies with me in the Holy Spirit, that I have great sorrow and unceasing grief in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were accursed, separated from Christ for the sake of my brethren, my kinsmen according to the flesh."

Read that again slowly. Paul says he would be willing — if it were possible — to be cut off from Christ himself if it meant his fellow Jews would be saved. That is not rhetorical exaggeration. Paul is modeling what genuine compassion for the lost actually looks like. Not mild concern. Not occasional prayer. Unceasing grief. Before we wrestle with the hard theology in this chapter, we need to ask ourselves: do we carry anything close to this weight for people around us who don't know Christ? That's the pastoral heartbeat underneath the doctrine.

Now Paul raises the question that drives the entire chapter. Verse 6: "But it is not as though the word of God has failed." That's the accusation he's preempting. If Jesus is the fulfillment of God's promises to Israel, and most of Israel has rejected Jesus, did God's promises collapse? Paul's answer is no — because God's promises were never based on physical descent alone. He says, "For they are not all Israel who are descended from Israel." God's covenant purpose always worked through a line of promise, not mere biology.

He gives two examples. Isaac was chosen over Ishmael — both were Abraham's sons, but only one was the child of promise. Jacob was chosen over Esau — and here's the part that makes people uncomfortable — before either of them had done anything good or bad. Paul is making a precise point: God's saving purpose in Christ is not a reward for human performance. It never was.

When Paul quotes Malachi — "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" — don't let that word "hated" throw you. In Hebrew idiom, this is a comparison of preference, not a declaration of contempt. But more importantly, Paul's argument is about the principle: God's saving purpose operates on His terms, not ours.

Then comes the hardest section. Verse 15: God says to Moses, "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." Paul is quoting Exodus 33:19 — God's own self-description. Mercy is not something God owes. Mercy, by definition, is undeserved. If God owed it to everyone equally, it would not be mercy — it would be a transaction.

The Pharaoh example follows immediately. God hardened Pharaoh's heart. That troubles people. But here's what the full account in Exodus shows us: Pharaoh hardened his own heart first, repeatedly. What God did was confirm and solidify the direction Pharaoh had already chosen. God did not manufacture Pharaoh's rebellion — He gave it its final, judicial form. This matters because it protects us from making God the author of human sin, which Scripture never does.

Now Paul anticipates the objection you're probably already forming. Verse 19: "You will say to me then, 'Why does He still find fault? For who resists His will?'" That's a fair question. Paul doesn't dismiss it. But notice his response. He doesn't give a philosophical answer. He redirects the posture of the question. Verse 20: "On the contrary, who are you, O man, who answers back to God? The thing molded will not say to the molder, 'Why did you make me like this,' will it?"

This is the Potter and the clay image drawn from Jeremiah 18. Paul is not saying humans are robots with no will or accountability. He's saying no creature stands in a position to put the Creator on trial. There is an asymmetry between God and humanity that we must never collapse. The clay does not interrogate the potter's design — not because the clay's experience doesn't matter, but because the clay lacks the vantage point to judge the potter's wisdom.

And then Paul lands the passage in a place of breathtaking grace. Verses 23 and 24: God prepared vessels of mercy — "even us, whom He also called, not from among Jews only, but also from among Gentiles." The whole argument arrives here: God's saving purpose in Christ has always been wider than ethnic Israel. It was always moving toward a people drawn from every nation.

Here's what this passage produces in a person who truly receives it. First, humility. You did not earn your place before God. Your heritage, your church background, your moral effort — none of it secured your salvation. God's grace did. Second, gratitude. If mercy is undeserved, then every day you walk with Christ is a gift, not a wage. Third, urgency. Paul's grief in verse 3 is the model. People around you — neighbors, coworkers, family members — are outside of Christ. Romans 9 doesn't make us passive. It makes us desperate, in the best possible way, to share the gospel.

The question to carry out of this room is not "Have I resolved every theological tension in this chapter?" The question is: "Does my life reflect the grief of verse 3 and the gratitude of verse 24?"

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "The Grief Gauge" — Small Group Discussion**
*(8–10 minutes | No materials needed | Works for any class size)*

Divide into groups of 3–4. Give each group this prompt: *Paul said he had "unceasing grief" for people who didn't know Christ. On a scale of 1–10, how would you honestly rate your own burden for the lost people in your life right now — and what's one specific person you carry that concern for?*

Each person shares briefly. Groups don't need to report back — this is a personal honesty exercise. Bring the class back together and ask: "What would change about how we engage our neighbors, coworkers, or family members if we carried even half of Paul's grief from verse 3?"

*Adaptation: For smaller classes, do this as a full-group conversation rather than breakouts.*

---

**Activity 2: "Potter's Hands" — Reflection Exercise**
*(5 minutes | Optional: paper and pen)*

Ask each student to write down one area of life where they have resisted God's shaping — a situation where they've been tempted to say, "Why did you make me like this?" (v. 20). They don't share this publicly. Then ask them to write one sentence of surrender beneath it.

Close by reading Jeremiah 18:6 aloud: *"Can I not do with you as this potter does?"* — and let that sit in silence for 30 seconds before moving on.

*Adaptation: In larger classes, this works as a silent individual exercise with no sharing required.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul opens Romans 9 with personal anguish before making any theological argument. What exactly does he say in verses 1–3, and why does the teacher suggest this is the "pastoral heartbeat" of the chapter?

**2.** **Understanding:** Paul argues in verse 6 that "they are not all Israel who are descended from Israel." What does he mean, and how do the examples of Isaac/Ishmael and Jacob/Esau support that point?

**3.** **Analysis:** The lesson distinguishes between Pharaoh hardening his own heart and God's judicial hardening. Why does that distinction matter theologically? What would be at stake if we ignored it?

**4.** **Application:** The Potter/clay image in verses 20–21 is not about humans being robots. What is Paul actually arguing, and how does that posture challenge the way mid-life adults typically think about fairness and what they deserve from God?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closes with three responses Romans 9 should produce — humility, gratitude, and urgency. Which of these is most personally convicting for you right now, and what would acting on it look like this week in your community or neighborhood?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can distinguish God's saving purpose from human merit or heritage, and whether they understand the Pharaoh hardening as judicial confirmation rather than divine manufacture of sin. Strong answers will reference specific verses rather than speaking in generalities.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged this passage will express some discomfort with how little credit they can claim for their salvation — and some renewed sense of burden for people outside of Christ. Watch for movement from intellectual wrestling to personal application.

**Closing Reflection:** *"If Paul's grief in verse 3 is the model for genuine compassion, what is one person in your life right now that God may be calling you to pursue with that kind of urgency?"*

## Section 8: Group Handout

**The Potter and the Clay: God's Sovereign Purpose in Salvation**

**Key Idea:** Salvation belongs entirely to God — not to human heritage, effort, or merit. Romans 9 calls every believer to respond with humility, gratitude, and urgent compassion for those who don't yet know Christ.

**Memory Verse:** "So then it does not depend on the man who wills or the man who runs, but on God who has mercy." — Romans 9:16 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Paul's grief for the lost in verses 1–3 is not rhetorical — it is the model for genuine evangelistic compassion. Before doctrine, there is a broken heart.
* God's saving purpose in Christ has never been based on bloodline, church background, or moral performance. It operates through His call and grace.
* "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" (v. 13) uses a Hebrew idiom of comparative preference — and Paul's point is that God's choice preceded any human action or achievement.
* The Potter/clay image (vv. 20–21) doesn't erase human accountability — it silences the creature's presumption to put the Creator on trial.
* God's saving purpose was always moving toward a people drawn from every nation, not ethnic Israel alone (vv. 23–24).

**Gospel Connection:** Romans 9 strips away every false foundation we might build our standing before God upon — family faith, church attendance, moral effort, or cultural heritage. What remains is grace alone, through Christ alone. Jesus lived the life we couldn't live, died the death we deserved, and rose so that mercy could reach anyone — Jew or Gentile, moral or broken, religious or far from God. If you are in Christ, you are there because God's grace found you, not because you found your way to Him.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where have you been tempted to believe your background or effort earned you favor with God?
**2.** Is there someone in your life right now for whom you carry "unceasing grief" — the kind Paul describes in verse 3?
**3.** Which response does Romans 9 most need to produce in you this week: humility, gratitude, or urgency toward someone who doesn't know Christ?

**Weekly Challenge:** Identify one person outside of Christ in your neighborhood, workplace, or family. This week, take one deliberate step toward them — a conversation, an invitation, a meal, or simply a prayer said out loud on their behalf. Let Paul's grief in verse 3 move from something you studied to something you carry.

**Prayer:** "Father, forgive me for the times I have treated Your grace as something I deserved. Thank You that salvation rests on Your mercy, not my merit. Break my heart for the people around me who don't yet know Christ. Give me Paul's grief and Your compassion. Shape me as the Potter shapes clay — according to Your purpose, not my comfort. In Jesus' name, Amen."
