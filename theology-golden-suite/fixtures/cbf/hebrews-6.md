## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Cooperative Baptist Fellowship
**Lesson Title:** *Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Anchor of God's Faithfulness*

**Lesson Summary:** Hebrews 6:1–12 calls believers to move beyond spiritual infancy toward genuine maturity, while issuing one of Scripture's most sobering warnings against turning away from Christ. The passage balances that warning with confident assurance rooted not in human performance but in God's character and the believer's demonstrated love and faithfulness. Students will wrestle honestly with what spiritual growth looks like in the middle decades of life.

**Main Theme:** Spiritual maturity is not automatic with age — it requires intentional forward movement, and God's faithfulness sustains those who press on.

**Key Takeaway:** Because God is faithful, mid-life believers can move past spiritual stagnation and press toward maturity with confidence rather than fear.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often carry accumulated spiritual experience alongside the risk of plateau. Many have heard foundational doctrines for decades but may be coasting. This passage speaks directly to that tension — honoring what they know while challenging them to go deeper.

## Section 3: Theological Background

**Literary and Historical Context**
Hebrews was written to Jewish Christians facing intense pressure to abandon their faith in Christ and return to the familiarity of Second Temple Judaism. The author (unnamed in the text; tradition suggests various candidates, though Scripture does not identify them) writes with pastoral urgency. Chapters 5–7 form a sustained argument about Christ as the superior high priest in the order of Melchizedek. The author interrupts that argument at 5:11 to rebuke the readers for spiritual dullness — they should be teachers by now but still need milk. Hebrews 6:1–12 is the direct response to that rebuke: stop circling the starting line and run the race.

**The "Elementary Teachings" (vv. 1–3)**
The six foundational items — repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, and eternal judgment — are not dismissed as unimportant. They are the foundation. The author's point is architectural: you do not keep laying a foundation once the house is being built. These doctrines are assumed and established; now build on them. For mid-life believers who have heard these truths for decades, the question becomes: what have you built on that foundation?

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4–8)**
This is among the most debated texts in the New Testament. The author describes people who have been "enlightened," "tasted of the heavenly gift," "made partakers of the Holy Spirit," "tasted the good word of God," and experienced "the powers of the age to come" — and who then fall away. Three major interpretive streams exist:

1. *Hypothetical warning*: The scenario is presented as a logical impossibility to motivate perseverance, not describe an actual occurrence.
2. *Loss of fellowship/reward, not salvation*: The "falling away" describes severe spiritual regression, not loss of eternal life.
3. *Apostasy warning to nominal believers*: Those described were never genuinely regenerate; the rich language describes proximity to grace, not possession of it.

CBF's commitment to soul freedom means teachers should present these views honestly without imposing one as the only faithful reading. What is not debated: the author's pastoral purpose is to motivate pressing forward, not to produce paralyzing fear. The agricultural image in vv. 7–8 reinforces this — land that receives rain and produces a crop is blessed; land that produces only thorns faces judgment. The emphasis is on fruit-bearing.

**Key Greek Insight**
The verb translated "press on" in verse 1 is *pherōmetha* — a passive form meaning "let us be carried forward" or "let us be borne along." This is not purely self-generated striving; it carries the sense of being moved by a current. Spiritual maturity involves yielding to the Spirit's forward momentum, not white-knuckling one's way to holiness.

**The Anchor: God's Faithfulness (vv. 9–12)**
The author pivots sharply in verse 9: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." The warning was real; the confidence is equally real. God is not unjust — He remembers their work and love. The call to imitate those who through faith and patience inherit the promises grounds assurance not in the believer's perfect performance but in God's character. This is the theological center: forward movement is possible because God is faithful.

**Cross-References**
- Hebrews 10:26–31 (parallel warning passage)
- James 1:2–4 (maturity through testing)
- 2 Peter 1:5–8 (building on faith progressively)

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Hook Question (5 minutes)**
Open with this question — do not answer it yet, just let it land:

*"If someone who knew you ten years ago sat down with you today, would they say your faith has grown deeper — or just older?"*

Give students sixty seconds of silent thought. Then ask two or three volunteers to share one word that came to mind. Do not process the answers yet — simply acknowledge them and move on.

**Individual Written Reflection — Warm-Up (5 minutes)**
Hand out index cards. Ask students to write privately (nothing shared):

*"Name one spiritual discipline or area of growth you were more intentional about five years ago than you are today. What changed?"*

This is not a guilt exercise — it is an honest inventory. Collect nothing. Students keep their cards for the closing journaling prompt in the main teaching.

**Transition into Teaching:**
"The author of Hebrews looked at a congregation with real spiritual history and said, 'You should be further along.' That's not a condemnation — it's an invitation. Let's look at what pressing forward actually means."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content

Today we're in Hebrews 6:1–12, and I want to start by reading verse 1 aloud together. The NASB says:

> "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity, not laying again a foundation of repentance from dead works and of faith toward God."
> — Hebrews 6:1 (NASB)

That phrase — "let us press on to maturity" — is the heartbeat of this entire passage. And I want to unpack it carefully, because it is both a challenge and an invitation.

**What Does "Leaving" the Foundation Mean?**

First, let's be clear about what the author is NOT saying. He is not saying the foundation doesn't matter. Repentance, faith, baptism, resurrection, judgment — these are not optional electives. They are bedrock. But here's the architectural reality: you don't keep pouring concrete once the house is going up. If you've been a follower of Christ for fifteen or twenty years and you are still only engaging with Christianity at the level of "Jesus loves me and I should be nicer," something has stalled. The foundation is there. What are you building on it?

Think about it this way. Imagine someone who graduated from college twenty years ago but still only reads at the level of their freshman orientation materials. We would say something has gone wrong. The same is true spiritually. The author of Hebrews is saying: you have the foundation. Now build.

**The Warning We Have to Take Seriously**

Now we come to verses 4–8, and I will not pretend this is easy territory. The author describes people who have experienced real spiritual things — enlightenment, the taste of the heavenly gift, participation in the Holy Spirit, the good word of God — and who then fall away. And he says it is impossible to renew them again to repentance.

Honest teachers have to acknowledge that faithful, Spirit-guided readers have interpreted this passage differently. Some read it as a hypothetical scenario — a logical impossibility meant to say, "If someone could fall away from this, there would be no recovery, but that's not what happens to genuine believers." Others read it as describing people who were close to the faith but never truly in it — they tasted but never swallowed. Still others read it as a warning about severe spiritual regression, not the loss of salvation itself.

What all of these readings share is this: the author's purpose is not to terrify you into paralysis. It is to motivate forward movement. The agricultural image makes that plain. Land that receives rain and produces a crop is blessed. Land that receives the same rain and produces only thorns is in trouble. The rain falls on both. The question is what you do with it.

Here's the pastoral truth for mid-life believers specifically: the danger the author is warning against is not dramatic apostasy in most cases. It is slow drift. It is the gradual substitution of religious familiarity for genuine encounter with the living Christ. It is knowing the vocabulary without living the reality. That is what "dull of hearing" looks like at forty-five.

**The Greek Word That Changes Everything**

Here's something worth sitting with. The phrase "let us press on" in verse 1 — the Greek word behind it is *pherōmetha*, and it is passive. It doesn't mean "grit your teeth and push harder." It means "let us be carried forward." There is a current moving. The Spirit is moving. The question is whether you are yielding to that movement or anchoring yourself to the starting line.

Spiritual maturity in your thirties, forties, and fifties is not about performing harder. It is about yielding more. Yielding your assumptions to deeper study. Yielding your comfort to genuine service. Yielding your digital distractions — the scrolling, the noise, the endless content — to actual silence before God. Think about how much of your mental and spiritual bandwidth gets consumed by screens and feeds. What would it look like to redirect even a fraction of that toward genuine growth?

**The Anchor: God Remembers**

Now here is where the passage turns, and I don't want you to miss this pivot. Verse 9: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." The author just issued a serious warning — and then he says, I believe better things about you. Why? Because of God's character.

Verse 10 says God is not unjust so as to forget your work and the love which you have shown toward His name. God sees the Sunday morning faithfulness. God sees the quiet acts of service no one else noticed. God sees the prayers you prayed in the car on the way to work. He does not forget. The call to press forward is not issued to people who have to earn God's attention. It is issued to people who already have it.

The closing challenge of this passage is verse 12: don't be sluggish, but imitate those who through faith and patience inherit the promises. Faith and patience together. Not faith alone as a one-time event, and not patience as passive waiting — but the two working together as a sustained, forward-moving trust in a faithful God.

Here is where I want to leave you today. On that index card you filled out at the beginning — the one where you named something you were more intentional about five years ago — I want you to turn it over. Write one sentence: *"One step I can take this week to press forward is..."*

God is not asking for perfection. He is asking for movement. And because He is faithful, movement is possible.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "Foundation or Framework?" Sorting Exercise**
*Time: 8–10 minutes | Materials: Index cards or slips of paper, pens*

Write each of the six elementary teachings from Hebrews 6:1–3 on separate slips: repentance from dead works, faith toward God, instruction about baptisms, laying on of hands, resurrection of the dead, eternal judgment.

Distribute slips to pairs or small groups. Ask each pair to answer two questions about their assigned teaching:
- "How well do I understand this foundational doctrine?"
- "What would *building on* this look like in my daily life right now?"

Each pair shares one insight with the class. This moves students from passive recall to active construction — exactly what the passage demands.

*Adaptation: For a smaller class, each person takes two slips and works individually.*

---

**Activity 2: Individual Written Reflection — "Yield or Anchor?"**
*Time: 5–7 minutes | Materials: Paper or journal, pens*

Remind students of the Greek word *pherōmetha* — "let us be carried forward." Ask each person to write privately in response to this prompt:

*"Where in your digital or daily life are you anchoring yourself to the starting line instead of yielding to the Spirit's forward movement? Name one specific habit, and write one sentence about what yielding might look like instead."*

No sharing required. This feeds directly into the closing journaling prompt from the teaching.

*Adaptation: In a large class, invite two or three volunteers to share only their "yielding" sentence, not the specific habit.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** What six foundational teachings does the author of Hebrews list in verses 1–3, and what architectural image does the passage use to explain why believers should move beyond them?

**2.** **Understanding:** Three interpretive approaches to the warning in verses 4–8 were presented. Describe one of them in your own words. What do all three approaches agree on regarding the author's pastoral purpose?

**3.** **Analysis:** The Greek word *pherōmetha* is passive — "let us be carried forward." How does this change the way you think about spiritual growth? What is the difference between striving for maturity and yielding to it?

**4.** **Application:** The lesson described "slow drift" as the more common danger for mid-life believers — substituting religious familiarity for genuine encounter with Christ. Where do you see that pattern most clearly in your own life or in the life of the church?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Verse 10 says God is not unjust — He will not forget your work and love shown toward His name. How does God's faithfulness change the emotional tone of the call to press forward? Does it feel more like a burden or an invitation when you read it that way?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can distinguish the warning's purpose (motivation, not paralysis) from its content, and whether they connect God's faithfulness in verses 9–10 to the possibility of forward movement rather than treating it as a separate idea.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged will speak about specific areas of stagnation rather than generalities, express the tension between the warning and assurance without collapsing either, and frame growth in terms of yielding rather than performance.

**Closing Reflection:** "If God already sees your work and love and will not forget it — what would you attempt for Him this week if fear were completely off the table?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

***Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Anchor of God's Faithfulness***
*Hebrews 6:1–12*

**Key Idea:** Spiritual maturity is not automatic — it requires intentional forward movement. Because God is faithful, mid-life believers can press toward growth with confidence rather than fear.

**Memory Verse:** "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity." — Hebrews 6:1a (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The six foundational teachings in verses 1–3 are not dismissed — they are the foundation. The question is what you are building on them.
* The warning in verses 4–8 is real and should be taken seriously, but its purpose is to motivate forward movement, not produce paralyzing fear.
* The Greek word behind "press on" is *pherōmetha* — a passive form meaning "let us be carried forward." Maturity is about yielding to the Spirit's movement, not white-knuckling your way to holiness.
* Slow drift — substituting religious familiarity for genuine encounter with Christ — is the specific danger the passage addresses for long-term believers.
* God is not unjust. He sees your faithfulness, your quiet service, your unnoticed prayers. The call to grow comes from a God who already knows your name.

**Gospel Connection:** The author of Hebrews does not call believers forward by threatening them — he calls them forward by reminding them of who God is. Christ, our great high priest, intercedes for us. His faithfulness is the ground under every step of growth. We press on not to earn God's favor but because we already have it through Christ.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** What is one area of your faith life where you have been circling the foundation instead of building on it?
**2.** Where in your daily or digital life might you be anchoring yourself to the starting line instead of yielding to the Spirit's forward momentum?
**3.** How does verse 10 — God's promise to remember your work and love — change the way you hear the call to press forward?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, identify one concrete step toward spiritual growth and take it — not out of fear, but out of trust in a faithful God. It might mean returning to a neglected spiritual discipline, having a conversation you've been avoiding, or simply putting down your phone for thirty minutes to sit in silence before God.

**Prayer:** "Lord, You see every step we have taken and every place we have stalled. Thank You that Your call to press on comes wrapped in Your faithfulness, not our performance. Carry us forward by Your Spirit. Where we have drifted, draw us back. Where we have coasted, stir us. We trust not in our own strength but in Yours. Amen."
