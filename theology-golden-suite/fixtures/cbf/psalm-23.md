## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Cooperative Baptist Fellowship

**Lesson Title:** *The Lord Is My Shepherd: Trusting God's Sufficiency in Every Season*

**Lesson Summary:** Psalm 23 is one of Scripture's most beloved and theologically rich passages, offering a portrait of God as the attentive, sufficient Shepherd who guides, restores, and accompanies believers through every dimension of life. This lesson invites mid-life adults to move beyond familiarity with the psalm's words and into genuine trust in the God those words describe. Students will examine what it means to confess "the LORD is my shepherd" not as sentiment, but as a declaration of dependence and confidence.

**Main Theme:** God's shepherding presence is sufficient for every circumstance — from green pastures to the valley of the shadow of death.

**Key Takeaway:** Because the Lord is your Shepherd, you lack nothing essential — and that truth reshapes how you face fear, loss, and uncertainty.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often carry the weight of competing responsibilities — aging parents, career pressures, raising teenagers, and personal health concerns. Psalm 23 meets them precisely where exhaustion and anxiety live, offering not platitudes but a Person.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Author and Context**
Psalm 23 is attributed to David, whose superscription reads "A Psalm of David." David's background as a literal shepherd before becoming king gives the imagery autobiographical weight — he knew from the inside what it meant both to shepherd and to be shepherded. The psalm belongs to the broader Psalter's collection of trust psalms, poems in which the writer moves from vulnerability to confidence through focused attention on who God is.

**Literary Structure**
The psalm divides naturally into three movements. Verses 1–3 describe God as active provider and guide — feeding, leading, restoring, directing. Verse 4 pivots sharply: the landscape changes from green pastures to "the valley of the shadow of death," and the pronouns shift from third person ("He leads me") to second person ("You are with me"). This grammatical shift is theologically significant — in the darkest moment, abstract description becomes intimate address. Verses 5–6 move from wilderness to table, from survival to abundance, culminating in dwelling in God's house forever.

**Key Hebrew Insights**
- *YHWH* (LORD, v. 1): The covenant name of God, not a generic title. The psalm's opening claim is covenantal — this Shepherd is the God who bound himself to his people in promise.
- *Shalom* (want/lack, v. 1): "I shall not want" does not promise the absence of hardship; it declares sufficiency. The Hebrew *chaser* means "to lack" — the psalmist testifies that with YHWH as shepherd, nothing essential is missing.
- *Nahal* (leads, v. 2): The word carries the image of a shepherd guiding animals to still water with gentle, patient movement — not driving but leading.
- *Tsalmaveth* (shadow of death, v. 4): This compound Hebrew word (*tsal* = shadow, *maveth* = death) describes deep, life-threatening darkness — not mere difficulty but mortal danger. God's presence does not remove the valley; it accompanies the walker through it.
- *Rod and staff* (v. 4): The rod was used for protection against predators; the staff for guiding and steadying sheep. Together they represent God's protective authority and gentle guidance operating simultaneously.

**Doctrinal Connections**
The psalm anticipates the New Testament's portrait of Jesus as the Good Shepherd (John 10:11–15), who lays down his life for the sheep. Psalm 23 is not merely comfort literature — it is a theological confession about the nature of God: present, sufficient, protective, and ultimately victorious over every threat. The table set "in the presence of my enemies" (v. 5) signals that God's provision is not contingent on circumstances being favorable — it operates *within* adversity, not after it resolves.

**Age-Appropriate Depth Note**
Mid-life adults have typically experienced enough of life to know that Psalm 23 is either genuinely true or merely poetic. The teacher should honor that tension. The psalm's power is not in its beauty but in its claim — and that claim deserves honest engagement.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — Thought-Provoking Question (3–4 minutes)**

Open by asking the class: *"If someone asked you right now, 'Is your life going well?' — what would you actually say, if you were being completely honest?"*

Give students thirty seconds of silence to sit with that question. Then ask: *"Now — what would change about your answer if you were absolutely certain that God was actively, personally shepherding your life at this very moment?"*

Don't collect answers yet. Let the tension sit.

**Activity 2 — Familiarity Check (2 minutes)**

Ask students to recite Psalm 23 from memory — or as much as they can. Most mid-life adults will know significant portions. Then say: *"Here's the question we're going to wrestle with today: Is this psalm something you know, or something you trust? Those are two very different things."*

**Transition:** "Let's open to Psalm 23 and read it together — but this time, slowly, as if you've never heard it before."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bible to Psalm 23. We're going to read it together in the NASB, and I want us to read it slowly — because one of the greatest dangers with a familiar passage is that we stop actually hearing it.

> "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want. He makes me lie down in green pastures; He leads me beside quiet waters. He restores my soul; He guides me in the paths of righteousness for His name's sake. Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I fear no evil, for You are with me; Your rod and Your staff, they comfort me. You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies; You have anointed my head with oil; my cup overflows. Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life, and I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever."
> — Psalm 23:1–6 (NASB)

Now — notice something before we go any further. The psalm doesn't open with a request. It opens with a declaration: *"The LORD is my shepherd."* David isn't asking God to be his shepherd. He's stating it as present reality. That's a confession of trust, not a wish.

And that word LORD — in your Bible it's probably in small capital letters. That's the translators' way of signaling that the Hebrew word is *YHWH* — the covenant name of God. Not a generic title for a divine being. The specific God who bound himself in covenant promise to his people. So when David says "The LORD is my shepherd," he's saying: the God who makes and keeps promises — *that* God is personally shepherding my life. That changes everything.

"I shall not want." Think about what that doesn't mean. It doesn't mean David had no problems, no enemies, no dark seasons. The rest of the psalm makes clear he had all three. What it means is that with this Shepherd, nothing *essential* is missing. The Hebrew word behind "want" means to lack — and David is testifying that YHWH provides what truly matters. This is not prosperity theology. This is sufficiency theology. There's a massive difference. Prosperity theology says God will give you everything you desire. Sufficiency theology says God will ensure you lack nothing you genuinely need.

Now watch the structure of this psalm, because it's doing something intentional. Verses 1 through 3 describe what God does: He makes, He leads, He restores, He guides. God is the subject of every verb. David is the object — the one being acted upon. That's a picture of grace. We don't generate our own restoration. We don't manufacture our own guidance. We receive it from a Shepherd who is already at work.

Then verse 4 arrives — and the landscape changes completely. Green pastures become "the valley of the shadow of death." The Hebrew behind that phrase describes deep, mortal darkness — not a bad week, but the kind of darkness where you genuinely fear you might not come out the other side. And here's what's remarkable: the grammar changes. David stops talking *about* God and starts talking *to* God. "You are with me. Your rod and Your staff, they comfort me." In the darkest moment, theology becomes conversation. Abstract truth becomes intimate address.

Think about what that means for your life right now. Many of you are in seasons that don't look like green pastures. Some of you are walking through valleys — a diagnosis, a marriage under strain, a child who has walked away from faith, a career that's collapsing, grief that won't lift. The psalm doesn't promise you'll avoid those valleys. It promises you won't walk through them alone. The Shepherd doesn't remove the valley — he enters it with you.

The rod and the staff are worth pausing on. The rod was a weapon — used to fight off predators threatening the flock. The staff was a tool — used to guide, steady, and rescue sheep who wandered or fell. Together they represent God's protective authority and his tender guidance operating at the same time. The same God who fights for you also gently steers you. You don't have to choose between a God who is powerful and a God who is gentle. Psalm 23 says he is both, simultaneously.

Then the psalm makes a surprising move in verse 5. We've gone from pasture to valley — and now we're at a table. "You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies." Not after the enemies are gone. Not once the threat has passed. In their presence. God's provision doesn't wait for your circumstances to improve. He sets a table in the middle of the conflict. That is a picture of a God who is not intimidated by what threatens you.

And notice the intimacy of verse 5 — anointed with oil, a cup that overflows. These are images of honored guests, of abundance, of being valued. God doesn't merely tolerate your presence; he honors it.

Verse 6 closes with a word that deserves our full attention: *"Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life."* That word "follow" in Hebrew carries the sense of pursuit — goodness and God's covenant love are not passive bystanders; they are actively chasing you down every day of your life. And the destination? Dwelling in the house of the LORD forever. The psalm moves from survival to flourishing, from provision to presence, from the valley to the eternal home.

Here is the challenge I want to leave with you: Psalm 23 is either true or it isn't. If it's true — if the LORD really is your Shepherd — then the question is whether you are living as someone who believes it. Not just someone who can recite it. Someone who trusts it in the valley, at the table, in the dark, and in the ordinary Tuesday afternoon of mid-life when nothing dramatic is happening but the weight of life is still very real.

The Good Shepherd — whom Jesus himself claimed to be in John 10 — has never lost a sheep he was determined to keep. That's the ground you stand on.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — Paired Sharing: "Which Verse Is Yours?" (5–7 minutes)**

Materials needed: None. Works with any class size.

Ask students to silently read Psalm 23 once more, then identify the single verse that speaks most directly to their current life season — the one that either comforts them most or challenges them most to actually believe.

Pair students together. Each person shares: (1) which verse they chose, and (2) one sentence about why it connects to where they are right now. Partners listen without responding — this is sharing, not debating.

Bring the group back together. Ask two or three volunteers to share what they heard from their partner (not themselves — this builds attentive listening). Note patterns: are most people drawn to the valley verse, the table verse, or the opening declaration?

*Adaptation: For smaller classes (under 8), skip pairing and share as a full group. For larger classes, keep pairs tight — no groups of three, which slow the exercise.*

**Activity 2 — Workplace Application Prompt (3–4 minutes)**

Pose this scenario: *"Think about your workplace on a typical stressful day — a difficult meeting, a conflict with a colleague, a project going sideways. Which image from Psalm 23 would you most need to hold onto in that moment, and why?"*

Allow one minute of quiet reflection, then take three or four brief responses. No extended discussion — this plants the application seed for the closing challenge.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** The teaching noted a significant grammatical shift in verse 4. What changes in the way David addresses God — and where exactly does that shift occur in the psalm?

**2.** **Understanding:** The lesson distinguished between "prosperity theology" and "sufficiency theology." In your own words, what is the difference, and why does it matter for how we read "I shall not want"?

**3.** **Analysis:** The psalm moves through three distinct landscapes — green pastures, the dark valley, and a prepared table. What does this progression suggest about how God's presence operates across different life circumstances?

**4.** **Application:** The teaching described the rod and staff as representing God's protective authority and tender guidance working simultaneously. Think about a current workplace or family pressure. Which of those two — protection or guidance — do you find yourself needing most right now?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closed with a direct challenge: Psalm 23 is either true or it isn't. What would it look like, concretely, for you to live this week as someone who trusts this psalm rather than merely knows it?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can articulate the difference between knowing the psalm as familiar text and trusting it as personal confession. Strong responses will reference specific verses and connect them to real-life circumstances rather than speaking in generalities.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students demonstrating genuine engagement will name specific valleys or pressures in their lives without deflecting, express honest tension between the psalm's claims and their experience, and show curiosity about what it would mean to live differently based on what the psalm declares.

**Closing Reflection:** "If the LORD truly is your Shepherd right now — not someday, not in theory, but today — what is the one thing you are carrying that you could actually set down?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**The Lord Is My Shepherd: Trusting God's Sufficiency in Every Season**

**Key Idea:** Psalm 23 is not sentimental poetry — it is a bold declaration that the covenant God of Scripture is personally, actively shepherding your life through every season, including the darkest ones.

**Memory Verse:** "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want." — Psalm 23:1 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The opening line is a confession, not a wish. David declares God *is* his shepherd — present tense, present reality.
* "I shall not want" means sufficiency, not the absence of hardship. With God as Shepherd, nothing truly essential is missing.
* In verse 4, when the valley arrives, David stops talking *about* God and starts talking *to* God — theology becomes conversation in the dark.
* The rod and staff work together: God fights for you and guides you at the same time. Power and gentleness are not opposites in this Shepherd.
* God sets a table *in the presence of your enemies* — his provision doesn't wait for your circumstances to improve first.
* Goodness and lovingkindness are not passive — they are actively pursuing you every day of your life.

**Gospel Connection:** Jesus declared, "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down His life for the sheep" (John 10:11, NASB). Psalm 23's portrait of the faithful Shepherd finds its fullest expression in Christ, who entered the darkest valley — death itself — to ensure his sheep would dwell in God's house forever. The sufficiency promised in the psalm is secured by the cross.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Which verse of Psalm 23 feels most true to your life right now — and which feels hardest to believe?
**2.** Where is your current "dark valley," and what would it mean to believe you are not walking through it alone?
**3.** What is one practical way you could remind yourself this week that God's goodness is actively pursuing you?

**Weekly Challenge:** Choose one verse from Psalm 23 that speaks directly to a pressure you are carrying at work, at home, or in a relationship. Write it somewhere you will see it daily — your phone, your desk, your mirror. Each time you see it, say it aloud as a declaration, not a wish: *"The LORD is my shepherd."*

**Prayer:** "Lord, I confess that I often know this psalm better than I trust it. Teach me to live as someone who believes you are truly shepherding my life — in the green pastures and in the valley, at the table and in the dark. You are my Shepherd. I lack nothing I truly need. Pursue me with your goodness today. Amen."
