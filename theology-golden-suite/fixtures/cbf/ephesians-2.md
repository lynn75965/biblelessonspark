## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Cooperative Baptist Fellowship

**Lesson Title:** *From Death to Life: The Grace That Remakes Us*

**Lesson Summary:**
Ephesians 2:1-10 presents one of Scripture's most complete portraits of salvation — where humanity has been, what God did about it, and where grace is taking us. Paul moves from the darkness of spiritual death to the breathtaking reality of resurrection life in Christ, grounding everything in God's initiative, not human achievement. Students will encounter a grace that is both deeply personal and cosmically significant.

**Main Theme Statement:**
God's grace rescues people from spiritual death not because of anything they have done, but because of who God is — and that grace always moves toward purpose.

**Key Takeaway:**
Salvation is entirely God's work, received by faith, and designed to send us into a life of good works prepared specifically for us.

**Audience Insight:**
Mid-life adults (36–50) often carry either spiritual complacency or accumulated guilt. This passage speaks directly to both — dismantling self-sufficiency and shame alike with the same grace. Many in this age group are also wrestling with questions of meaning and legacy, making the "created for good works" conclusion especially resonant.

## Section 3: Theological Background

**Literary and Historical Context:**
Paul wrote Ephesians from prison, likely around AD 60–62, to a predominantly Gentile congregation in Ephesus. Chapter 2 opens mid-argument — the "and you" of verse 1 connects directly to the power Paul described in chapter 1 that raised Christ from the dead. Paul is now applying resurrection power to human spiritual condition. The passage divides naturally into two movements: verses 1-3 (the diagnosis) and verses 4-10 (the remedy and purpose).

**The Diagnosis — Verses 1-3:**
Paul uses three interlocking descriptions of spiritual death. First, "the course of this world" (*aiōn* — age or era) suggests an invisible cultural current pulling people away from God. Second, "the prince of the power of the air" identifies a personal, malevolent spiritual force — Paul's readers in Ephesus, a center of occult practice, would feel the weight of this immediately. Third, "the lusts of our flesh" locates the problem internally — not just external pressure but internal corruption. The phrase "children of wrath" (*tekna orgēs*) is not about God's hatred of people but about the natural consequence of living in rebellion against the source of life. Wrath in Paul is the moral order of a universe where sin carries consequence.

**The Remedy — Verses 4-7:**
The hinge of the entire passage is two of the most powerful words in Scripture: "But God." Everything before them is the diagnosis; everything after is grace. Paul stacks three "together with Christ" verbs — made alive together (*synezōopoiēsen*), raised together (*synēgeiren*), seated together (*synekathisen*) — each prefixed with *syn* (with/together), emphasizing that believers share in Christ's own resurrection trajectory. The language is past tense, describing a completed spiritual reality. "The surpassing riches of His grace" (v. 7) suggests God's purpose extends beyond the individual — salvation is God's ongoing demonstration to the cosmos of what grace looks like.

**Grace, Faith, and Works — Verses 8-10:**
*Charis* (grace) appears as a gift (*dōron*) that cannot be self-generated. Paul's insistence that faith itself is not "of yourselves" guards against making faith a human contribution that earns salvation. The word *poiēma* in verse 10 — translated "workmanship" or "masterpiece" — is the root of the English word "poem." Believers are God's creative work, his crafted expression. The good works Paul mentions are not the basis of salvation but its destination — "prepared beforehand" suggests God's purposeful design for each believer's life.

**Doctrinal Connections:**
This passage is the biblical foundation for the Baptist conviction that salvation is by grace alone through faith alone. It also grounds the priesthood of all believers — every person remade by grace is equipped for works of ministry, not just ordained ministers. For CBF congregations, verse 10 supports the conviction that mission encompasses both proclamation and embodied service — the good works God prepared are not limited to evangelism but include justice, mercy, and neighbor-love.

**Age-Appropriate Depth Note:**
Mid-life adults can handle the full weight of verses 1-3 without softening. Many have lived long enough to recognize the pull of cultural conformity, spiritual opposition, and internal self-destruction. The passage's honesty about human condition will resonate as true, not harsh.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Object Lesson Preview**

Bring a blank piece of paper and a pen. Before class begins, write a single sentence on the paper and fold it, setting it aside visibly without explanation.

Open by holding up a fresh blank sheet and asking: "What is this worth right now?" Students will say very little — it's just paper. Then ask: "What if I told you something has already been written on this page — a purpose it was made to fulfill — and you just can't see it yet?"

Set it down without explanation. Tell the class you'll come back to it.

*Time estimate: 3 minutes*

**Warm-Up Discussion Prompt**

Ask students: "Think about a moment in your life when something changed you fundamentally — not just a decision you made, but something that happened *to* you that altered who you are. You don't have to share details. Just sit with the memory for a moment."

Then say: "Paul is going to describe the most fundamental change a human being can experience — and he's going to make the case that it happened *to* us before we could do anything about it."

*Time estimate: 3 minutes*

**Transition:** "Open to Ephesians 2, and let's read about what God did."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content

Today we're in Ephesians 2, verses 1 through 10. This is one of those passages where Paul doesn't ease you in gently. He starts with a verdict: you were dead.

Not struggling. Not confused. Not spiritually underperforming. Dead.

Now, Paul isn't being dramatic for effect. He's being precise. Spiritual death in Paul's vocabulary means the same thing physical death means — an inability to respond, to initiate, to help yourself. A dead person doesn't need encouragement. They don't need a better strategy. They need resurrection. And that's exactly what Paul is building toward.

But before he gets to the remedy, he wants us to feel the weight of the diagnosis. He names three forces that ruled us in that condition. The first is "the course of this world" — that's the cultural current, the invisible assumptions of an age that runs contrary to God. Think about how much of what we believe about success, worth, and identity we absorbed without ever consciously choosing it. That's the *aiōn* — the spirit of an era that shapes us before we're even aware it's happening.

The second force is what Paul calls "the prince of the power of the air." For the Ephesians, this wasn't abstract. Ephesus was one of the ancient world's centers of occult practice — the city of the Temple of Artemis, of magic scrolls, of spiritual powers taken very seriously. Paul is telling them: that world you came out of had a ruler, and he was real. For us, the application isn't that we need to be paranoid about spiritual opposition — but we do need to be honest that the resistance to God in our world isn't purely intellectual. There's a personal force behind it.

The third force is the one closest to home: "the lusts of our flesh." The problem isn't just out there in culture or up there in spiritual powers — it's in here. Internal corruption means we weren't just victims of external pressure. We were willing participants. Paul says we "indulged the desires of the flesh and of the mind." That's an uncomfortable word — indulged. It means we liked it. We chose it. The diagnosis is honest about human agency even while describing human bondage.

And then Paul adds "children of wrath." Hear that carefully. This isn't God despising people. Wrath in Paul is the moral logic of a universe where rebellion against the source of life produces death. It's consequence, not contempt. And Paul says "among whom we all formerly lived" — he includes himself. This isn't Paul pointing a finger at the Ephesians from a safe distance. He's standing in the same story.

And then — two words that change everything.

"But God."

Everything before those two words is the diagnosis. Everything after them is grace. Paul stacks three verbs, and every one of them begins with the Greek prefix *syn*, which means "together with." Made alive together with Christ. Raised together with Christ. Seated together with Christ. Paul is saying your spiritual biography is now fused with Christ's biography. His resurrection is your resurrection. His position before the Father is your position. This isn't metaphor — Paul is describing a real transfer of spiritual reality.

Now here's something worth sitting with: Paul says God did this "even when we were dead." Not when we got better. Not when we cleaned ourselves up. When we were dead. Grace doesn't wait for you to become worthy of it. It can't — because dead people can't become worthy of anything. This is why Paul insists twice in verses 8 and 9 that salvation is not from you, not your doing, not something you can take credit for.

And that's not meant to diminish you. It's meant to free you.

Think about what it would mean to carry the weight of earning your own salvation. Every good day would be a deposit; every failure would be a withdrawal. You'd spend your whole life wondering if you'd done enough. Paul is cutting that entire system down at the root. Salvation is a gift. *Charis* — grace — is by definition something you receive, not something you achieve.

But here's where the passage refuses to let grace become passive. Verse 10: "We are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

That word "workmanship" is *poiēma* in Greek — the root of our word "poem." You are God's crafted expression. A poem isn't accidental. It's intentional, structured, purposeful — every word chosen. And the good works Paul describes aren't an afterthought. They were "prepared beforehand." Before you were saved, God was already designing the life your salvation would send you into.

Go back to that folded piece of paper from the beginning of class. Open it now.

The sentence written on it: *"Created in Christ Jesus for good works."*

The paper didn't write that sentence. The paper didn't earn it. But the purpose was there before the paper knew what it was for.

That's you. That's every person in this room.

You were dead. God acted. You were made alive. And now you're walking into works that were prepared for you before you ever took a step.

Here's the challenge: Don't spend this week performing for God's approval. You already have it. Spend it asking what work God prepared for *you* — and take one step toward it.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "But God" Timeline**
*Materials: Index cards, pens | Time: 8–10 minutes | Works for any class size*

Give each student two index cards. On the first card, they write 2–3 words describing what their life looked like spiritually *before* — not necessarily before salvation, but before a moment when God clearly intervened. On the second card, they write 2–3 words describing what changed. Cards stay private unless students choose to share.

After writing, invite 2–3 volunteers to read both cards aloud, pausing between them and saying "But God" in the gap. This dramatizes the hinge Paul builds in verses 4–5. Close by reading verse 4 together aloud.

*Adaptation: Larger classes can share in pairs instead of with the full group.*

---

**Activity 2: Poiēma Role Play**
*Materials: None | Time: 6–8 minutes | Best for groups of 6 or more*

Divide into pairs. One person plays a sculptor who has just finished a piece; the other plays someone who walks in and asks, "Did the clay choose what it would become?" The sculptor explains — without using religious language — what the work was designed to express and why the design came from the artist, not the material.

After 3 minutes, bring the class back together. Ask: "What did that conversation reveal about the relationship between the creator's intent and the created thing's purpose?" Then connect directly to *poiēma* in verse 10.

*Adaptation: Solo reflection journaling works for smaller or quieter groups.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul names three forces ruling spiritually dead people in verses 1–3. Can you name all three? Which one surprised you most when you first encountered it in this passage?

**2.** **Understanding:** Why does Paul include himself in the phrase "among whom we all formerly lived"? What does that choice tell us about how he wants his readers to receive this diagnosis?

**3.** **Analysis:** Paul uses three *syn-* verbs — made alive together, raised together, seated together with Christ. Why does it matter that believers share in Christ's own resurrection trajectory rather than simply receiving a benefit from it?

**4.** **Application:** Verse 10 says the good works were "prepared beforehand." How does that change the way you approach serving others in your congregation — is it more like fulfilling a calling or earning approval? What's the practical difference?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson described grace as something that "doesn't wait for you to become worthy." Where in your life right now is it hardest to actually believe that? What would change if you did?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can distinguish between what saves (grace through faith, entirely God's work) and what salvation produces (good works, prepared beforehand). Strong responses will hold both without collapsing them — neither earning salvation through works nor treating grace as a license for passivity.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students grasping this passage will show reduced performance anxiety in how they talk about their faith, increased curiosity about their specific calling rather than generic obligation, and a willingness to name honestly where they've experienced the pull of the world, spiritual opposition, or internal desire.

**Closing Reflection:** "If the works were prepared before you were saved, what might God have been preparing you for that you haven't stepped into yet?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

***From Death to Life: The Grace That Remakes Us***
*Ephesians 2:1–10*

---

**Key Idea:** God rescued us from spiritual death not because of anything we did, but because of who God is — and that same grace sends us into a purposeful life already designed for us.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8–9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Spiritual death means inability, not just struggle — we couldn't fix ourselves
* Three forces ruled our dead condition: the world's current, a real spiritual adversary, and our own internal desires
* "But God" — two words that split human history into before and after
* God made us alive *together with* Christ — our story is now fused with His resurrection story
* We are God's *poiēma* — His crafted masterpiece, not an accident
* Good works are where grace is going, not how grace is earned

**Gospel Connection:** The gospel of Ephesians 2 is not a self-improvement story — it's a resurrection story. You didn't climb out of spiritual death; God reached in. Jesus' resurrection wasn't just a historical event; it became the pattern for your own spiritual life. Every person who trusts Christ is raised with Him, seated with Him, and sent by Him into works that were designed before they ever took a step. That's not pressure — that's purpose.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where do you most feel the pull of "the course of this world" shaping how you think about your worth or success?
**2.** What would change in your daily life if you genuinely believed your salvation rested entirely on God's action and not on your performance?
**3.** What is one "good work" you sense God may have prepared specifically for you in your congregation or community — and what is one step you could take toward it this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** Choose one act of service this week — not to earn anything, but as someone walking into a work God already prepared. Before you do it, pray: *"This is Yours. I'm just stepping in."* Afterward, notice how it felt different from obligation.

**Prayer:** "Lord, I confess I have lived as though my standing with You depended on my performance. Remind me today that I was dead and You made me alive — not because I deserved it, but because of Your great love. Show me the work You prepared for me. Give me the courage to walk in it without fear and without pride. I receive Your grace. Amen."
