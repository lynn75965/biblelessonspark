## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Cooperative Baptist Fellowship

**Lesson Title:** *The Potter's Freedom and Our Faithful Response: Romans 9:1-24*

**Lesson Summary:** Romans 9:1-24 confronts believers with one of Scripture's most challenging theological tensions — God's sovereign freedom and human responsibility before a holy God. Paul's anguish over Israel's unbelief opens a window into both the depth of genuine love for others and the mystery of divine purpose working through history. This lesson explores how God's freedom to show mercy does not diminish human dignity but rather calls us to deeper trust and humble response.

**Main Theme:** God's sovereign mercy operates on terms of grace, not human merit — and that reality should produce both humility and compassion in us.

**Key Takeaway:** Because God's mercy is freely given and not earned, we are freed from pride and called to extend that same mercy to others.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (ages 36-50) often carry significant life experience with unanswered questions, personal loss, and vocational identity. This passage speaks directly to people wrestling with why things unfold as they do — in their lives, their families, and the world. Expect honest pushback and deep engagement.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Structural Context**

Romans 9-11 functions as a single theological unit within Paul's letter. Chapters 1-8 establish the gospel of grace; chapters 9-11 then address the urgent question that gospel raises: if God's covenant promises are reliable, why has Israel largely rejected Jesus as Messiah? Romans 9:1-24 is Paul's opening argument — God's word has not failed because God's covenant purposes have always operated through sovereign election, not ethnic lineage.

The passage moves through three movements: Paul's personal anguish (vv. 1-5), the principle of elective purpose illustrated through Israel's own history (vv. 6-13), and the defense of God's justice in that freedom (vv. 14-24).

**Historical and Audience Context**

Paul writes to a mixed Jewish-Gentile congregation in Rome, likely in the late 50s AD. The question of Israel's standing before God was not abstract — it was pastoral and urgent. Jewish believers were asking whether their heritage still mattered. Gentile believers risked arrogance. Paul addresses both by anchoring everything in God's character, not human categories.

**Key Theological Concepts**

*Election in Romans 9* is primarily corporate and historical before it is individual. When Paul references Jacob and Esau (vv. 10-13, drawing from Genesis 25:23 and Malachi 1:2-3), he is quoting texts about nations, not merely two men's eternal destinies. "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" uses the Hebrew idiom of comparative preference, not emotional hatred. The CBF tradition rightly holds that soul freedom means individuals respond to God's grace personally — and Paul's argument here is that God's freedom to show mercy does not override human responsibility but precedes it.

*The Pharaoh Illustration (vv. 17-18)* is frequently misread as God arbitrarily damning Pharaoh. Scripture's fuller account (Exodus 4-14) shows Pharaoh hardening his own heart repeatedly before God's hardening is mentioned. Paul's point is that God can work through even human resistance to accomplish redemptive purposes — the Exodus became the defining act of salvation in Israel's memory.

*The Potter and Clay (vv. 20-24)* draws from Isaiah 29:16 and 45:9. The image does not argue that humans are passive objects with no moral agency. Rather, it establishes the creature's impropriety in demanding an accounting from the Creator. The rhetorical question "Who are you, O man, who answers back to God?" is not silencing honest inquiry — Paul himself raises objections throughout Romans. It is confronting the posture of the creature who demands that God justify himself on human terms.

**Key Greek Insight**

The word translated "mercy" (*eleos*, v. 15-16) and "compassion" (*oiktirō*) both appear in Paul's quotation of Exodus 33:19. Together they signal that God's response to human need is not mechanical or obligatory but flows from divine character — freely given, not extracted.

**Doctrinal Connection**

CBF theology affirms that salvation is by grace through faith, that soul freedom means every person stands before God personally, and that God's sovereignty and human responsibility are held together without collapsing either. This passage is not a proof text for rigid determinism; it is a doxological argument that God's mercy cannot be reduced to human systems of fairness.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — "Imagine This" Hook (3-4 minutes)**

Open with this scenario: "Imagine you've worked at a company for twenty years. You've followed every rule, met every standard. Then someone hired last month receives a promotion you've been waiting for — and when you ask why, the answer is simply, 'That was the owner's decision.' How do you feel? What questions rise up immediately?"

Allow 2-3 brief responses. Then say: "That tension — between what seems fair to us and what authority figures decide — is exactly the tension Paul is walking into in Romans 9. Only the stakes are infinitely higher."

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Question (2 minutes)**

Ask: "Has there ever been a moment in your life when you genuinely didn't understand what God was doing — and you had to decide whether to trust anyway?" Invite one or two brief, voluntary responses.

**Transition:** "Paul begins Romans 9 not with an argument but with a broken heart. Let's start there."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Turn in your Bibles to Romans 9, beginning at verse 1. Before we get into the theology — and there is deep theology here — I want you to notice something that's easy to skip past. Paul opens this chapter not with a doctrine but with grief.

He writes: "I am telling the truth in Christ, I am not lying, my conscience testifies with me in the Holy Spirit, that I have great sorrow and unceasing grief in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were accursed, separated from Christ for the sake of my brothers and sisters, my kinsmen according to the flesh" (Romans 9:1-3, NASB).

Three layers of confirmation — truth in Christ, no lying, conscience bearing witness in the Holy Spirit — before he even states what's wrong. Paul is bracing you for something that sounds almost impossible: he would be willing to be cut off from Christ if it meant his people Israel would be saved. That's not rhetorical exaggeration. That echoes Moses in Exodus 32, who asked God to blot his own name from the book of life for Israel's sake. This is what genuine love for people who don't yet know Christ actually looks like. Not frustration. Not condescension. Grief. Unceasing grief. Ask yourself — is there anyone in your life whose lostness genuinely breaks you like that?

Now Paul lists Israel's privileges in verses 4-5 — the adoption, the glory, the covenants, the Law, the temple worship, the promises, the patriarchs, and most significantly, the human lineage of the Messiah himself. These are real gifts. And yet, something has gone wrong. So Paul's question, which he answers through the rest of the chapter, is this: Has God's word failed?

His answer in verse 6 is immediate and firm: "But it is not as though the word of God has failed." And then he makes a move that would have stunned his Jewish readers: "For they are not all Israel who are descended from Israel." Being a physical descendant of Abraham does not automatically place you within the covenant community of promise. He proves this from Israel's own history.

He points to Isaac and Ishmael — both sons of Abraham, but the promise ran through Isaac, not Ishmael. Then he goes further: Jacob and Esau, twin sons of the same mother, same father, same moment of conception — and before either had done anything good or bad, God declared that the older would serve the younger. Paul quotes this from Genesis 25:23, which is an oracle about nations, not just two men. The point is that God's purposes operate through sovereign choice, not human achievement or birth order.

Here's where people get uncomfortable, and I want to address that directly. Paul anticipates the objection in verse 14: "What shall we say then? There is no injustice with God, is there?" And his answer is not a philosophical argument. He quotes God's own words to Moses from Exodus 33:19 — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will show compassion to whom I show compassion." What Paul is saying is that mercy, by definition, cannot be owed. The moment mercy becomes something you earn or deserve, it stops being mercy and becomes a transaction. God's freedom to show mercy is not a threat to human dignity — it is the only basis on which any of us can hope.

Think about it this way. Imagine you owe an enormous debt you cannot repay. The creditor forgives it entirely — not because you negotiated well, not because you earned it, but out of pure generosity. Would you stand up and say, "That's not fair to the people whose debts weren't forgiven"? Maybe. But the more honest response is overwhelming gratitude, because you know you had no claim to that forgiveness in the first place.

Then Paul turns to Pharaoh in verse 17. God says he raised Pharaoh up for this very purpose — to display God's power and spread his name throughout the earth. This sounds harsh until you read the full Exodus account. Pharaoh hardened his own heart repeatedly before God's hardening is ever mentioned. God did not create a man to damn him. God worked through a man's own stubborn resistance to accomplish the most significant act of redemption in Israel's entire history. The Exodus became the story Israel told for generations about who God is and what God does.

The potter and clay image in verses 20-24 is the climax of the argument. Paul quotes Isaiah — the clay does not interrogate the potter about why it was shaped this way. Now, this is not Paul saying "stop asking questions." He's been raising objections himself throughout this letter. What he's confronting is a specific posture — the creature demanding that the Creator justify himself on human terms of fairness. There is a difference between honest wrestling with God, which Scripture is full of, and the demand that God conform to our categories.

Here's the pastoral landing point for all of this. Paul ends this section in verses 23-24 with the language of glory — vessels of mercy prepared for glory, called not only from among Jewish people but also from among Gentiles. The whole argument has been building toward this: God's sovereign freedom in mercy is the very thing that opens salvation to everyone. If God's grace were based on ethnic heritage or moral achievement, most of us would have no hope at all.

So here is the challenge I want to leave with you: The same God who is free to show mercy to you — without your earning it, without your deserving it — is the God who calls you to extend that same posture toward others. The people in your life who seem furthest from God, the ones whose choices baffle or frustrate you — Paul's response to those people was not judgment. It was unceasing grief and a willingness to sacrifice everything for their sake. That is the shape of gospel love.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — Paul's Grief Inventory (8-10 minutes)**

*Materials: Index cards, pens*

Give each student an index card. Ask them to write one name — someone in their life whose distance from God genuinely grieves them. They don't share the name aloud. Then ask them to write one honest sentence describing their actual emotional response to that person's spiritual condition: frustration? indifference? grief? hope?

After two minutes of writing, ask: "Paul described his anguish as *unceasing grief* — strong enough that he'd sacrifice his own standing with Christ. How does your sentence compare to that?" Invite two or three voluntary reflections — not the names, just the emotional honesty.

Close this activity by reading Romans 9:2-3 aloud together. Use the card as a prayer prompt in the next activity.

*Adaptation: In a smaller class, allow fuller sharing. In a larger class, pair students briefly before opening to the group.*

---

**Activity 2 — Potter and Clay Prayer Practice (5-7 minutes)**

*Materials: The index card from Activity 1*

Lead students through a brief structured prayer using three postures drawn directly from the passage:

**Humility** — "Lord, I am the clay. I release my demand to understand every decision you make."
**Gratitude** — "Your mercy toward me was not earned. Thank you."
**Intercession** — Students silently lift the name on their card before God, echoing Paul's posture of grief-turned-prayer.

Allow 90 seconds of silence after each prompt. Close with a spoken sentence of commissioning: "Send us out with Paul's grief and your mercy as our motivation."

*Adaptation: For groups uncomfortable with extended silence, reduce each pause to 30 seconds.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul opens Romans 9 with three layers of confirmation before stating his grief. What are they, and why do you think he stacks them so deliberately before making his point?

**2.** **Understanding:** Paul argues in verse 6 that "they are not all Israel who are descended from Israel." What distinction is he drawing, and how do the examples of Isaac/Ishmael and Jacob/Esau support it?

**3.** **Analysis:** The theological background notes that the Jacob/Esau quotation comes from texts about nations, not just individuals. How does that corporate framing change the way you read Paul's argument about God's freedom?

**4.** **Application:** Paul says mercy, by definition, cannot be owed — the moment it is earned, it becomes a transaction. Where in your own life are you most tempted to treat God's grace as something you've maintained rather than something freely given?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closes with a challenge about extending gospel love to people whose choices baffle or frustrate you. Who is that person for you right now, and what would Paul's posture of "unceasing grief" look like practically in how you engage them — including in your digital and online interactions?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can distinguish between God's sovereign freedom and arbitrary unfairness — specifically, whether they grasp that mercy *by definition* cannot be owed. Students who understand the passage will also connect Paul's grief in verses 1-3 to the application rather than treating it as mere introduction.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Watch for students who move from intellectual engagement with the theology to honest self-examination about their own posture — whether they hold grace as gift or achievement, and whether their response to spiritually distant people resembles Paul's grief or something lesser.

**Closing Reflection:** "If the mercy God showed you was entirely unearned — what would it look like to live this week as someone who genuinely knows that?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**The Potter's Freedom and Our Faithful Response: Romans 9:1-24**

**Key Idea:** God's mercy is freely given, not earned — and that truth should produce humility in us and compassion toward others.

**Memory Verse:** "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will show compassion to whom I show compassion." — Romans 9:15 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Paul opens with grief, not argument — his anguish over Israel's unbelief shows what genuine love for spiritually distant people actually looks like.
* God's covenant purposes have always worked through sovereign choice, not human achievement or birth order — the examples of Isaac and Jacob prove this from Israel's own history.
* Mercy cannot be owed. The moment grace becomes something you earn, it is no longer grace — it is a transaction.
* The potter/clay image does not silence honest questions; it confronts the posture of demanding that God justify himself on our terms of fairness.
* God's freedom to show mercy is the very thing that opens salvation to everyone — including us.

**Gospel Connection:** The same freedom that allowed God to show mercy to Pharaoh's Egypt through the Exodus is the freedom that opened the door of salvation to every person — Jew and Gentile alike. None of us arrived at grace through heritage or achievement. We were called. That calling carries a responsibility: to extend toward others the same unearned mercy we ourselves have received. The gospel does not make us gatekeepers; it makes us witnesses.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Is there someone in your life whose spiritual condition genuinely grieves you — or have you drifted toward frustration or indifference? What would Paul's posture look like toward that person?
**2.** Where are you most tempted to treat your standing with God as something you've maintained rather than something freely given?
**3.** How might your online and digital interactions with people you disagree with look different if they were shaped by Paul's grief rather than judgment?

**Weekly Challenge:** Choose one person whose distance from God weighs on you. This week, replace one moment of frustration toward them with a specific, honest prayer — echoing Paul's posture of grief and intercession. Let mercy received become mercy extended.

**Prayer:** "Lord, I confess I sometimes hold your grace as though I earned it. Remind me today that mercy, by definition, cannot be owed — and that I stand before you only because of your freedom to give it. Shape me like the potter shapes clay: with purpose, with patience, and with a glory I cannot yet fully see. Give me Paul's grief for the people around me who don't yet know you. Send me toward them not with judgment but with love. Amen."
