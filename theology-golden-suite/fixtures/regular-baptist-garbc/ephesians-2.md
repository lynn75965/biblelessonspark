## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Regular Baptist (GARBC) — Dispensational, Cessationist, Inerrantist

**Lesson Title:** *Dead Men Walking: Grace That Raises the Spiritually Dead*

**Lesson Summary:** Ephesians 2:1-10 presents the starkest possible contrast in all of Scripture — the total spiritual deadness of humanity apart from Christ and the sovereign, transforming grace that raises dead sinners to new life. Paul's argument moves from ruin to redemption, grounding salvation entirely in God's initiative, not human effort. Students will see that the gospel is not self-improvement but resurrection.

**Main Theme:** Salvation is entirely God's work — from diagnosing our spiritual death to raising us by grace through faith — so that no human being can claim credit for it.

**Key Takeaway:** Because you were spiritually dead and God made you alive, every dimension of your salvation is a gift to receive, not an achievement to earn.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36-50) often carry either religious performance fatigue or a settled-but-shallow understanding of grace. This passage cuts through both. These students are capable of wrestling with theological depth and connecting doctrine to how they actually live, parent, work, and engage their communities.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Paul wrote Ephesians from Roman imprisonment, likely around A.D. 60-62. Ephesus was a major commercial and religious hub dominated by the cult of Artemis and saturated with occult practice (Acts 19 records extraordinary spiritual warfare there). Paul's readers understood spiritual darkness firsthand. Ephesians 1-3 forms the doctrinal foundation of the letter; chapters 4-6 build the practical application on that foundation. Chapter 2 opens mid-argument — the "and you" of verse 1 connects directly to the power Paul described in 1:19-20, the same resurrection power that raised Christ now applied to dead sinners.

**The Three Enemies: World, Flesh, Devil**

Verses 1-3 diagnose spiritual death with surgical precision. "The course of this world" (v. 2) translates *aiōn* — the present age with its value system opposed to God. "The prince of the power of the air" identifies Satan as a real, personal being who exercises domain over those outside of Christ (cf. John 12:31; 2 Cor. 4:4). "The flesh" (v. 3) refers not merely to the physical body but to fallen human nature — the entire orientation of the unregenerate person toward self and sin. These three are not equal forces; Satan is a created being operating under God's sovereign permission. But together they describe total spiritual bondage — not sickness requiring a remedy, but death requiring resurrection.

**"Dead in Trespasses and Sins"**

The Greek *nekrous* is the same word used for a physical corpse. Paul is not describing spiritual weakness or moral struggle — he is describing the absence of spiritual life. A corpse cannot respond, choose, or cooperate with its own resurrection. This is the Baptist and broadly evangelical understanding of total depravity: not that sinners are as evil as possible, but that every faculty — mind, will, affections — is corrupted and incapable of turning toward God without divine intervention.

**"But God" — The Hinge of History**

Verse 4 is one of the most theologically loaded pivots in all of Paul's writing. "But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us" — the cause of salvation is located entirely in God's character, not human potential. *Eleos* (mercy) addresses our misery; *agapē* (love) addresses our unworthiness. God did not respond to something good in us — He acted from His own nature.

**Grace, Faith, and Works (vv. 8-9)**

"For by grace you have been saved through faith" — the perfect passive *sesōsmenoi* indicates a completed action with ongoing results. Salvation happened and its effects continue. "That" in verse 8 (Greek *touto*) refers to the entire salvation event, not to faith alone — the whole package is the gift of God. Verse 9 closes the door on any human boasting. Verse 10 immediately redirects: we are God's *poiēma* (workmanship, masterpiece) — a word used for a crafted work of art — "created in Christ Jesus for good works." Works are the fruit and purpose of salvation, never its ground.

**Doctrinal Connections**

This passage anchors eternal security: those God made alive, He made alive completely. It also grounds missional living — the "good works, which God prepared beforehand" are not accidental but purposeful, connecting personal salvation to outward engagement in the world.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — The Rescue Story (5 minutes)**

Open with this scenario: "Picture a rescue diver pulling someone from a submerged vehicle. The person inside is unconscious — not struggling, not signaling for help, not cooperating. The diver breaks the window, unbuckles the seatbelt, and pulls them to the surface alone. Now — who gets the credit for that rescue?"

Let students respond briefly. Then ask: "What if the unconscious person later said, 'I really helped make that happen'?" Let the absurdity land.

Say: "Paul's description of your spiritual condition before Christ is not 'struggling swimmer.' It's 'unconscious in a sinking car.' That changes everything about how we understand salvation."

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Question (3 minutes)**

Ask students: "Before you became a Christian, what did you think you needed to do to get right with God?"

Allow 2-3 brief responses. Don't correct yet — just collect answers.

**Transition:** "Paul addresses every one of those answers in Ephesians 2. Let's read what he actually says."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Ephesians 2, beginning at verse 1. We're going to read the whole passage together, and then I want to walk through it carefully, because there is more packed into these ten verses than most people realize.

> "And you were dead in your trespasses and sins, in which you formerly walked according to the course of this world, according to the prince of the power of the air, of the spirit that is now working in the sons of disobedience. Among them we too all formerly lived in the lusts of our flesh, indulging the desires of the flesh and of the mind, and were by nature children of wrath, even as the rest."
> — Ephesians 2:1-3 (NASB)

Notice Paul doesn't say "you were sick" or "you were struggling." He says *dead*. That's the Greek word for a corpse. And that matters enormously, because we tend to think of the spiritual problem as a self-improvement problem — like we just need better habits, better intentions, a little more willpower. Paul says no. The problem is death, and dead people don't improve themselves.

Now look at the three sources of that death. First, "the course of this world" — this is the surrounding culture's value system, its assumptions about what matters, what success looks like, what you owe yourself. Every generation has a version of this, and it runs directly against the kingdom of God. Second, "the prince of the power of the air" — that's Satan. Paul is not being poetic here. He's identifying a real, personal being who exercises influence over those outside of Christ. Satan is not God's equal — he's a created being operating under God's sovereign permission — but his influence over unregenerate humanity is real and serious. Third, "the lusts of our flesh" — fallen human nature, the entire inward orientation of the person before regeneration, bent toward self and away from God.

Here's what's important: these three don't cancel each other out or give you an excuse. They describe total bondage. You were not mostly free with some outside interference. You were dead, and the world, the devil, and your own nature were all working in the same direction.

Now look at verse 4. Two of the most important words in the Bible: *But God.*

"But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us, even when we were dead in our transgressions, made us alive together with Christ."

The cause of your salvation is not located in you. It's located in God's character — His mercy and His love. Mercy addresses your misery. Love addresses your unworthiness. God didn't look at you and find something worth saving. He looked at you and acted from who He is. That's a completely different foundation for your spiritual life.

Think about what it means that He "made us alive together with Christ." Your spiritual resurrection is connected to Christ's literal resurrection. The same power Paul described in chapter 1 — the power that raised Jesus from the dead — is the power that raised you from spiritual death. This is not a metaphor for feeling better about yourself. This is a new creation event.

Now verses 8 and 9 — and I want you to really hear this:

"For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast."

The verb "have been saved" is a Greek perfect tense — it describes something that happened and whose effects are still ongoing. You *have been* saved and you *remain* saved. That's not arrogance; that's what Paul says. And the phrase "that not of yourselves" — the word "that" points back to the entire salvation event. The grace, the faith, the whole package — it's the gift of God. You didn't generate the faith that saved you. God granted it. That's why no one can boast.

Now here's where some people get nervous. If salvation is entirely God's work, does that mean we just sit back and do nothing? Paul answers that immediately in verse 10: "For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

The word "workmanship" is the Greek *poiēma* — it's where we get our word "poem." It means a crafted masterpiece. You are not a rough draft. You are not a work in progress that God is hoping will eventually turn out okay. You are a finished creation — recreated in Christ for a specific purpose.

And that purpose is good works. Not to earn salvation — that's already settled. But because salvation has a direction. It points outward. God prepared these works beforehand, which means your engagement in your community, your neighborhood, your workplace — the ways you serve and love and speak truth — these are not accidental. They were woven into God's design for you before you ever walked in them.

So here's the challenge I want to leave with you this week: identify one specific place in your civic or community life — a school board, a neighborhood association, a volunteer role, a conversation you've been avoiding — where the fact that God made you alive calls you to show up and act. Not to earn anything. Not to impress anyone. But because you are His workmanship, and the works were prepared. Walk in them.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — "But God" Timeline (10 minutes)**
*Case Study Analysis | Materials: Paper or index cards, pens*

Give each student an index card. Ask them to draw a simple horizontal line and label the left end "Before Christ" and the right end "In Christ." Then instruct them to write 2-3 words on the left side describing their spiritual condition before salvation (using Paul's categories: dead, enslaved, children of wrath) and 2-3 words on the right describing what God made them (alive, His workmanship, seated with Christ).

In the middle of the line, have them write "BUT GOD."

Pair students and let them share their cards for 3 minutes. Then ask one or two volunteers to share with the class. Reinforce: the hinge is always God's initiative, never human effort.

*Adaptation: Smaller classes can share with the full group; larger classes keep it in pairs.*

---

**Activity 2 — Prepared Works Mapping (8 minutes)**
*Civic/Community Application | Materials: None required*

Ask students to silently consider this question for 90 seconds: "Where in your community — a neighborhood, school, civic organization, workplace, or local need — have you sensed a pull to engage but haven't acted?"

Then open briefly for 3-4 volunteers to share. After each response, ask the class: "How does being God's *poiēma* — His crafted masterpiece — change how you approach that opportunity?"

*Adaptation: In smaller classes, everyone shares. In larger classes, discuss in groups of three.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses three sources to describe spiritual death in Ephesians 2:1-3. Can you name all three and briefly describe what each one means?

**2.** **Understanding:** Why does it matter that Paul calls unregenerate people *dead* rather than *sick* or *struggling*? How does that word change what kind of solution is needed?

**3.** **Analysis:** In verse 8, Paul says salvation is "not of yourselves" and "not as a result of works." Some people worry this removes human responsibility entirely. How does verse 10 answer that concern without contradicting verses 8-9?

**4.** **Application:** The Greek word *poiēma* describes believers as God's crafted masterpiece. How should that identity reshape the way a mid-life adult thinks about their role in their neighborhood, workplace, or community — especially in areas where they feel unqualified or overlooked?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closed with a challenge to identify one specific civic or community engagement where your salvation calls you to act. What came to mind, and what has held you back from walking in it?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can distinguish spiritual death from moral weakness, and whether they can articulate that good works in verse 10 flow *from* salvation rather than contributing *to* it. Confusion on this point often reveals a performance-based understanding of grace that the passage directly corrects.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged this passage will speak about their community involvement with a sense of calling and purpose rather than obligation or guilt. Watch for language that reflects identity ("I am His workmanship") rather than transaction ("I need to do more").

**Closing Reflection:** "If God prepared your good works beforehand, what does it mean that you haven't walked in some of them yet — and what would it look like to start this week?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Dead Men Walking: Grace That Raises the Spiritually Dead**

**Key Idea:** You were not a struggling sinner who needed help — you were spiritually dead. God didn't improve you; He raised you. That changes everything about how you understand salvation and how you live it out.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8-9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Before Christ, every person is spiritually dead — not sick, not struggling, but dead — enslaved to the world's value system, to Satan's influence, and to fallen human nature.
* "But God" (v. 4) is the hinge of history. God acted from His own mercy and love, not because He found something worth saving in us.
* Salvation is a completed event with ongoing effects. The Greek tense of "have been saved" means it happened and it holds — you remain saved.
* You are God's *poiēma* — His crafted masterpiece — recreated in Christ Jesus for good works He designed specifically for you.
* Those good works point outward: into your neighborhood, your workplace, your civic community. They were prepared. Walk in them.

**Gospel Connection:** The resurrection of Lazarus in John 11 gives a vivid picture of what Ephesians 2 describes. Jesus called a dead man out of a tomb — Lazarus contributed nothing to his own resurrection. In the same way, God called you out of spiritual death through Christ. Your salvation rests entirely on His power and grace, secured by Christ's death and resurrection, received through faith alone. Because Christ rose, you were made alive — and that life has a direction.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Which of the three sources of spiritual death — the world, the devil, or the flesh — do you see most actively working against people in your community right now?
**2.** How does knowing you are God's *poiēma* (masterpiece) change the way you see your daily responsibilities and relationships?
**3.** What is one specific "prepared work" — a civic role, a relationship, a community need — that God may be calling you to walk in this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, take one concrete step into the community engagement you identified during class. It doesn't have to be large — show up to a meeting, make a phone call, have the conversation you've been postponing. Do it not to earn anything, but because you are His workmanship and the works were prepared before you were born.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that You did not wait for me to find my way to You. When I was dead, You made me alive. When I was enslaved, You set me free. Remind me this week that I am Your masterpiece — created for works You already planned. Give me the courage to walk in them, for Your glory and not my own. Amen."
