## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Regular Baptist (GARBC) — Dispensational Premillennial, Biblically Inerrant, Eternally Secure

**Lesson Title:** *Dead Men Walking: The Miracle of Grace in Ephesians 2:1-10*

**Lesson Summary:** Ephesians 2:1-10 presents one of Scripture's most complete portraits of salvation — from the utter hopelessness of spiritual death to the breathtaking reality of new life in Christ. Paul moves from humanity's darkest condition to God's richest mercy, anchoring everything in divine grace rather than human effort. Students will see that salvation is entirely God's initiative, entirely God's accomplishment, and entirely God's glory.

**Main Theme:** God rescues spiritually dead sinners through grace alone, raising them to new life in Christ for His glory and their good works.

**Key Takeaway:** Because salvation originates entirely in God's grace and not human effort, believers can live with unshakable confidence and humble gratitude.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36-50) often carry years of religious activity, personal achievement, and self-sufficiency. This passage confronts the assumption that spiritual standing is earned and invites a fresh encounter with grace that frees rather than flatters.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Paul wrote Ephesians from Roman imprisonment, likely around AD 60-62. The letter is circular in nature — tradition holds it circulated among several churches in the Lycus Valley region, though the Ephesian church was its primary recipient. Unlike Paul's more combative letters (Galatians, Corinthians), Ephesians is architecturally structured: chapters 1-3 establish doctrinal foundations; chapters 4-6 build practical application on top. Ephesians 2:1-10 sits at the structural heart of the doctrinal section, following Paul's doxological prayer in chapter 1 and grounding the believer's identity in the historical act of salvation.

**The Condition: Spiritual Death (vv. 1-3)**

The Greek word *nekros* (dead) is not metaphorical softening — it describes the complete inability of the unregenerate person to respond to God. Paul identifies three overlapping dimensions of this death: walking according to "the course of this world" (the age-system of fallen culture), following "the prince of the power of the air" (Satan's domain over unregenerate humanity), and living according to "the lusts of our flesh" (internal moral corruption). The phrase "sons of disobedience" is a Hebraic idiom meaning those whose defining characteristic is rebellion. Critically, Paul includes himself — "we too all formerly lived" — eliminating any notion of a spiritually neutral class of people. Total depravity means every faculty of the unregenerate person is corrupted, not that every person commits every sin, but that no part of the person escapes sin's corrupting reach.

**The Intervention: God's Mercy and Grace (vv. 4-7)**

Verse 4 opens with one of Scripture's most dramatic pivots: "But God." Against the backdrop of complete spiritual death, God acts — not because He found something worthy in the sinner, but because of His own "great love" and "rich mercy." The Greek *eleos* (mercy) addresses misery; *charis* (grace) addresses demerit. God responds to both simultaneously. The aorist tense verbs "made alive," "raised up," and "seated" describe completed actions — Paul uses the past tense because the believer's positional reality in Christ is already accomplished, even while physical resurrection awaits. This "already/not yet" tension is standard Pauline eschatology. The phrase "in the heavenly places" (used five times in Ephesians) refers to the spiritual realm where Christ reigns and where believers are positionally co-seated with Him.

**The Mechanism: Grace Through Faith (vv. 8-9)**

"For by grace you have been saved through faith" — the perfect passive *sesōsmenoi* indicates a past action with ongoing present results. Grace is the sphere or means; faith is the channel. Paul adds "and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God" — the antecedent of "that" (*touto*) is the entire salvation package, not faith alone, though the point is that nothing in the transaction originates in the sinner. Works are explicitly excluded "so that no one may boast." This directly addresses the human tendency — especially pronounced in achievement-oriented adults — to find spiritual standing through religious performance.

**The Purpose: Created for Good Works (v. 10)**

The Greek *poiēma* (workmanship) is the word from which we derive "poem" — believers are God's crafted masterpiece. Good works are the purpose of salvation, never its ground. They are prepared beforehand by God, meaning they are part of His sovereign design for each believer's life. This prevents both antinomianism (grace means no responsibility) and legalism (works earn standing).

**Doctrinal Connections**

This passage is the biblical anchor for total depravity, sovereign grace, and eternal security. The security of the believer rests not on the strength of the believer's faith but on the power of the God who raised and seated them.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Hook Question (5 minutes)**

Open with this question — don't answer it yet, just let it sit: *"If God graded your spiritual life the way an employer grades a performance review, what score would you give yourself — and how confident are you in that number?"*

Let two or three students respond briefly. Most mid-life adults will either inflate their score out of habit or feel quietly uncertain. Either response sets up the passage perfectly.

**Warm-Up Discussion Prompt (3 minutes)**

Ask: *"Think about a time you received something you knew you didn't deserve — not earned, not merited, just given. What did that feel like?"*

This activates the emotional register of grace before the theological explanation arrives.

**Transition into Teaching**

Say: "What we just described in our own small experiences is a shadow of what Paul is about to show us in Ephesians 2. But before grace arrives in this passage, Paul takes us somewhere uncomfortable — and we need to go there first."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Ephesians 2, beginning at verse 1. I want to read the entire passage before we unpack it, because it's the kind of text that needs to be heard whole before it's examined in pieces.

Read Ephesians 2:1-10 aloud, slowly and deliberately.

Now — before we go any further — I want you to notice something about the structure of what Paul just said. Verses 1 through 3 describe what we were. Verses 4 through 9 describe what God did. Verse 10 describes what we're now for. That's the whole movement: our condition, God's intervention, our purpose. Keep that frame in mind.

**What We Were: Spiritually Dead**

Paul says in verse 1: "You were dead in your trespasses and sins." Not sick. Not confused. Not spiritually underperforming. Dead. That word in Greek — *nekros* — is the same word used for a physical corpse. And a corpse cannot contribute to its own resurrection. That's the point.

Now, some of us grew up in church. We've been in Bible study for decades. And it's easy, over time, to quietly rewrite our spiritual biography — to think of ourselves as people who made a good decision for God, who chose wisely, who turned our lives around. Paul is dismantling that narrative here. He says we were walking according to the course of this world, following the prince of the power of the air — that's Satan's domain over unregenerate humanity — and living out the desires of our flesh and mind. Notice he says "we all" in verse 3. Paul includes himself. There is no spiritually neutral category of human being. Every person outside of Christ is, by nature, a child of wrath.

What does "child of wrath" mean? It means that God's just judgment against sin is not an overreaction — it is the appropriate response to what we actually were. This is not God being harsh. This is God being honest about the moral reality of the human condition.

Here's why this matters for mid-life adults specifically: by our 40s, most of us have built something. A career. A family. A reputation. A religious track record. And we can subtly start measuring our spiritual standing by those achievements. Paul says: none of that changes what you were before God acted. The ground is level at the foot of the cross.

**What God Did: Acted Out of Mercy and Grace**

Now we get to verse 4, and I want you to feel the weight of those two words: "But God." Everything changes in two words. Against the backdrop of complete spiritual death and just condemnation, God acts. Not because He found something admirable in us. Not because we showed potential. Verse 4 says it was because of "His great love" and "rich mercy."

Here's a distinction worth holding onto: mercy and grace are not the same thing. Mercy addresses misery — God sees our desperate condition and responds with compassion. Grace addresses demerit — God gives what we don't deserve and couldn't earn. We needed both, and God provided both simultaneously.

Paul says God "made us alive together with Christ," "raised us up with Him," and "seated us with Him in the heavenly places." Those are past-tense verbs describing a completed reality. Right now, your position in Christ is already secured. You are already — positionally — seated with Christ in the heavenly places. That's not a future hope; that's a present spiritual reality. The physical resurrection is still ahead, but the verdict has already been rendered.

**How It Works: Grace Through Faith, Not Works**

Verses 8 and 9 are among the most memorized in all of Scripture, but familiarity can blunt their force. "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast."

Think about what Paul is ruling out. He's not just saying works are insufficient — he's saying they're irrelevant to the transaction. Grace is the sphere in which salvation exists. Faith is the channel through which we receive it. And even the entire package — salvation itself — is described as God's gift, not our achievement.

Why does this matter? Because the human heart is wired for merit. We instinctively want to contribute something. We want to feel like we earned at least part of it. But Paul says that impulse, if followed into your theology of salvation, will destroy your assurance. Because if your standing before God depends even partially on your performance, then your standing is only as secure as your last good week. That's not good news — that's a treadmill.

Eternal security isn't a Baptist quirk. It flows directly from this text. If salvation is entirely God's gift, entirely God's work, grounded in Christ's finished atonement — then its security rests on God's faithfulness, not yours.

**What You're For: God's Masterpiece**

Verse 10 is the payoff: "For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

The Greek word for workmanship is *poiēma* — it's where we get the English word "poem." You are God's crafted work. Not a rough draft. Not a project still in process of earning approval. A finished creative act of God.

But here's the crucial sequence: good works follow salvation; they don't produce it. God prepared those works beforehand — they are part of His design for your life. So the question isn't "How do I earn God's favor through service?" The question is "What has God already prepared for me to walk in?" That's a completely different posture. One is striving; the other is discovery.

Here's your challenge going forward: identify one good work — one act of service, one expression of love in your church community — that you've been postponing because you've been too busy building your own life. God prepared it for you. It's time to walk in it.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: "But God" Timeline (10 minutes)**
*Materials: Index cards, pens*

Give each student two index cards. On the first, they write a brief, honest description of their spiritual condition before salvation — using Paul's categories from Ephesians 2:1-3 as prompts (the world's pull, spiritual opposition, internal desires). On the second, they write what God did — using verses 4-9 as a framework. Students share one card with a partner, not both. The exercise makes the "But God" pivot personal rather than abstract.

*Adaptation: Smaller classes share both cards aloud. Larger classes keep both cards private and use them as personal prayer prompts.*

---

**Activity 2: Service Project Planning — "Walk in Them" (15 minutes)**
*Materials: Whiteboard or large paper, markers*

As a class, brainstorm specific good works — acts of service within your church community — that have been collectively delayed or overlooked. Reference verse 10: God "prepared beforehand" these works. List them visibly. Then vote on one the class will commit to planning together before the next gathering. Assign a point person.

This directly applies the lesson's closing challenge: moving from theological understanding to concrete obedience in the church community.

*Adaptation: Online classes use a shared document or chat responses. Solo learners write a personal list and circle one.*

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses three phrases in verses 1-3 to describe spiritual death. Can you name them, and what does each one tell us about the unregenerate person's condition?

**2.** **Understanding:** Why does Paul include himself in the phrase "we too all formerly lived" (v. 3)? What theological point does his self-inclusion make?

**3.** **Analysis:** Verse 4 opens with "But God." What makes those two words such a dramatic pivot in the passage? What specifically had to be true about God — not about us — for that pivot to happen?

**4.** **Application:** If salvation is entirely God's gift and works are explicitly excluded from justification (vv. 8-9), how should that change the way a mid-life adult measures their spiritual standing before God?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Verse 10 says good works were "prepared beforehand" for you to walk in. What is one specific work in your church community you believe God has prepared for you — and what has kept you from walking in it?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can distinguish between mercy (addressing misery) and grace (addressing demerit), and who understand that the past-tense verbs in verses 5-6 describe a completed positional reality, not merely a future hope. Students who conflate works with the ground of salvation — rather than its purpose — need gentle redirection to the sequence in verse 10.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged this passage will speak about their salvation with confidence grounded in God's action rather than their performance. Watch for a shift from "I decided" language to "God acted" language. Willingness to name a specific good work in the church community — rather than speaking in generalities — signals that verse 10 has landed personally.

**Closing Reflection:** "If your spiritual standing rests entirely on what God has already done in Christ — not on what you did last week or will do next week — what would it feel like to actually live from that reality rather than toward it?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Dead Men Walking: The Miracle of Grace in Ephesians 2:1-10**

**Key Idea:** Salvation is entirely God's initiative, entirely God's accomplishment, and entirely for God's glory. You brought nothing to the transaction — and that is the best news you will ever hear.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8-9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Before God acted, every person is spiritually dead — not sick, not struggling, but dead. No part of us escapes sin's reach (vv. 1-3).
* "But God" — two words that change everything. God acted out of His own mercy and grace, not because He found something worthy in us (v. 4).
* Your position in Christ is already secured. "Made alive," "raised up," and "seated with Him" are completed realities, not future possibilities (vv. 5-6).
* Grace is the sphere; faith is the channel; the entire package is God's gift. Works are excluded from justification so that no one can boast (vv. 8-9).
* You are God's *poiēma* — His crafted masterpiece — created for good works He already prepared for you to walk in (v. 10).

**Gospel Connection:** The gospel does not call spiritually sick people to try harder. It calls spiritually dead people to receive life from the only One who can give it. Christ's substitutionary death fully satisfied God's just demands against sin, and His resurrection secured the new life Paul describes here. Faith in Him — not religious achievement, not moral track record — is the only condition of salvation. If you have never trusted Christ alone for salvation, today is the day to receive what God has already prepared.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where have you been quietly measuring your spiritual standing by your own performance rather than by what God has already done in Christ?
**2.** What does it mean practically that your salvation is as secure as God's faithfulness — not as secure as your last good week?
**3.** What specific good work in your church community do you believe God has prepared for you, and what is your next step toward walking in it?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, stop postponing one act of service or love in your church community. Verse 10 says God prepared it beforehand — which means it is already yours to walk in, not yours to earn. Identify it, name it to someone who will hold you accountable, and take the first step before the week ends.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that my salvation does not rest on my performance but on Your grace. Forgive me for the times I have tried to earn what You freely gave. Help me to live from the finished work of Christ — not toward it — with confidence and humility. Show me the good works You prepared for me, and give me the courage to walk in them for Your glory. Amen."
