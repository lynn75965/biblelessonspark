## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews is a first-century sermon-letter addressed to Jewish Christians — likely in Rome — facing intense pressure to abandon Christianity and return to Judaism. The author's pastoral concern throughout is that some in the community may be intellectually convinced of Christ's superiority without being genuinely regenerated. Hebrews 6:1-12 sits immediately after 5:11-14, where the author rebukes his readers for remaining spiritual infants when they should be teachers. Chapter 6 is the direct application of that rebuke.

**The Elementary Teachings (vv. 1-3)**

The six foundational items — repentance, faith, baptisms (plural, likely referring to the Jewish purification washings versus Christian baptism), laying on of hands, resurrection, and eternal judgment — are not dismissed as unimportant. The author's point is that a mature believer does not need to keep re-laying the foundation. These are starting points, not endpoints. The Greek verb *pherometha* ("let us press on") is passive — carried forward, suggesting movement enabled by God's power, not mere human resolve.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4-6) — Interpretive Options**

This is one of the most debated texts in the New Testament. Three main interpretive positions deserve the teacher's awareness:

1. *Loss of salvation* — These are genuinely saved people who fall away permanently. This view conflicts with John 10:28-29, Romans 8:38-39, and the author's own assurance in verses 9-12.

2. *Hypothetical warning* — The author describes a hypothetical impossibility to make a logical point: IF someone could fall away after such experiences, restoration would be impossible. The very impossibility proves the point.

3. *Descriptive of near-believers who never regenerated* — This is the strongest view consistent with eternal security. The language of verses 4-5 describes genuine spiritual privileges — tasting heavenly gifts, being enlightened, sharing in the Holy Spirit's convicting work — without the vocabulary of regeneration. Scripture uses specific language for the new birth (*born again*, *new creation*, *sealed*). Notably absent here. These individuals experienced the Spirit's work around them without being transformed by it. Matthew 13's rocky-soil hearer and 1 John 2:19 support this reading.

**Key Greek Insights**

*Parapiptō* (v. 6, "fallen away") appears only here in the New Testament — a decisive departure, not a stumble. *Anastaurou̅ntas* ("crucifying again") is a present participle indicating ongoing, willful rejection — not a single failure but a settled posture of repudiation. The agricultural metaphor in verses 7-8 reinforces this: land that receives rain but produces only thorns is *near* to being cursed — it bears the marks of exposure without the fruit of transformation.

**Pastoral Assurance (vv. 9-12)**

The author's pivot in verse 9 is striking: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." The "better things" are the things that accompany salvation — *sōtēria*. He points to their labor of love and ministry to the saints as evidence of genuine faith. This is not works-based assurance; it is fruit-based confirmation. The call to "show the same diligence" (v. 11) is a call to perseverance in faith, not a threat that salvation hangs in the balance.

**Doctrinal Connection**

GARBC theology affirms eternal security while taking seriously the biblical call to self-examination. These are not in tension. The warning passages in Hebrews function as God's means of preserving His people — the Spirit uses them to stir genuine believers toward diligence and to expose those whose faith was never saving.

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Hebrews 6, and let's start by reading verses 1 through 12 together. This passage has a reputation for being difficult — and honestly, that reputation is earned. But difficult doesn't mean unclear. It means we have to read carefully and let the whole passage speak, not just the parts that make us nervous.

Here's the big picture before we dive in: The author of Hebrews is writing to Jewish Christians who are under enormous pressure to walk away from Christ and return to Judaism. Some of them are genuinely saved. Some of them may have been deeply exposed to the gospel, convinced intellectually, even moved emotionally — without ever being truly born again. The author knows this, and he writes to both groups at once. That's why this passage feels like it has two different tones — because it does.

**The Call to Move Forward (vv. 1-3)**

Look at verse 1: "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about Christ, let us press on to maturity." Notice what he doesn't say. He doesn't say "abandon the foundation." He says leave it behind — meaning, don't keep rebuilding it. A builder doesn't keep re-pouring the foundation every morning. He builds on it.

The six foundational items he lists — repentance, faith, baptisms, laying on of hands, resurrection, eternal judgment — these are the ABCs. They're essential, but they're starting points. The Greek word translated "press on" is passive — it carries the idea of being carried forward, moved along by a power outside yourself. Spiritual maturity isn't something you manufacture through sheer willpower. It's something you cooperate with as God works in you. But cooperation still requires movement. The question this raises for us is honest and uncomfortable: Are we building on the foundation, or are we still arguing about whether to pour it?

**The Warning That Stops Everyone (vv. 4-6)**

Now we get to the hard part. Verses 4 through 6 describe people who were "once enlightened," who "tasted of the heavenly gift," who "were made partakers of the Holy Spirit," who "tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come" — and then fell away. And the author says it is impossible to renew them to repentance.

This terrifies people. And it should make us think carefully. But here's what you need to see: the author never uses the vocabulary of regeneration for these people. He never says they were born again, sealed by the Spirit, made new creations, or given eternal life. He describes people who had genuine, powerful spiritual exposure — who experienced the Spirit's convicting work, who tasted real spiritual realities — without being transformed by them.

Think about it this way. Imagine someone who grew up in a Christian home, sat under faithful preaching for decades, felt genuine emotion during worship, even served in ministry — but never surrendered to Christ as Lord and Savior. They experienced everything around saving faith without the saving faith itself. That's the person the author has in view.

The Greek word for "fallen away" — *parapiptō* — appears only here in the entire New Testament. It describes a decisive, deliberate departure. And the present participle "crucifying again" describes not a single failure but an ongoing, settled posture of rejection — treating Christ as a fraud, publicly repudiating Him. This is not a believer having a bad year. This is someone who, having been fully exposed to the truth, makes a final, willful decision to walk away and keep walking.

And here's why restoration is impossible for such a person: not because God's grace runs out, but because they've hardened themselves against the very means by which God brings people to repentance. You can't be brought back through the gospel if you've made the gospel itself the object of your contempt.

The agricultural picture in verses 7 and 8 makes this concrete. Land that receives rain — that's the exposure to God's truth and the Spirit's work — and produces a crop is blessed. Land that receives the same rain and produces only thorns is near to being cursed. The rain is identical. The difference is what's underneath. The soil reveals what it is by what it produces.

**The Assurance That Anchors (vv. 9-12)**

Now watch the pivot in verse 9. The author says, "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation." He's essentially saying: I described that dangerous category — but I don't believe you're in it. Why? Because of what he sees in verses 10 and 11: their labor of love, their ministry to the saints, their service in the name of Christ.

This is not the author saying, "You're earning your salvation by your works." He's saying that genuine saving faith produces visible fruit, and that fruit is evidence — not cause — of salvation. This is the same logic John uses in 1 John: we examine ourselves not to earn assurance but to confirm what God has already done.

The call in verse 11 to "show the same diligence" is a call to keep going, to not plateau, to not coast. And verse 12 gives us the model: "those who through faith and patience inherit the promises." Faith. Patience. Both. Not a sprint of emotional intensity followed by years of spiritual drift. Sustained, forward-moving trust in God.

Here's the challenge I want to leave you with today. The author isn't trying to make you doubt your salvation — he's trying to wake up anyone who's been coasting on religious exposure without genuine transformation. If you are in Christ, the evidence is forward movement. Not perfection. Not constant emotional highs. But a life that, over time, looks more like Jesus than it did five years ago.

So here's your practical action step this week: identify one specific area — one — where you've been standing still spiritually. Maybe it's a discipline you've let go, a relationship you've avoided reconciling, a pattern of sin you've tolerated. Name it. Bring it to God. And take one concrete step forward. That's what pressing on looks like.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** According to Hebrews 6:1-2, what are the six "elementary teachings" the author lists? Why does he call believers to move *past* them rather than keep returning to them?

**2.** **Understanding:** The teacher explained that the people described in verses 4-6 are never called "born again," "sealed," or "new creations." Why does that distinction matter for how we interpret this warning passage?

**3.** **Analysis:** How do the agricultural images in verses 7-8 — land receiving rain — help clarify the difference between spiritual exposure and genuine regeneration? What does the "rain" represent, and what does the soil reveal?

**4.** **Application:** The author pivots in verse 9 to say he is "convinced of better things" about his readers. What specific evidence does he point to in verses 10-11? What does this suggest about how we can have genuine assurance without falling into pride or presumption?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The lesson closed with a challenge to identify one area of spiritual standstill. Without requiring disclosure, what makes it difficult to honestly assess our own spiritual growth — and what makes it necessary?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students distinguishing between *experiencing the Spirit's work* and *being regenerated by the Spirit*. Students who grasp the passage will recognize that the warning and the assurance in verses 9-12 are not contradictory — they address two different groups simultaneously. Weak answers will treat verses 4-6 as a threat to eternal security rather than a pastoral diagnostic.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students demonstrating genuine engagement will move beyond doctrinal analysis to honest self-examination — asking not just "what does this mean?" but "what does this reveal about me?" Watch for willingness to name areas of plateau without defensiveness or dismissiveness.

**Closing Reflection:** "If someone examined your life the way the author of Hebrews examined his readers — looking for the labor of love, the ministry to others, the forward movement — what would they find as evidence that your faith is genuine and growing?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance God Keeps His Own**

**Key Idea:** Genuine saving faith does not stand still. God's faithfulness guarantees the perseverance of those who are truly His — and the evidence of that perseverance is forward movement toward Christ-likeness.

**Memory Verse:** "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation." — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The six elementary teachings in Hebrews 6:1-2 are the foundation — essential, but meant to be built *upon*, not endlessly re-laid.
* The people described in verses 4-6 experienced powerful spiritual exposure — conviction, enlightenment, even tasting spiritual realities — but the language of genuine regeneration (born again, sealed, new creation) is entirely absent.
* The Greek word for "fallen away" (*parapiptō*) describes a decisive, willful departure — not a stumbling believer, but someone who has settled into ongoing rejection of Christ.
* The agricultural image is clarifying: identical rain falls on both soils. What differs is what's underneath. Fruit reveals what the soil truly is.
* Verses 9-12 are not a contradiction — they are pastoral assurance. The author points to visible fruit — love, service, diligence — as confirmation of genuine faith, not the cause of it.

**Gospel Connection:** This passage does not threaten the security of those who belong to Christ — it exposes the danger of religious proximity without personal surrender. The gospel calls us not merely to be near the truth but to be transformed by it. Christ's finished work on the cross is sufficient for every sinner who trusts Him. If you have genuinely placed your faith in Him, God Himself is the guarantee of your perseverance (John 10:28-29). The warning is a mercy — God uses it to stir His own people toward diligence and to call the unconverted to genuine faith before it is too late.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Looking at the six foundational teachings in verses 1-2, which one do you feel least confident about — and what would it look like to grow in that area?
**2.** The author points to "labor of love" and ministry to others as evidence of genuine faith. What fruit in your own life confirms that your faith is real and active?
**3.** Where have you been spiritually standing still — and what is one step forward you can take this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, take the closing action step seriously. Name one specific area of spiritual plateau — a neglected discipline, an unresolved relationship, a tolerated pattern of sin. Write it down. Pray over it. Take one concrete step forward before your next class meeting. Pressing on is not a feeling — it is a decision followed by movement.

**Prayer:** "Lord, thank You that You are faithful even when I am not. Search me and show me where I have been coasting on religious familiarity instead of pressing into genuine growth. I do not want to be near the truth — I want to be transformed by it. Stir in me the diligence, the love, and the forward movement that marks a life truly kept by You. In Christ's name, Amen."
