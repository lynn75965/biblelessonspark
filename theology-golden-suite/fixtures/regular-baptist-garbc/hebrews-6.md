## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Regular Baptist (GARBC) — Dispensational, Cessationist, Eternal Security

**Lesson Title:** *Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of Grace*

**Lesson Summary:** Hebrews 6:1–12 confronts believers with a sobering call to move beyond spiritual infancy toward genuine Christian maturity. The passage contains one of Scripture's most debated warning sections, followed by an anchor of encouragement rooted in God's faithfulness. Students will wrestle honestly with what the warning means without abandoning the security that belongs to every true believer.

**Main Theme:** Genuine saving faith produces spiritual growth, and God's faithfulness guarantees that genuine believers will press forward toward maturity.

**Key Takeaway:** A truly saved person does not coast — they grow; and God's own character is the guarantee that genuine faith will not fail.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often carry spiritual complacency alongside real-life pressure. Many have heard this passage misused to threaten their salvation. This lesson gives them theological clarity, honest engagement with a hard text, and a motivating challenge to pursue depth rather than drift.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews was written to a Jewish-Christian audience facing severe social and religious pressure to abandon Christ and return to Judaism. The author (unnamed in Scripture) writes with pastoral urgency throughout, alternating between doctrinal exposition and direct warning. Chapter 6 follows a sharp rebuke in 5:11–14, where the author accuses his readers of spiritual arrested development — they should be teachers but still need milk. Chapter 6 opens as a direct response: stop rehashing elementary truths and press toward maturity.

**The Six Foundational Doctrines (vv. 1–2)**

The author lists six foundational teachings: repentance from dead works, faith toward God, instruction about baptisms, laying on of hands, resurrection of the dead, and eternal judgment. These are not dismissed as unimportant — they are called a *foundation*. The problem is not the foundation itself but remaining on it indefinitely. A building that is only foundation is not yet a building. These six items correspond closely to basic Jewish catechesis adapted for Christian proclamation, which explains why the author urges his audience not to keep re-laying what has already been established.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4–6) — Interpretive Background**

This is one of the most contested passages in the New Testament. Four major interpretations exist: (1) the warning describes genuine believers who lose salvation; (2) it describes genuine believers hypothetically — the condition is impossible; (3) it describes professing believers who were never truly regenerate; (4) it describes genuine believers who face temporal discipline, not eternal loss.

The GARBC position, consistent with eternal security and the broader witness of Scripture, best aligns with interpretations (2) and (3). The Greek participles in verses 4–5 — *enlightened*, *tasted the heavenly gift*, *partakers of the Holy Spirit*, *tasted the good word of God* — describe real spiritual experience and exposure, but the language of *tasting* (Greek: *geuomai*) does not require full reception. Judas Iscariot, for instance, experienced extraordinary proximity to Christ without genuine regeneration. Matthew 13:20–21 and 1 John 2:19 both describe people with genuine religious experience who ultimately proved unregenerate. The impossibility stated in verse 6 — "it is impossible to renew them again to repentance" — functions as a logical reductio: if apostasy from Christ were possible for a true believer, restoration would be equally impossible, since Christ cannot be recrucified. The argument exposes the absurdity of the hypothetical, not its actuality.

**The Agricultural Metaphor (vv. 7–8)**

The land that drinks rain and produces useful vegetation is blessed; land that produces thorns is worthless and near cursing. This mirrors the Parable of the Soils. Genuine faith produces fruit. The metaphor does not teach that a fruitful field can become a thorn field — it distinguishes two kinds of ground from the start.

**The Encouragement and Assurance (vv. 9–12)**

The author's pastoral tone shifts: "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you." The word *convinced* (Greek: *peithō*) expresses settled confidence. God is not unjust to forget their labor of love (v. 10) — a statement grounded in God's own character, not human performance. Verse 11 calls for diligence to the end — not to *earn* assurance but to *demonstrate* it through perseverance. Verse 12 closes with a call to imitate those who through faith and patience inherit the promises.

**Doctrinal Connections**

This passage connects directly to the GARBC doctrine of eternal security — not as a license for laziness, but as a foundation for confident, motivated growth. The warning is real; the assurance is equally real. Both serve the same pastoral goal: motivating genuine believers toward maturity.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Hook Quotation (2 minutes)**

Open with this quotation from A.W. Tozer: *"It is doubtful whether God can bless a man greatly until He has hurt him deeply."* Write it on a whiteboard or display it visually. Ask students: "What do you think Tozer meant? Do you agree?" Allow two or three brief responses. This surfaces the tension between spiritual comfort and spiritual growth that Hebrews 6 directly addresses.

**Warm-Up Discussion Prompt (3 minutes)**

Ask: "Think about something you learned in your first year as a Christian — something that was brand new and life-changing. Is it still producing growth in you, or has it become background noise?" Give students 60 seconds to think, then invite two or three responses. This personalizes the author's concern about spiritual stagnation before the text is even opened.

**Transition into Teaching:**
"The writer of Hebrews had the same concern for his readers. Let's look at what he said — and why it still matters for every one of us today."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Hebrews chapter 6, beginning at verse 1. We're going to read the first twelve verses together, and I want to warn you upfront — this is one of those passages that has caused genuine confusion and, in some cases, genuine fear. Our goal today is to understand what it actually says, what it actually means, and what God intends for us to do with it.

Let me read verses 1 and 2 first.

> "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity, not laying again a foundation of repentance from dead works and of faith toward God, of instruction about washings and laying on of hands, and the resurrection of the dead and eternal judgment."
> — Hebrews 6:1–2 (NASB)

Notice what the author is *not* saying. He is not saying these foundational truths are wrong or unimportant. He calls them a *foundation* — and every structure needs one. The problem is a believer who spends their entire Christian life re-pouring the same foundation and never builds anything on top of it. Think about what that looks like practically: someone who has heard the gospel dozens of times, understands repentance and faith, knows about baptism and resurrection — but has never moved into deeper understanding of who Christ is, what the Spirit does, or how to live out the Word under real pressure. That person is spiritually stalled.

The author's command is "press on to maturity." The Greek word behind "press on" carries the idea of being carried forward — it is active and intentional movement. Maturity in the New Testament is not about age or tenure in the church. It is about depth of understanding and consistency of obedience. A fifty-year-old believer who never opens their Bible outside of Sunday is not mature in this sense, regardless of how long they have been saved.

Now let's read the hard part — verses 4 through 6.

> "For in the case of those who have once been enlightened and have tasted of the heavenly gift and have been made partakers of the Holy Spirit, and have tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come, and then have fallen away, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance, since they again crucify to themselves the Son of God and put Him to open shame."
> — Hebrews 6:4–6 (NASB)

This is the passage that has made people lie awake at night wondering if they have committed some unforgivable spiritual error. Let me give you the honest interpretive work this text requires.

First, notice the word *tasted*. In Greek, *geuomai* can mean to taste without fully consuming. The same word is used in Hebrews 2:9 of Christ tasting death — and there it clearly means full experience. But context determines meaning. Here, the language of tasting, being enlightened, and being a partaker describes someone with genuine spiritual exposure — someone who sat under the Word, experienced the community of the Spirit, witnessed God's power — but the text does not use the language of regeneration, of being *born again*, of being *in Christ*. Those are the terms Scripture reserves for genuine saving faith.

Second, the argument the author is making is actually a logical trap — and it is set to protect you, not threaten you. He is saying: *if* a true believer could fall away, restoration would be impossible, because Christ cannot be recrucified. The impossibility of the hypothetical is the point. He is not describing something that happens — he is describing something that cannot happen, and using that impossibility to show the absurdity of abandoning Christ. Think of it this way: imagine someone says, "I'm going to jump off this building and land safely." You don't need to watch them jump to know the outcome is impossible. The author is doing the same thing — the hypothetical is designed to stop the drift, not describe a real destination.

Third, look at what comes immediately after in verses 9 through 11.

> "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation, though we are speaking in this way."
> — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

The author himself draws a line. He says "better things — things that accompany salvation." He distinguishes his readers from the hypothetical apostates. He is *convinced* of this — not hoping, not guessing. And the ground of that conviction is not their performance. Verse 10 says God is not unjust to forget their work and love. God's own justice and faithfulness are the guarantee.

Here is the evangelistic edge of this passage that we cannot miss: not everyone who has religious experience has saving faith. There are people in our churches — perhaps people we love — who have been exposed to every element of Christianity: baptism, preaching, the Lord's Supper, community, answered prayer around them — and who have never genuinely trusted Christ. The warning in this passage is a gospel warning. It says: exposure is not enough. Proximity is not salvation. If you are in that category today, the call is not to despair — it is to genuinely receive what you have only been near.

For those who are genuinely saved, the call of verses 11 and 12 is this: show the same diligence to the end. Not to earn assurance — you already have it in Christ. But to *demonstrate* it. Genuine faith does not coast. It grows. It produces. It presses forward. The agricultural image in verses 7 and 8 makes this vivid — the same rain falls on two fields. One produces a harvest. One produces thorns. The difference is not the rain. The difference is what was always in the soil.

So here is your challenge as you leave today: identify one area of your Christian life where you have been re-laying foundation instead of building. Pick one concrete step — a book of the Bible you will commit to studying, a spiritual discipline you will begin, a person you will disciple or be discipled by. Press on. That is not a suggestion in this passage. It is a command backed by the full faithfulness of God.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: Foundation or Building? (Case Study Analysis)**
*Time: 8–10 minutes | Materials: Printed case studies or read aloud*

Present two brief scenarios to the class:

**Case A:** Marcus has attended church for twenty years. He was baptized, knows the gospel, and can explain repentance clearly. But he reads his Bible only at Sunday services, has never led anyone to Christ, and admits his faith feels the same as it did in year one.

**Case B:** Diane became a believer at age 38. Within two years she joined a small group, began studying Hebrews independently, and now mentors a newer believer. She still struggles with doubt but keeps pressing forward.

Ask small groups of 3–4 to discuss: *Which person reflects Hebrews 6:1's command to "press on to maturity"? What specific change would the author of Hebrews urge for each person?*

Bring groups back together and ask one spokesperson per group to share one observation. Draw the connection to the agricultural metaphor from verses 7–8.

*Adaptation for small classes:* Discuss as a full group, asking individuals to respond to one scenario each.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** According to Hebrews 6:1–2, what are the six foundational doctrines the author lists, and what is his stated reason for urging believers to move beyond them?

**2.** **Understanding:** The author uses the word *tasted* in verses 4–5 to describe the experience of those who fall away. Based on the teaching, why does that word choice matter for interpreting whether these individuals were genuinely regenerate?

**3.** **Analysis:** How does the logical structure of verses 4–6 function as a protection for believers rather than a threat? What does the impossibility of recrucifying Christ reveal about the author's actual argument?

**4.** **Application:** Verse 10 grounds assurance in God's character — "God is not unjust so as to forget your work and the love which you have shown toward His name." How does anchoring assurance in God's justice rather than personal performance change the way a believer pursues growth?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The passage distinguishes between religious exposure and genuine saving faith. How would you explain that distinction to someone who has attended church for years but is uncertain whether they are truly saved?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can articulate that the warning passage describes spiritual exposure without necessarily indicating regeneration, and that the impossibility in verse 6 is a logical argument, not a description of an actual event. Students should connect assurance to God's character in verse 10, not to personal achievement.

**Spiritual Indicators:** A student who has genuinely engaged this lesson will be able to hold the warning and the assurance together without collapsing one into the other. Watch for honest acknowledgment of personal spiritual stagnation alongside renewed confidence in God's faithfulness — not anxiety, not complacency.

**Closing Reflection:** "If God is not unjust to forget your labor of love, what labor of love are you currently giving Him to remember?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of Grace**

**Key Idea:** Genuine saving faith produces growth. God's own faithfulness — not your performance — is the guarantee that true faith will not ultimately fail.

**Memory Verse:** "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation, though we are speaking in this way." — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The six foundational doctrines in Hebrews 6:1–2 are not wrong — but a believer who spends their whole life re-laying the same foundation never builds anything on top of it.
* The warning in verses 4–6 describes someone with real spiritual exposure — but exposure is not the same as regeneration. Proximity to Christianity is not the same as saving faith in Christ.
* The impossibility in verse 6 is the author's logical argument *against* apostasy, not a description of something that actually happens to true believers.
* God's own character — His justice and faithfulness — is the anchor of your assurance (v. 10). Your security rests on who He is, not how well you performed this week.
* Genuine faith demonstrates itself through diligent, forward movement — not to earn assurance, but because assurance produces motivation to grow.

**Gospel Connection:** This passage carries an urgent evangelistic edge. Not everyone who has been near Christianity has genuinely received Christ. Baptism, church attendance, answered prayers witnessed, sermons heard — none of these, alone or together, constitute saving faith. If you have been *near* the gospel without ever truly trusting Christ, today is the day to receive what you have only been close to. Salvation is not inherited by proximity — it is received by genuine faith in Jesus Christ, who died for sin and rose from the dead.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where in your Christian life have you been re-laying foundation instead of building on it?
**2.** Is your assurance resting on God's character and Christ's finished work, or on your own spiritual track record?
**3.** Who in your life might be in the category of religious exposure without genuine saving faith — and how might God use you to speak clearly to them?

**Weekly Challenge:** Before next week, identify one concrete growth step — a book of the Bible to study, a spiritual discipline to begin, or a person to disciple or be discipled by. Write it down. Tell one person. Press on.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that my security rests on Your faithfulness, not my own. Forgive me for the times I have coasted when You called me to grow. Open my eyes to where I have been re-laying foundation instead of building. Give me courage to press on toward maturity, and use my life to point others to the genuine faith that only comes through Christ. Amen."
