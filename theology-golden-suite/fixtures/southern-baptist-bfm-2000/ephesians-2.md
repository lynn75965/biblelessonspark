## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Southern Baptist (BF&M 2000)

**Lesson Title:** *Dead No More: Grace, Faith, and the Life God Gives*

**Lesson Summary:** Ephesians 2:1-10 presents the starkest possible contrast in Scripture — what humanity is apart from God, and what God does about it through Jesus Christ. Paul moves from the devastating reality of spiritual death to the breathtaking gift of salvation by grace through faith, culminating in the declaration that believers are God's workmanship created for good works. This lesson unpacks that transformation with theological precision and pastoral warmth.

**Main Theme:** God's grace rescues spiritually dead people through faith in Christ and remakes them for purposeful living.

**Key Takeaway:** Every student will leave understanding that salvation is entirely God's gift — received through faith, never earned — and that this gift comes with a calling to walk in good works.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often carry deep awareness of their own failures and the weight of years lived. This passage speaks directly to that weight — not with condemnation, but with the liberating news that God acts on behalf of the spiritually helpless. This age group also wrestles with purpose and legacy, making the "created for good works" conclusion especially resonant.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Paul wrote Ephesians from prison, likely in Rome around A.D. 60–62. The letter is not addressing a specific crisis but is a sweeping theological treatise on the nature of the church and the believer's identity in Christ. Chapter 2 sits at the structural heart of the letter's doctrinal section (chapters 1–3), and verses 1-10 form one of the most carefully constructed salvation passages in the New Testament.

Grammatically, verses 1-3 are a long subordinate clause — Paul piles up the evidence of human spiritual ruin before he reaches the pivot in verse 4: "But God." That two-word hinge is intentional and dramatic. Everything before it is diagnosis; everything after is rescue.

**Spiritual Death (vv. 1-3)**

The Greek word translated "dead" (*nekros*) is not metaphorical softening — it means genuinely, actually dead. Paul says the Ephesians "were dead in your trespasses and sins." Spiritual death means the total inability of fallen humanity to initiate relationship with God or earn standing before Him. Three forces characterize this condition:

1. **The world** (*aion* — the age, the present evil system of values and priorities that runs contrary to God)
2. **The devil** — called "the prince of the power of the air," a phrase indicating his domain of influence over fallen humanity
3. **The flesh** — the sinful nature, the internal disposition bent toward self rather than God

Paul includes himself: "among them we too all formerly lived." This is not a description of pagans only — it is the universal condition of fallen humanity before grace.

**"But God" — The Pivot of Grace (vv. 4-7)**

Verse 4 introduces the divine motivation: God is "rich in mercy" and acts out of "His great love with which He loved us." This is not cold theological machinery — it is personal, affectionate divine initiative. The verbs in verses 5-6 are stunning in their scope: God "made us alive together," "raised us up," and "seated us...in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus." All three are aorist tense in Greek — completed action. The believer's spiritual position is already secured.

The phrase "by grace you have been saved" appears twice (vv. 5, 8), functioning as a refrain. *Charis* (grace) means unmerited favor — a gift given not because the recipient deserves it but because the giver is generous.

**Faith, Not Works (vv. 8-9)**

Verse 8 is precise: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." The phrase "and that not of yourselves" most naturally refers to the entire salvation package — grace, faith, and the resulting salvation — as God's gift. Faith is the instrument through which grace is received, not the merit that earns it.

Verse 9 closes the door on human boasting permanently. No one stands before God with a résumé. Salvation is received, not achieved.

**Created for Good Works (v. 10)**

Verse 10 is the theological capstone. The Greek word *poiema* (translated "workmanship") is where we get the English word "poem." Believers are God's *poiema* — His crafted masterpiece. The good works are not the basis of salvation but the purpose and evidence of it. Critically, Paul says these works were "prepared beforehand" by God — meaning purposeful living is not self-invented but Spirit-directed discovery.

**Cross-References:** Romans 5:8; Titus 3:3-7; 2 Corinthians 5:17

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — "Before and After" Hook**
*(3-4 minutes)*

Open with this question: "Think about the most dramatic 'before and after' transformation you've ever seen — a home renovation, a person's health turnaround, a relationship restored. What made it remarkable?" Let two or three students respond briefly.

Then say: "Paul is about to describe the most radical before-and-after transformation in the universe — not a house, not a body, but a human soul. And the gap between the 'before' and the 'after' is not improvement. It's resurrection."

**Activity 2 — Written Reflection**
*(2 minutes, individual and silent)*

Hand students a notecard or ask them to open a notes app on their phone. Prompt: *"In one sentence, write what your life was oriented around before you trusted Christ — or if you grew up in faith, write what you think life looks like for someone with no anchor to God."*

Hold responses until the closing application. This grounds the theological content in personal experience from the start.

**Transition:** "Let's read Ephesians 2:1-10 together and let Paul's words frame everything we discuss today."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Go ahead and follow along in Ephesians 2, starting in verse 1. I want to read the whole passage before we dig in, because the full sweep of it matters.

*[Read Ephesians 2:1-10 aloud from the NASB.]*

Okay. Take a breath. That is one of the most compressed, powerful passages in the entire New Testament. Ten verses. The whole story of what God does for a human soul. Let's walk through it carefully.

**We Were Dead — Not Sick, Not Struggling. Dead.**

Paul doesn't say we were spiritually weak, or spiritually confused, or spiritually searching. He says we were *dead*. That's the Greek word *nekros* — actually, genuinely dead. And that matters, because dead people cannot help themselves. A dead person cannot decide to get better. They cannot choose to climb out of the grave on their own effort.

This is Paul's point about fallen humanity. Before grace, we were not people who needed a little help or a moral upgrade. We were people who needed resurrection. And you cannot resurrect yourself.

Now notice the three forces Paul says were running our lives in that condition. First, the world — the system of values, priorities, and assumptions that runs completely contrary to God. Think about what the world tells you to live for: status, comfort, control, approval. None of those are God. Second, the devil — Paul calls him "the prince of the power of the air." That's a first-century way of describing his domain of influence over fallen humanity. He is not omnipresent and he is not all-powerful, but he is real, and he works in the children of disobedience. Third, the flesh — the internal pull toward self. Not just sexual sin, but the whole orientation of living for yourself rather than for God.

And here's the part that should land hard: Paul says "we too all formerly lived" in this. He includes himself. This is not a description of especially wicked people. This is every human being before grace. Mid-life adults in this room know what it is to carry years of choices made from those three drives. Paul is not being harsh — he is being honest.

**Two of the Most Important Words in the Bible**

Verse 4. "But God." Two words. Everything changes.

After three verses of spiritual diagnosis — dead, disobedient, driven by the world, the flesh, and the devil — Paul pivots on two words. *But God.* God, who is rich in mercy. God, who acts out of great love. God, who makes the first move when we were incapable of making any move at all.

Think about what those two words mean for your own story. Whatever was true about your "before" — whatever you were living for, whatever you had done, however far you had drifted — "But God" is the hinge. God intervenes not because we cleaned ourselves up, but because He is merciful and He loves.

The verbs Paul uses in verses 5 and 6 are breathtaking. God "made us alive together with Christ." God "raised us up with Him." God "seated us with Him in the heavenly places." All of those are past-tense, completed actions. The believer's standing before God is not a work in progress — it is already secured in Christ. You are not working your way toward acceptance. If you are in Christ, you are already seated with Him.

**Grace Through Faith — Why This Formula Matters**

Verse 8 is one of the most memorized verses in the New Testament, and for good reason: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God."

Let's be precise about what Paul is saying. Grace is the source — God's unmerited favor, His gift freely given. Faith is the channel — the hand that receives what God offers. And Paul immediately adds "not of yourselves" and "not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." He closes every door to human pride.

Here's why this matters practically: most of us carry a quiet, unspoken belief that our standing with God rises and falls with our performance. Think about it — when you're reading Scripture consistently and serving faithfully, you feel close to God. When you've had a hard week morally, you feel distant and disqualified. We've all been there. But that feeling reflects a works-based relationship with God that Paul is dismantling right here. Grace means your standing is based on what Christ did, not on what you did this week.

Faith is not a work. Faith is not something you produce that earns God's favor. Faith is simply the open hand that receives what God is already offering.

**You Are God's Masterpiece**

Verse 10 is where Paul lands, and it's stunning. "For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

The word translated "workmanship" is *poiema* in Greek — it's where we get the English word "poem." You are God's crafted work. His masterpiece. Not a rough draft. Not a project He's frustrated with. A *poiema*.

Now here's the critical distinction: good works are not the basis of salvation — they are the *purpose* of salvation. You are not saved *by* good works. You are saved *for* good works. There is a world of difference. The person who does good works to earn God's favor is exhausted and never certain. The person who does good works because they have already been saved by grace is free — they are walking in what God prepared for them.

And notice: Paul says these works were "prepared beforehand." That means your purposeful life is not something you have to invent. It is something you discover as you walk with God. The question is not "What should I do to matter?" The question is "What has God already prepared for me to walk in?"

Here is your challenge this week: take one concrete step in your digital and online life that reflects the *poiema* God made you to be. What you post, how you engage, what you consume — these are all spaces where God's workmanship either shows or doesn't. Start there. One intentional choice. That is walking in the good works He prepared.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — "But God" Timeline**
*(Individual written reflection — 5 minutes)*

Materials: Notecard or notes app (already in hand from opening activity)

Ask students to draw a simple horizontal line on their notecard. On the left side, write "Before" — on the right side, write "After." In the middle, write "But God." Under "Before," they write one phrase describing what was driving their life (the world, the flesh, or the devil — use Paul's categories). Under "After," they write one phrase describing what God replaced that with.

Invite two or three volunteers to share. This makes the Ephesians 2 structure personally concrete without requiring lengthy testimony.

*Adaptation for larger classes:* Have students share with one neighbor instead of the full group.

---

**Activity 2 — *Poiema* Inventory**
*(Pair discussion — 4 minutes)*

Pose this question to pairs: "If good works were prepared beforehand for you to walk in, what is one area of your life — including your online or digital life — where you sense God nudging you toward purposeful action?"

Give pairs two minutes to discuss, then ask one or two pairs to share a sentence with the class.

This directly reinforces the Section 5 closing challenge without repeating the theological explanation.

*Adaptation for smaller classes:* Do this as a full-group roundtable.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses the word "dead" rather than "sick" or "struggling" to describe humanity apart from God. What is the difference, and why does that distinction matter for understanding salvation?

**2.** **Understanding:** Paul identifies three forces that characterized life before grace — the world, the devil, and the flesh. Which of these do you think is most underestimated or overlooked in everyday conversation, and why?

**3.** **Analysis:** In Ephesians 2:8-9, Paul says salvation is "not of yourselves" and "not as a result of works." How does this directly challenge the quiet, unspoken belief that our standing with God rises and falls with our weekly performance?

**4.** **Application:** The lesson distinguishes between being saved *by* works and saved *for* works. How does understanding that difference change the motivation behind serving, giving, or living faithfully?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Verse 10 calls believers God's *poiema* — His crafted masterpiece. What would change about how you use your digital and online presence if you genuinely believed that description of yourself?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can clearly articulate the "by grace through faith" formula without collapsing faith into a work or merit. Strong answers will distinguish the *source* (grace) from the *channel* (faith). Watch for students who can name all three forces of spiritual death from the text.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged will begin reframing their daily choices — especially online behavior — as expressions of their identity as God's *poiema* rather than attempts to earn standing. They will speak of good works with freedom rather than obligation or anxiety.

**Closing Reflection:** "If someone asked you tomorrow, 'What has God prepared for you to walk in this week?' — what would your honest answer be?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Dead No More: Grace, Faith, and the Life God Gives**

**Key Idea:** God rescues spiritually dead people — not because they earned it, but because He is rich in mercy. That rescue comes with a new identity and a purpose already prepared.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8-9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Before grace, every person is spiritually dead — driven by the world's values, the devil's influence, and the pull of the sinful nature. This is not just a description of "bad people." Paul includes himself.
* "But God" — two words that change everything. God intervenes out of mercy and love, not because we cleaned ourselves up first.
* Salvation is received, not achieved. Grace is the source; faith is the open hand that receives it. Neither your best week nor your worst week changes your standing in Christ.
* You are God's *poiema* — His crafted masterpiece (Ephesians 2:10). That word is where we get the English word "poem." You are not a rough draft.
* Good works are not the price of salvation — they are the purpose of it. God prepared specific works for you to walk in. Your job is to discover and do them.

**Gospel Connection:** Ephesians 2:1-10 is the gospel in miniature. Every person begins in spiritual death, unable to save themselves. God, motivated by love and mercy, sends Christ — whose death and resurrection make spiritual life possible. Anyone who responds in faith receives that life as a free gift. Salvation is available to all who will trust in Christ, because He died for all and rose for all.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Where do you still catch yourself believing your standing with God depends on your performance this week?
**2.** What is one area — including your online life — where you sense God has prepared a good work for you to walk in?
**3.** How does being called God's *poiema* change the way you see yourself on a hard day?

**Weekly Challenge:** Choose one specific habit in your digital or online life this week that reflects who God made you to be. It might be what you post, how you respond to someone, what you stop consuming, or how you encourage someone through a message. One intentional step. Walk in what He prepared.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You that 'But God' is the hinge of my story. I was dead, and You made me alive. I did nothing to earn it — You gave it freely. Help me this week to live as Your *poiema*, walking in the good works You prepared for me, especially in the spaces no one sees. Let my life be a reflection of Your grace. In Jesus' name, Amen."
