## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Southern Baptist (BF&M 2000)

**Lesson Title:** *The Shepherd Who Leaves No One Behind*

**Lesson Summary:** Psalm 23 is one of Scripture's most beloved passages, yet its six verses carry profound theological weight about God's provision, guidance, and presence. This lesson examines David's shepherd metaphor through a missional lens, asking not only what God provides for us, but how His shepherding shapes us into people who pursue others. The class will discover that the psalm moves from personal comfort to table fellowship — a picture of God's grace extended outward.

**Main Theme:** God's shepherding care is both personally sustaining and missionally transforming — those who experience His provision are equipped and compelled to extend His grace to others.

**Key Takeaway:** Because the Good Shepherd meets every need, His followers can live with open hands and outward focus, inviting others into the same abundant life.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (ages 36–50) often carry significant responsibilities — careers, aging parents, teenagers, financial pressures. They are drawn to Psalm 23 for comfort, but may not have considered its missional implications. This lesson will honor their need for assurance while challenging them toward purposeful community engagement.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Genre and Literary Context**

Psalm 23 is a psalm of trust — a subcategory of the lament genre in which the crisis is resolved and the psalmist rests in confident reliance on God. Attributed to David, it draws on his lived experience as a shepherd boy before becoming king (1 Samuel 16:11). The psalm's six verses move through a deliberate arc: God as shepherd providing rest and guidance (vv. 1–3), God as companion through mortal danger (v. 4), God as host at a table of honor (v. 5), and a final declaration of lifelong covenant loyalty (v. 6).

**Historical and Cultural Context**

Shepherding in the ancient Near East was not a romantic occupation — it was demanding, dangerous, and socially humble. A shepherd's entire purpose was the welfare of the flock: locating water, guiding through treacherous terrain, protecting from predators, and restoring wandering sheep. When David calls the Lord his shepherd, he is attributing to God the full range of that demanding, self-giving role. The phrase "green pastures" (v. 2) refers not to lush meadows but to tender spring grass in a semi-arid land — provision that required the shepherd to know where to lead. "Still waters" translates the Hebrew *mê menûḥôt* — waters of rest or quietness — because sheep will not drink from turbulent streams.

**Key Hebrew Word Insights**

- *Rāʿâ* (shepherd, v. 1): The verb form means "to tend, to feed, to lead to pasture." God is not merely a guardian; He is actively engaged in nourishment.
- *Yehweh rōʿî* (v. 1): "The LORD is my shepherd" — the divine name YHWH paired with the intimate possessive "my" makes this a covenant declaration, not merely a metaphor.
- *Nepeš* (soul, v. 3): Often translated "soul," this Hebrew word refers to the whole person — breath, life, vitality. "He restores my soul" means God renews the entire person, not merely the spiritual dimension.
- *Ṣalmāwet* (v. 4): Traditionally rendered "shadow of death," this word likely describes deep, dense darkness — a valley so dark it feels lethal. The missional implication is significant: God does not reroute around dark valleys; He accompanies His people through them.

**Doctrinal Framing**

The psalm affirms God's active, personal provision — not a distant deity but a God who leads, restores, and accompanies. The prepared table "in the presence of my enemies" (v. 5) is a picture of grace extended in hostile territory — God's provision is not contingent on favorable circumstances. This anticipates the New Testament revelation of Jesus as the Good Shepherd (John 10:11), who lays down His life for the sheep. Christ's unlimited atonement — His death sufficient for all — is the ultimate fulfillment of Ezekiel 34:11–16, where God promised to personally seek out the lost sheep. The overflowing cup (v. 5) and the pursuit of "goodness and lovingkindness" (v. 6) — *ḥesed*, covenant faithfulness — point toward a God whose grace is abundant and available to all who respond in faith.

**Age-Appropriate Depth Note**

Mid-life adults appreciate the psalm's realism: it does not promise the absence of dark valleys but the presence of God within them. This is theologically honest and pastorally powerful for a generation navigating real suffering.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — Story Hook (3–4 minutes)**

Open with this scenario: "Imagine you're hiking an unfamiliar trail in rugged mountain terrain. You've taken a wrong turn, your water is low, and the light is fading. Suddenly, a seasoned guide appears — someone who knows every path, every water source, every danger on that mountain. How does your entire experience of that trail change the moment that guide is with you?"

Invite two or three brief responses. Then say: "That shift — from anxious and alone to accompanied and guided — is exactly what David is describing in Psalm 23. But the guide he's describing isn't just competent. He's the Creator of the mountain."

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Discussion Prompt (2–3 minutes)**

Ask: "When you hear Psalm 23, what's the first image or feeling that comes to mind? And when did you first encounter this psalm — was it a funeral, a childhood memory, a crisis moment?"

Allow brief sharing. This surfaces emotional connections and sets up the lesson's goal: moving from familiarity to fresh discovery.

**Transition:** "Today we're going to look at this psalm not just as a source of personal comfort — though it absolutely is that — but as a picture of how God's provision shapes us into people who reach outward."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Psalm 23. We're going to read it together, and I want you to try to hear it as if for the first time.

> "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want. He makes me lie down in green pastures; He leads me beside quiet waters. He restores my soul; He guides me in the paths of righteousness for His name's sake. Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I fear no evil, for You are with me; Your rod and Your staff, they comfort me. You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies; You have anointed my head with oil; My cup overflows. Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life, and I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever."
> — Psalm 23:1–6 (NASB)

Six verses. Probably the most memorized passage in all of Scripture. And yet most of us have never fully unpacked what David is actually claiming here.

Let's start at the beginning: "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want." That word "want" doesn't mean desire — it means lack. David is making a radical declaration of sufficiency. He's saying: because the LORD is my shepherd, there is nothing I genuinely need that He will not provide. That's not a prosperity promise. It's a covenant statement. The name he uses — LORD, in all capitals in your Bible — is the covenant name YHWH. David isn't describing a general religious feeling. He's saying: the God who entered into covenant with Israel is personally shepherding me.

Now think about what a shepherd actually does. David knew this from personal experience — he spent years in the fields before he wore a crown. A shepherd doesn't just watch the flock from a distance. He finds the water. He locates the grass. He walks ahead into danger to clear the path. "He makes me lie down in green pastures" — sheep will not lie down unless they feel completely safe. The shepherd has done the work of securing the environment before the sheep can rest. That's the picture of what God does for His people.

"He leads me beside quiet waters." The Hebrew here is *mê menûḥôt* — waters of rest. Sheep won't drink from rushing, turbulent streams. They need still water. God doesn't just point you toward provision — He prepares the provision in a form you can actually receive.

Then verse 3: "He restores my soul." The Hebrew word for soul here is *nepeš* — it means your whole self, your entire vitality. This isn't just spiritual refreshment. God restores the whole person — emotionally, physically, spiritually. And notice why He guides in paths of righteousness: "for His name's sake." God's guidance isn't primarily about your comfort — it's about His character. He leads you rightly because that's who He is. That's actually more reliable than if He did it just for you, because His character never changes.

Now we reach verse 4, and the psalm shifts dramatically. "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death..." — that Hebrew phrase *ṣalmāwet* describes a darkness so dense it feels lethal. And notice: God doesn't reroute around it. He walks through it with you. "I fear no evil, for You are with me." The comfort isn't the absence of the dark valley — it's the presence of the Shepherd inside it. This is theologically honest. God never promised to remove every hard circumstance. He promised to be present in every hard circumstance.

Here's where the psalm takes a missional turn that we often miss. Verse 5: "You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies." This is a stunning image. God isn't waiting until your enemies are gone to bless you. He sets a table in hostile territory. He anoints your head with oil — a sign of honor and welcome. Your cup overflows. This is abundance in the middle of opposition.

Think about what that means for how you engage your community. God's provision for you isn't meant to be hoarded inside comfortable walls. The overflowing cup is meant to spill out. The table God sets is spacious — there's room for others. When you've experienced the Shepherd's care through your own dark valleys, you become someone who can walk alongside others in theirs. That's the missional shape of Psalm 23.

Verse 6 is the declaration that seals it: "Surely goodness and lovingkindness will follow me all the days of my life." That word "lovingkindness" is the Hebrew *ḥesed* — it means covenant faithfulness, loyal love, the love that doesn't quit. And the word translated "follow" can also mean "pursue" — God's goodness and covenant love are actively chasing you down, every single day.

This psalm ends where it began — in relationship. "I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever." Not just visiting. Dwelling. Permanent residence in the presence of God.

Here's the challenge I want to leave you with this week: The Shepherd who has walked through dark valleys with you has equipped you to walk alongside someone else. Think of one person in your neighborhood, workplace, or circle who is currently in a valley. Not someone you need to fix — just someone who needs to know they're not alone. The overflowing cup means you have something to give. Your specific challenge this week: reach out to that one person. Not with a sermon — with presence. That's what the Shepherd modeled. That's what Psalm 23 looks like with skin on it.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — Provision Mapping (8–10 minutes)**
*Case Study Analysis | Materials: whiteboard or large paper, markers*

Divide the class into three groups. Assign each group two verses from Psalm 23 (Group 1: vv. 1–2; Group 2: vv. 3–4; Group 3: vv. 5–6). Each group answers two questions about their verses:

1. What specific need does God meet here?
2. Who in your community currently has this need?

Groups share findings with the class. Record responses in two columns on the whiteboard: *"What God provides"* and *"Who needs this."* The visual connection between God's provision and community need reinforces the lesson's missional turn.

**Adaptation:** For smaller classes (under 8), work through all six verses together as one group, one verse at a time.

**Activity 2 — The Overflowing Cup (4–5 minutes)**
*Personal Reflection | No materials needed*

Ask students to silently answer this question in their minds: *"Which provision from Psalm 23 — rest, restoration, guidance, presence in darkness, or abundance — have you most recently experienced personally?"*

After 60 seconds of silence, ask: *"Now — who in your life needs exactly that same thing right now?"*

Invite two or three volunteers to share. This bridges personal experience to outward focus without requiring vulnerability beyond what students choose.

**Adaptation:** In larger classes, have students share with a partner rather than the full group.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** What does the covenant name YHWH — translated "LORD" in all capitals — tell us about the kind of relationship David is describing in verse 1? Why does the possessive "my shepherd" matter?

**2.** **Understanding:** The psalm never promises the removal of dark valleys — only God's presence through them. How does that distinction change the way you read verse 4 compared to how you may have understood it before?

**3.** **Analysis:** The teaching identified a "missional turn" at verse 5 — the table prepared in enemy territory with an overflowing cup. Why would God's provision in hostile circumstances point outward rather than inward? What does an overflowing cup imply about hoarding versus sharing?

**4.** **Application:** The Hebrew word *ḥesed* (v. 6) describes loyal love that actively pursues. If God's covenant faithfulness is chasing you down every day, how should that reality shape the way you pursue people in your community who are spiritually wandering?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The closing challenge named one person in a valley who needs presence, not a sermon. Have you identified that person? What is one specific, practical step you could take this week?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students connecting the shepherd metaphor to God's active, personal engagement — not passive oversight. Strong answers will distinguish between God's provision as personal comfort and as missional fuel. Watch for students who can name *ḥesed* as loyal, pursuing love rather than general kindness.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who are genuinely processing this lesson will name a specific person when answering question 5 rather than giving a theoretical response. Watch for movement from "God comforts me" to "God equips me to comfort others" — that shift signals the lesson's main theme has landed.

**Closing Reflection:** *"The Shepherd walks through the valley with you — whose valley are you willing to walk through with someone else?"*

## Section 8: Group Handout (Standalone)

**The Shepherd Who Leaves No One Behind**
*A Study in Psalm 23:1–6*

**Key Idea:** God's shepherding care is not only personally sustaining — it is missionally transforming. Those who experience His provision are equipped and compelled to extend His grace to others.

**Memory Verse:** "The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want." — Psalm 23:1 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* "I shall not want" is a covenant declaration, not a prosperity promise — because YHWH is your shepherd, nothing you genuinely need will be withheld.
* God prepares provision in a form you can actually receive — still waters, not turbulent streams; rest secured before the sheep lie down.
* God does not reroute around dark valleys. He walks through them with you. The comfort is His presence, not the absence of difficulty.
* The table prepared "in the presence of my enemies" means God's blessing is not contingent on favorable circumstances — abundance arrives in hostile territory.
* *Ḥesed* — the loyal, covenant love of God — is actively pursuing you every day of your life. That pursuing love shapes how you pursue others.

**Gospel Connection:** Psalm 23 finds its fullest meaning in Jesus, who declared, "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down His life for the sheep" (John 10:11, NASB). The shepherd metaphor David celebrated is not just poetry — it is prophecy. Christ's death on the cross is the ultimate act of *ḥesed*, covenant love that pursues every wandering person. His sacrifice is sufficient for all, and the table He sets is open to anyone who responds in faith. If you have never trusted Christ as your Shepherd, that invitation stands today.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** Which image from Psalm 23 — green pastures, still waters, the dark valley, the prepared table, the overflowing cup — best describes where you are in life right now? Why?
**2.** God's guidance runs "for His name's sake" (v. 3), not merely for your comfort. How does grounding His leadership in His character rather than your circumstances give you more confidence, not less?
**3.** Who in your neighborhood, workplace, or circle is currently walking through a dark valley? What would it look like to be present with them this week — not to fix, but to accompany?

**Weekly Challenge:** Identify one person this week who needs the Shepherd's presence — someone in a hard season, feeling alone or directionless. Reach out with a simple act of presence: a text, a meal, a phone call, a walk. You are not their savior. You are a sheep who knows the Shepherd, pointing the way. Your overflowing cup has something to give.

**Prayer:** "Lord, thank You for being a Shepherd who does not watch from a distance but walks through every valley with me. Restore my soul where I am weary. Lead me where I need to go. And where my cup overflows, give me eyes to see who is thirsty. Make me someone who accompanies others the way You have accompanied me. For Your name's sake. Amen."
