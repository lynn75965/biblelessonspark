## Section 1: Lens + Lesson Overview

**Theological Profile:** Southern Baptist (BF&M 1963) — Christ-centered, Scripture-authoritative, evangelistic emphasis

**Lesson Title:** *From Dead to Alive: The Gospel of Grace in Ephesians 2*

**Lesson Summary:**
Ephesians 2:1-10 delivers one of Scripture's most complete portraits of salvation — where humanity has been, what God did about it, and where believers now stand. Paul moves from the devastating reality of spiritual death to the breathtaking reality of resurrection life in Christ. The passage anchors salvation entirely in God's grace, received through faith, expressed in transformed living.

**Main Theme Statement:**
God, in His mercy and love, rescues spiritually dead people through the finished work of Christ — not because of anything they deserve, but so that His grace might be displayed and His purposes fulfilled in them.

**Key Takeaway:**
Every believer was once spiritually dead, is now made alive in Christ by grace through faith, and has been created for a life of purposeful good works.

**Audience Insight:**
Mid-life adults (36-50) often carry complex spiritual histories — some have walked with Christ for decades, others carry regret over wasted years. This passage speaks directly to both: the depth of grace and the dignity of purpose.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Paul wrote Ephesians from Roman imprisonment, likely around A.D. 60-62. The letter is not addressing a specific crisis but is a sweeping theological treatise on the nature of the church and the believer's identity in Christ. Ephesians 2:1-10 sits at the structural heart of the letter's first half, following Paul's prayer that believers would understand the resurrection power already at work in them (1:19-20). Chapter 2 opens by showing *why* that power was necessary — they were dead.

The audience was largely Gentile, people with no prior covenant relationship with Israel's God. Paul's use of "you" (vv. 1-2) and "we" (vv. 3-5) is deliberate: both Gentiles and Jews share the same pre-salvation condition. No ethnic or religious background earns exemption from spiritual death.

**Key Greek Word Insights**

- *Nekros* (dead, v. 1): This is the same word used for physical corpses. Paul is not describing someone spiritually sick or weakened — he is describing a person with zero capacity to generate spiritual life. The analogy is precise: dead people do not respond to their environment.
- *Parabasis* (trespasses): A deliberate stepping across a known boundary — willful transgression.
- *Hamartia* (sins): Missing the mark — falling short of God's standard whether knowingly or not.
- *Charis* (grace, v. 8): Unmerited favor — God's disposition to give what is not deserved and cannot be earned.
- *Pistis* (faith, v. 8): Active trust and reliance — not mere intellectual assent. Faith is the instrument through which grace is received.
- *Poiema* (workmanship, v. 10): The Greek root gives us the English word "poem." Believers are God's crafted masterpiece — intentional, purposeful, designed.

**Doctrinal Framing — Southern Baptist Perspective**

Paul's description of spiritual death establishes that humanity's fallen nature leaves every person incapable of saving themselves. Salvation is entirely God's initiative — He is "rich in mercy" (v. 4) and acts from love. This is not God selecting certain individuals while bypassing others; Paul's framing throughout Ephesians 2 is universal in scope. The "we" of verse 3 encompasses all humanity, and the grace described is the same grace available to all who respond in faith.

Verse 8 is critical: "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and this is not of yourselves, it is the gift of God." The phrase "this is not of yourselves" refers to the entire salvation event — grace, faith, and the resulting new life are all God's gift. Faith is not a human work that earns salvation; it is the God-given means of receiving what God freely offers. This preserves both the sovereignty of God's grace and the genuine human responsibility to respond.

Verse 9 explicitly rules out boasting — no one can stand before God claiming their salvation was earned. Verse 10 then reorients works entirely: believers are not saved *by* works, but *for* works. The good works were "prepared beforehand" — God's purpose was always that redeemed people would live redemptively in the world.

**Theological Significance**

This passage is one of Scripture's clearest gospel summaries. It answers four questions every person asks: Who was I? (Dead in sin.) What did God do? (Made me alive in Christ.) How was I saved? (Grace through faith, not works.) Why am I here now? (Created for good works.) For evangelistic purposes, Ephesians 2:1-10 functions as a complete gospel framework.

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Activity 1 — "Imagine This" Hook**
*(3-4 minutes)*

Open with this scenario: "Imagine you're a first responder arriving at the scene of an accident. You find someone who is completely unresponsive — no pulse, no breathing. At that moment, what does that person need most? They don't need advice. They don't need encouragement. They need someone to do for them what they cannot do for themselves."

Ask the class: "What would it mean if your spiritual condition before Christ was described using that exact word — *dead*? Not struggling, not searching — dead."

**Activity 2 — Warm-Up Discussion Prompt**
*(3-4 minutes)*

Pose this question to the class: "Think about a time in your life — either before faith or during a spiritually dry season — when you felt spiritually empty or disconnected from God. Without getting too personal, what did that feel like? What did you find yourself reaching for?"

Allow 2-3 brief responses. This surfaces the experiential reality the passage addresses theologically.

**Transition:** "Paul doesn't just describe that feeling — he diagnoses it, explains what God did about it, and tells us who we are now because of it. Let's open to Ephesians 2."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Ephesians 2, beginning at verse 1. We're going to read the whole passage together before we unpack it, because these ten verses form one of the most complete pictures of the gospel anywhere in Scripture.

Read Ephesians 2:1-10 aloud.

Now — let's talk about what just happened in those ten verses. Paul takes us on a journey from a graveyard to a throne room. That's not an exaggeration. Look at where he starts: "And you were dead in your trespasses and sins."

Dead. Not sick. Not confused. Not spiritually searching. Dead.

Here's why that word matters so much. When Paul uses the Greek word *nekros* — the same word used for a physical corpse — he is making a precise theological claim. A dead person cannot respond to their environment. They cannot choose to get better. They cannot decide to start living. They have zero capacity to generate life from within themselves. That is the condition Paul says every human being is in apart from Christ — spiritually dead, cut off from the life of God.

And notice verse 3 — Paul includes himself. "We too all formerly lived in the lusts of our flesh." He's not pointing a finger at the worst people in Ephesus. He's describing the universal human condition. Jew and Gentile, religious and irreligious, moral and immoral — all of us were dead.

Now look at verse 4. Two of the most important words in the Bible: "But God."

That pivot changes everything. "But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us..." — notice what drives God to act. Not our potential. Not our effort. Not our religious background. His mercy. His love. His richness in grace. God acts because of who He is, not because of what we brought to the table.

Verse 5: "Even when we were dead in our transgressions, made us alive together with Christ — by grace you have been saved."

Think about the timing here. God didn't wait until we cleaned ourselves up. He acted while we were dead. That's the scandal of grace — it arrives before we deserve it, before we ask for it, before we even know we need it. The Spirit of God convicts, draws, and moves toward people who are spiritually dead, and by His grace, people respond.

Now let's get to verse 8, because this is the verse many of us memorized but may not have fully unpacked. "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and this is not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast."

Here's a question worth sitting with: Is faith a work? Is believing something you do to earn salvation? Paul says no. The entire salvation event — the grace, the faith, the new life — is God's gift. Faith is not the price you pay for grace. Faith is the open hand that receives what God freely gives. Think about it this way: imagine someone hands you a gift. The act of reaching out and taking it doesn't mean you earned it. The gift is still entirely from them. That's what faith is — the God-given means of receiving what God freely offers.

This is why no one gets to boast. Verse 9 is explicit: "not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." If salvation were even partially earned, heaven would be full of people comparing their résumés. But grace levels every human being before God. The only ground at the cross is level ground.

Now here's where the passage takes a turn that surprises a lot of people. Verse 10: "For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand so that we would walk in them."

The Greek word for "workmanship" is *poiema* — the root of our English word "poem." You are God's crafted masterpiece. Not a rough draft. Not an afterthought. A deliberate, purposeful creation.

And here's the distinction that changes everything for how we live: we are not saved *by* good works, but we are absolutely saved *for* good works. Works don't produce salvation — salvation produces works. The order matters enormously. When you reverse it, you get religion. When you keep it right, you get the gospel.

Think about your friendships, your relationships with coworkers, neighbors, the people you see every week. Those are not accidental. Paul says these good works were "prepared beforehand." God has already laid out a path of purposeful living for you — acts of service, words of grace, moments of witness — and your job is simply to walk in them.

Here's the challenge I want to leave you with this week: identify one person in your circle of relationships who needs to hear or see the gospel. Not a stranger — someone you already know. Ask God to show you one specific act of grace or one honest conversation that could open a door. You were created in Christ Jesus for exactly that kind of moment.

You were dead. God made you alive. Now walk in it.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1 — "But God" Timeline**
*(Small Group Discussion — 8-10 minutes)*

*Materials needed:* Index cards or small slips of paper, pens

Divide into groups of 3-4. Give each person two index cards. On the first card, ask them to write a word or phrase describing their spiritual condition *before* Christ (or before a significant season of renewal). On the second card, write a word or phrase describing where they stand now in Christ.

After 2-3 minutes of writing, have each small group share their cards and discuss: "Where do you see the 'But God' moment in your own story?" Groups don't need to report back — this is a personal processing exercise.

*Adaptation for smaller classes:* Do this as a full-class exercise with open sharing instead of small groups.

**Activity 2 — Poiema Reflection**
*(Individual, then Pair Share — 4-5 minutes)*

Ask each person to silently complete this sentence on paper: *"One good work God has prepared for me to walk in this week — specifically in a friendship or relationship — might be ___________."*

After 90 seconds of writing, invite willing participants to share with a partner. Reinforce that Ephesians 2:10 frames this as purposeful, not accidental.

*Adaptation for larger classes:* Skip pair sharing; invite 2-3 volunteers to share with the full group.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** Paul uses the word "dead" rather than "sick" or "struggling" to describe our condition apart from Christ. What does the distinction between spiritual death and spiritual sickness imply about our ability to save ourselves?

**2.** **Understanding:** Verses 4-5 hinge on the phrase "But God." What three characteristics of God does Paul name in verses 4-5 that drive His decision to act, and why does the timing — "even when we were dead" — matter to the gospel?

**3.** **Analysis:** Verse 8 says salvation is "not of yourselves, it is the gift of God." How does understanding faith as the open hand that *receives* a gift — rather than a work that *earns* one — change how a person approaches God?

**4.** **Application:** Verse 9 says no one may boast. How does the "no boasting" principle affect the way believers relate to people who are not yet followers of Christ — particularly in friendships and peer relationships?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** Verse 10 calls you God's *poiema* — His crafted masterpiece, created for good works prepared beforehand. What would it look like practically this week to walk in one of those prepared works in a relationship you already have?

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for whether students can clearly distinguish spiritual death from mere moral weakness, and whether they understand that faith receives grace rather than earns it. Strong answers to question 3 will reflect the gift-and-open-hand framing from the teaching.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students who have genuinely engaged this passage will speak about their pre-salvation condition without minimizing it, express gratitude rooted in grace rather than personal effort, and name a specific relationship or action when answering question 5 — not a vague intention.

**Closing Reflection:** "If someone who doesn't know Christ asked you today, 'Why do you think God would want anything to do with someone like me?' — how would Ephesians 2:1-10 shape your answer?"

## Section 8: Group Handout (Standalone)

**From Dead to Alive: The Gospel of Grace in Ephesians 2**

**Key Idea:** God, in His mercy and love, rescues spiritually dead people through the finished work of Christ — not because of anything they deserve, but so that His grace might be displayed and His purposes fulfilled in them.

**Memory Verse:** "For by grace you have been saved through faith; and this is not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast." — Ephesians 2:8-9 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* Before Christ, every person is spiritually *dead* — not merely struggling — and completely unable to generate spiritual life from within.
* God acts from His own mercy and love, not from anything we bring. He moved toward us while we were still dead.
* Faith is not a payment for grace — it is the open hand that receives what God freely gives.
* No one earns salvation, so no one gets to boast. The ground at the cross is level for every person.
* You are God's *poiema* — His crafted masterpiece — saved not *by* good works, but *for* good works already prepared for you to walk in.

**Gospel Connection:** Ephesians 2 answers the four questions every person carries: *Who was I?* Dead in sin. *What did God do?* Made me alive in Christ. *How was I saved?* Grace through faith, not effort. *Why am I here now?* Created to live purposefully for God's glory. This is the gospel in full — it begins with honest diagnosis and ends with extraordinary purpose. Christ's death and resurrection are the hinge on which the entire passage turns. Because He was raised, we are raised with Him.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** When you consider the word "dead" rather than "sick" to describe life apart from Christ, what does that tell you about what you needed — and what God provided?
**2.** Is there a relationship in your life right now where you have been treating grace as something to be earned rather than freely received and freely given?
**3.** What is one specific good work — a conversation, an act of service, a moment of honesty — that God may have already prepared for you in a friendship this week?

**Weekly Challenge:** This week, identify one person in your existing circle of relationships who needs to experience the grace described in Ephesians 2. Pray for them by name each day. Then take one concrete step — a text, a meal, an honest conversation — that opens a door for them to see or hear the gospel through you. You were created in Christ Jesus for exactly that kind of moment.

**Prayer:** "Father, thank You for acting when I was dead and could not act for myself. Thank You that salvation is entirely Your gift — that I have nothing to boast in except Your grace. Make me aware this week of the good works You have already prepared for me to walk in. Give me courage to step into the relationships and conversations where You want Your grace to be seen. Let my life be a poem that points to You. In Jesus' name, Amen."
