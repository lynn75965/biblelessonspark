**Lesson Title:** *Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of God's Faithfulness*

**Lesson Summary:** Hebrews 6:1–12 confronts believers with a sobering warning against spiritual stagnation while simultaneously anchoring them in the confidence of God's faithfulness toward those who persevere. The passage calls the mature believer to press forward in faith rather than perpetually revisiting foundational truths. Students will wrestle with what genuine spiritual growth looks like in the middle seasons of life.

**Audience Insight:** Mid-life adults (36–50) often face a plateau in their spiritual lives — busy careers, parenting demands, and familiarity with church can breed spiritual coasting. This passage speaks directly to that danger with both urgency and grace.

## Section 3: Theological Background (Deep-Dive)

**Literary and Historical Context**

Hebrews is a sermon-letter addressed to Jewish Christians facing intense pressure to abandon their faith and return to Judaism. The author is unknown — tradition suggests Paul, Apollos, or Barnabas, though Scripture does not specify. The recipients understood the Levitical system intimately, which is why the author's argument from priesthood and sacrifice carries such weight. Chapter 6 arrives after a sharp rebuke in 5:11–14, where the author accuses the readers of becoming "dull of hearing" — spiritually regressed to infancy when they should be teachers.

**The Six Foundational Doctrines (vv. 1–2)**

The author lists six elementary teachings: repentance from dead works, faith toward God, instruction about washings (likely distinguishing Christian baptism from Jewish ceremonial washings), laying on of hands, resurrection of the dead, and eternal judgment. These are not unimportant — they are the foundation. But a foundation exists to support a building, not to be perpetually re-laid. The call to "press on to maturity" (v. 1) uses the Greek *phero*, meaning to be carried forward, suggesting the Spirit's active role in moving believers ahead.

**The Warning Passage (vv. 4–6) — Interpretive Background**

This is one of the most debated texts in the New Testament. The author describes those who have been "enlightened," "tasted of the heavenly gift," "made partakers of the Holy Spirit," "tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come" — and then fallen away. Four main interpretations exist:

1. **True believers who lose salvation** — rejected by Southern Baptists based on John 10:28–29, Romans 8:38–39, and the doctrine of eternal security.
2. **Hypothetical warning** — the scenario is presented as impossible, serving as a rhetorical device to motivate perseverance.
3. **Professing believers who never truly believed** — their "tasting" was exposure without genuine saving faith; they experienced the blessings of the covenant community without personal regeneration.
4. **Warning against apostasy for genuine believers** — the warning is real but describes a sin so severe (public, deliberate rejection of Christ) that restoration becomes practically impossible, not because God refuses, but because the heart has so hardened itself.

The third and fourth views are most consistent with a Southern Baptist reading. The agricultural imagery in verses 7–8 reinforces this: land that receives rain (God's grace, His Word, the Spirit's work) and produces thorns is "worthless" — it has received blessing but yielded no fruit. This echoes Jesus' Parable of the Soils in Matthew 13.

**Doctrinal Significance**

The passage does not teach that a genuinely saved person can lose salvation. Rather, it warns that religious exposure — even deep religious experience — without genuine, persevering faith is spiritually dangerous. The author immediately pivots in verses 9–12 to express confidence that his readers show "things that accompany salvation," including love and ministry to fellow believers. This pivot is crucial: the warning is a pastoral tool designed to produce self-examination and forward movement, not despair.

**Key Greek Insight**

The word translated "maturity" in verse 1 is *teleiotes*, meaning completeness or full development. Spiritual maturity is not perfection but wholeness — a life increasingly shaped by Christ in every dimension.

**Cross-References**
- John 10:28–29 (eternal security)
- Romans 8:38–39 (nothing separates from God's love)
- Matthew 13:3–23 (fruit-bearing soil)
- 2 Peter 2:20–22 (false profession and return to corruption)

## Section 4: Opening Activities

**Hook — Problem/Tension Statement (5 minutes)**

Open with this question written on a whiteboard or spoken aloud: *"What's the difference between someone who has been around Christianity their whole life and someone who actually knows Christ?"*

Give students 60 seconds to think silently, then invite two or three responses. Don't resolve the tension yet — let it sit.

**Warm-Up Discussion (3–4 minutes)**

Ask: "Think about your own spiritual life five years ago compared to today. Are you further along — more like Christ, more fruitful, more surrendered — or are you roughly in the same place?"

Encourage honest reflection. Remind the class this is not about guilt — it's about honest self-examination, which is exactly what the author of Hebrews is calling his readers to do.

**Transition into Teaching:**

"Hebrews 6 is one of the most challenging passages in the New Testament — and one of the most freeing, once you understand what it's actually saying. Let's dig in."

## Section 5: Main Teaching Content (Teacher Transcript)

Open your Bibles to Hebrews 6, beginning at verse 1. Before we read it, I want you to know something: this passage has caused more confusion, more anxiety, and more theological debate than almost any other in the New Testament. But it has also, when properly understood, produced some of the most powerful moments of spiritual renewal in people's lives. So let's read it carefully and take it seriously.

> "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity, not laying again a foundation of repentance from dead works and of faith toward God, of instruction about washings and laying on of hands, and the resurrection of the dead and eternal judgment. And this we will do, if God permits."
> — Hebrews 6:1–3 (NASB)

The author opens with a word that should stop us: *leaving*. Not abandoning — leaving. You don't tear down a foundation to build a house. You build on it. The six doctrines listed here — repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, eternal judgment — these are not optional extras. They are the starting line. But you cannot run a race by standing permanently at the starting line.

Here is the question the author is pressing: *Are you building on your foundation, or are you just admiring it?*

For mid-life believers, this hits differently. You may have been a Christian for twenty or thirty years. You know the basics. You've heard the gospel hundreds of times. But knowing the foundation is not the same as building on it. Maturity — the Greek word *teleiotes* — means completeness, full development. It's a life where faith is working its way into every room of your house: your marriage, your parenting, your finances, your ambitions, your fears. That's what the author is calling you toward.

Now we come to the hard part. Verses 4 through 6.

> "For in the case of those who have once been enlightened and have tasted of the heavenly gift and have been made partakers of the Holy Spirit, and have tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come, and then have fallen away, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance, since they again crucify to themselves the Son of God and put Him to open shame."
> — Hebrews 6:4–6 (NASB)

Someone in this room is thinking: *Does this mean I can lose my salvation?* That's the right question to ask. The answer, consistent with the whole of Scripture, is no — but this passage is not giving you a free pass to coast.

Look at the language carefully. "Enlightened." "Tasted." "Partakers." These words describe someone who has been deeply exposed to the things of God — someone who has sat in worship services, heard the Word preached, experienced the community of believers, maybe even felt the Spirit's conviction. But *tasting* is not the same as *eating*. You can taste something and spit it out. The agricultural image in verses 7 and 8 makes this vivid: ground that receives rain — all that blessing, all that grace poured out — and still produces only thorns is, in the author's words, "worthless." Not because the rain failed. Because the ground never truly yielded.

This is not describing a saved person who stumbles and falls. Every genuine believer stumbles. This is describing someone who received every external blessing of the covenant community and then deliberately, publicly turned away — essentially saying, "I reject Christ, and I'm not coming back." The author says that kind of hardening makes restoration practically impossible — not because God's grace runs out, but because the heart has closed itself off entirely.

Here is where John 10:28–29 and Romans 8:38–39 anchor us. Jesus said no one can snatch His sheep from His hand. Paul said nothing — *nothing* — can separate us from the love of God in Christ. Eternal security is not a loophole. It is a promise to those who genuinely belong to Christ.

But — and this is the pastoral point — the warning exists because genuine faith produces fruit. Look at verses 9 through 12.

> "But, beloved, we are convinced of better things concerning you, and things that accompany salvation, though we are speaking in this way."
> — Hebrews 6:9 (NASB)

The author immediately pivots. He says: I believe better things about you. I see the fruit. I see the love you've shown. I see the ministry you've done. *These are the things that accompany salvation.* The warning was a mirror, not a verdict. He held it up so they — and we — would look honestly at our lives and ask: *Is there fruit here? Is my faith moving forward or standing still?*

For you, in this season of life, that question lands in specific places. Is your faith shaping how you lead your home? How you handle conflict with your spouse? How you're raising your children to know Christ — not just know about Him? Are you investing in someone spiritually younger than you? Are you serving, giving, going?

The passage ends with a call to imitate "those who through faith and patience inherit the promises" (v. 12). Faith is not passive. It presses on. It bears fruit. It perseveres.

Here is the challenge I want to leave with you: The warning of Hebrews 6 is not meant to make you doubt your salvation. It is meant to make you examine your fruit. If you see fruit — love, service, perseverance, growth — that is God's evidence in your life. If you see a field of thorns — years of religious exposure with no real change, no movement, no surrender — then the call today is not to despair but to respond. The Spirit is still drawing. The door is still open. Press on.

## Section 6: Interactive Activities

**Activity 1: Foundation vs. Building Audit (Whole-Group Interactive)**
*Time: 8–10 minutes | Materials: Whiteboard or large paper, markers*

Draw two columns on the board: **"Foundation"** and **"Building On It."**

Read aloud the six doctrines from Hebrews 6:1–2 (repentance, faith, baptism, laying on of hands, resurrection, eternal judgment). Ask the group: "For each of these, what does *knowing about it* look like versus *actively building your life on it*?"

Fill in both columns together. For example: Knowing about repentance vs. practicing ongoing confession and course-correction in your marriage or parenting.

Goal: Students visually see the gap between doctrinal familiarity and lived application. Close by asking, "Which column describes more of your daily life right now?"

*Adaptation for small classes (under 8):* Work through just two or three doctrines in depth rather than all six.

---

**Activity 2: Fruit or Thorns — Personal Inventory**
*Time: 4–5 minutes | Materials: Index cards or paper*

Give each student an index card. Ask them to write privately:
- One area of their family or home life where they see genuine spiritual fruit
- One area where they honestly see stagnation or thorns

Cards are not collected or shared. This is between them and God. Close the activity with a 60-second silent prayer inviting the Spirit's honest assessment.

*Adaptation for larger classes:* Pairs may share their "fruit" observation only if comfortable.

## Section 7: Discussion & Assessment

**Discussion Questions:**

**1.** **Recall:** According to Hebrews 6:1–2, what are the six foundational doctrines the author lists? Why does he call believers to move *beyond* them rather than simply master them?

**2.** **Understanding:** The teaching described four interpretive views of Hebrews 6:4–6. Which view is most consistent with what the rest of Scripture teaches about eternal security, and why does that view matter for how we read the warning?

**3.** **Analysis:** The passage uses agricultural imagery — rain-soaked ground producing either a crop or thorns. How does this image connect to Jesus' Parable of the Soils in Matthew 13? What does "receiving rain" represent, and what does the harvest reveal?

**4.** **Application:** The author pivots in verse 9 to say he is "convinced of better things" and points to love and ministry as "things that accompany salvation." What specific evidence of fruit should a mid-life believer expect to see in their family and home relationships if their faith is genuinely moving forward?

**5.** **Personal Reflection:** The teacher asked: *"Are you building on your foundation, or just admiring it?"* Where in your life right now — marriage, parenting, finances, ambitions — is your faith most actively under construction? Where has it stalled?

---

**Comprehension Check:** Listen for students who can distinguish between *tasting* and *genuine saving faith*, who understand the warning as a pastoral mirror rather than a threat to eternal security, and who can connect the agricultural imagery to fruit-bearing discipleship rather than reading it as condemnation.

**Spiritual Indicators:** Students demonstrating genuine understanding will move from abstract theology toward honest personal inventory — naming specific areas of stagnation or growth without deflecting into debate about the warning passage. Watch for willingness to name thorns alongside fruit.

**Closing Reflection:** "If the author of Hebrews looked at the ground of your life right now — all the rain God has poured on you, every sermon, every answered prayer, every year of walking with Him — what would he see growing there?"

## Section 8: Group Handout

**Press On: Maturity, Warning, and the Assurance of God's Faithfulness**

**Key Idea:** God calls every believer to move forward in faith, not coast on past exposure. Real faith builds on its foundation — it produces fruit, perseveres through difficulty, and trusts God's promises in every season of life.

**Memory Verse:** "Therefore leaving the elementary teaching about the Christ, let us press on to maturity." — Hebrews 6:1 (NASB)

**Big Points:**
* The six foundational doctrines in Hebrews 6:1–2 are the *starting line*, not the finish line. Faith builds on them — it doesn't just revisit them.
* Hebrews 6:4–6 warns against religious exposure without genuine, persevering faith. Tasting is not the same as eating. Receiving blessing is not the same as bearing fruit.
* Eternal security is a promise to those who genuinely belong to Christ — grounded in John 10:28–29 and Romans 8:38–39. The warning is a mirror for self-examination, not a verdict against struggling believers.
* The author's pivot in verse 9 is crucial: love, service, and perseverance are "things that accompany salvation." Fruit is the evidence, not the condition, of genuine faith.
* Spiritual maturity (*teleiotes*) means completeness — faith working its way into every area of life: marriage, parenting, finances, relationships.

**Gospel Connection:** The warning of Hebrews 6 exists because grace is real and costly. Christ was crucified once for all — His sacrifice is not to be treated as ordinary. For anyone who has been exposed to the gospel and pulled back, the Spirit is still drawing. Salvation remains available to all who respond in genuine faith and repentance. For those who belong to Christ, the passage is not a threat — it is an invitation to press deeper into the life He died to give you.

**Reflection Questions:**
**1.** In what area of your home or family life is your faith most actively growing right now — and where has it plateaued?
**2.** When you honestly examine your life, do you see more fruit or more thorns? What does that tell you?
**3.** Who in your life could you invest in spiritually this week — someone who needs to see faith that presses forward?

**Weekly Challenge:** Choose one specific area of your family or home life where your faith has stalled. This week, take one concrete step of forward movement — a conversation you've been avoiding, a spiritual discipline you've let go, a commitment to pray with your spouse or children. Don't just admire the foundation. Build.

**Prayer:** "Lord, thank You that nothing can separate me from Your love. Search me honestly — show me where I am building and where I am stalling. Where there are thorns, give me the courage to name them. Where there is fruit, let me give You the glory. I choose today to press on. Carry me forward by Your Spirit. Amen."
